The Uncertified Line

Lisbon's Funicular Tragedy and the Price of Neglect

Tuesday, 21 October 2025 08:24

Abstract

The catastrophic derailment of the Ascensor da Glória in September 2025, which claimed sixteen lives, has exposed profound systemic failures within Lisbon's historic public transport network. A preliminary investigative report has since revealed that the funicular's haulage cable was uncertified for passenger transport and that maintenance protocols were critically deficient. The disaster has forced the immediate suspension of the city's other iconic funiculars, raising urgent questions about the regulatory oversight and the sustainability of maintaining century-old infrastructure under the strain of modern tourism.

Historical Context

  • The Ascensor do Lavra, Lisbon's oldest funicular, opened in 1884.
  • The Glória funicular, a national monument, opened in 1885.
  • The funiculars were designed by engineer Raoul Mesnier du Ponsard.
  • The Glória funicular carries approximately three million passengers annually.
  • The Glória funicular derailed in May 2018 due to maintenance failure.

Recent Findings

  • The derailment of the Ascensor da Glória claimed sixteen fatalities and twenty-three injuries.
  • The funicular carriage collided at an estimated speed of 60 kilometres per hour.
  • The haulage cable was uncertified for public passenger transport use.
  • The report detailed five instances of deficient maintenance standards.
  • The cable was installed 337 days prior to the accident.

The Descent into Disaster

At 18:04 West European Summer Time on 3 September 20252, the Ascensor da Glória, one of Lisbon’s most iconic funicular railways, began its final, fatal descent2. The funicular, which connects the Praça dos Restauradores with the Bairro Alto neighbourhood2,14, had travelled no more than six metres from its upper station when the haulage cable linking its two carriages suddenly disconnected9,10,12. With the counterweight of the descending car instantly removed, the carriage at the top of the hill lost its balancing force and began to accelerate uncontrollably down the steep incline of the Calçada da Glória2,10,12. The vehicle’s brake-guard immediately activated both the pneumatic and manual brakes9,10. However, these measures proved insufficient to reduce the funicular’s speed9,16. The carriage hurtled approximately 170 metres10 before derailing on a curve and crashing into a building near the bottom of the line2,16. The entire catastrophic sequence unfolded in less than 50 seconds6,9,10, with the car colliding at an estimated speed of 60 kilometres per hour (37 mph)6,9,10. The wreckage, a mangled mass of yellow metal and debris, was described by witnesses as a “mangled death trap”3. Emergency services worked for hours to remove passengers from the wreckage14. The final toll of the disaster was sixteen fatalities2,4,7 and twenty-three injuries2,4, with five of the injured initially listed in critical condition2,3. The victims included the funicular’s brakeman, André Marques2,4, and four staff members of the charitable organisation Santa Casa da Misericórdia2,22. The tragedy transcended national borders, with eleven of the fatalities being foreign citizens6. The dead included three Britons, two South Koreans, two Canadians, an American, a Ukrainian, a Swiss national, and a French national2,5,12. The Portuguese government declared the following day, 4 September, a national day of mourning2,14, while Lisbon’s Mayor, Carlos Moedas, announced three days of mourning for the city3. Prime Minister Luís Montenegro called the crash “one of the biggest tragedies in our recent history”5.

The Uncertified Cable and the Fatal Flaw

The initial findings from the Office for the Prevention and Investigation of Accidents in Civil Aviation and Rail (GPIAAF)2,9 pointed to a cable failure, but a more comprehensive preliminary report released in October 20256,7,16 revealed a far more alarming systemic failure. The report concluded that the steel haulage cable that broke was defective6 and had never been certified for public transport use6,7,16. The cable had been acquired by Carris, Lisbon’s public transport operator2,7, in 20227. Furthermore, investigators found that the cable had not been installed according to the manufacturer’s instructions16. The cable had been in place for less than a year16, having been installed 337 days prior to the accident, despite having an expected operational life of 600 days10. The report detailed that the cable’s failure disabled the counterweight system, which is fundamental to the funicular principle of operation7,19. The funicular system relies on two cars being permanently attached to opposite ends of a haulage cable, with one car acting as a counterweight for the other as they ascend and descend simultaneously13,19. When the cable snapped, the safety systems immediately cut power to the car16. This action, however, simultaneously rendered the pneumatic brake inoperable16. The manual brake, which the operator attempted to apply, was not powerful enough to stop the car’s rapid acceleration down the steep gradient9,16. The funicular’s design, which dates back to the late 19th century8, proved fatally vulnerable to a single point of failure in the haulage system, a vulnerability compounded by the use of substandard, uncertified equipment6,7.

A Litany of Maintenance Failings

Beyond the critical failure of the uncertified cable, the GPIAAF preliminary report uncovered a deeper pattern of negligence and poor governance within the funicular’s maintenance regime6,16. The report specifically described five instances in which the funicular’s maintenance standards were found to be “non-existent, inapplicable, or outdated”16. This finding stood in stark contrast to the initial statements from Carris and earlier GPIAAF observations, which had claimed that the scheduled maintenance plan was “up to date”9,11 and that a visual inspection conducted on the very morning of the accident, starting at 9:13 a.m.15, had detected no anomalies in the cable or braking systems9,11. The investigators clarified that the section of the cable that ultimately separated was “not visible without dismantling”9, suggesting the morning’s visual check was inherently limited in its scope9. The maintenance issues were not a sudden development. Workers’ unions had previously voiced concerns that safety warnings were being ignored14. These warnings were attributed to years of budget cuts and chronic under-investment in Lisbon’s ageing transport network14. Further scrutiny was drawn to Carris after it was revealed that the company had cancelled its most recent maintenance tender in late August 2025 because all bids had exceeded the allocated budget15. While Carris insisted that a new, valid maintenance contract had been in effect since 1 September15, the preliminary report’s findings on the substandard cable and outdated procedures suggest a culture where cost-saving and operational continuity were prioritised over rigorous safety compliance7,16. The funicular’s maintenance regime had been under pressure for some time, highlighting the strain placed on old infrastructure in a rapidly modernising city14,22.

The Ageing Infrastructure and the Tourist Boom

The Glória funicular, which opened in 188525, is one of three historic funiculars in Lisbon, alongside the Ascensor do Lavra (inaugurated in 1884)8,20 and the Ascensor da Bica (opened in 1892)13,20. These inclined railways, designed by engineer Raoul Mesnier du Ponsard13,17,20, were originally conceived to overcome the significant transportation challenge posed by Lisbon’s steep topography8. They are classified as national monuments13,14,23 and are operated by the municipal public transport company, Carris2,13,17. The funiculars have become an integral part of the city’s identity and a major draw for the millions of tourists who visit the Portuguese capital each year8,14,22. The Glória funicular alone carries approximately three million passengers annually14. The sheer volume of traffic, driven by a massive tourism boom in recent years3,14, has placed immense strain on the century-old mechanisms14,22. The Glória funicular had a history of minor incidents, including a derailment in May 2018 that was attributed to maintenance failure, though no one was injured in that event21. The Bica funicular also experienced a minor collision with a garbage truck in July 200621. The 2025 disaster, however, was unprecedented in its scale and severity for Lisbon’s funicular network21. The tragedy has brought into sharp focus the delicate balance between preserving historic transport heritage and ensuring that such systems meet contemporary safety standards, especially when operating at peak capacity for a global audience18,22.

Grounding the Fleet and the Search for Accountability

In the immediate aftermath of the Glória crash, the Lisbon City Council took the decisive step of suspending the operation of the city’s other funiculars and trams until further safety checks could be completed14. Following the release of the preliminary report, investigators formally recommended that all of Lisbon’s cable car carriages remain out of service until inspectors could confirm that their braking systems were capable of immobilising the cabins6,7. The Ascensor da Bica, often cited as the most photographed funicular in Lisbon19,23, and the Ascensor do Lavra, the city’s oldest8,20, were thus grounded indefinitely13. The investigation into the disaster is proceeding on two independent tracks9,11. The GPIAAF is conducting a technical inquiry to establish the facts, analyse the causes, and issue safety recommendations7,9,11. A fuller report from this body is expected to take about eleven months to complete7. Simultaneously, the public prosecutor’s office has launched a formal criminal investigation9,11,14. While the GPIAAF report is not intended to determine liability or blame7,16, the criminal probe will focus on establishing who is responsible for the use of the uncertified cable and the deficient maintenance protocols5,7. The political pressure for accountability is intense, with Mayor Moedas stating that the city “needs answers”5 and Prime Minister Montenegro promising a swift investigation to determine all responsibilities5. The findings have already raised serious questions about the procurement processes within Carris and the effectiveness of the regulatory oversight body responsible for ensuring the safety of these national monuments7,13.

Conclusion

The tragedy of the Ascensor da Glória is more than a devastating accident; it is a stark illustration of the risks inherent in neglecting critical infrastructure, particularly in a city where heritage and modernity intersect under the pressure of mass tourism. The revelation of an uncertified cable and a pattern of outdated maintenance standards points not to a random mechanical failure, but to a profound lapse in corporate and regulatory duty. The funiculars are symbols of Lisbon, and their sudden grounding represents a break in the city’s historical continuity, forcing a painful reckoning with the true cost of preserving the past without adequate investment in safety. Restoring public confidence, both for residents and the international visitors who constitute the majority of the victims, will require more than just replacing a cable; it demands a complete overhaul of the safety culture, maintenance protocols, and regulatory framework governing these beloved, yet fragile, national treasures. The long road to recommissioning the fleet will be defined by the rigour of this reform, ensuring that the city’s iconic elevators can once again ascend and descend without the shadow of September’s catastrophe.
