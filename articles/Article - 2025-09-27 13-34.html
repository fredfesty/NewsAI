Unidentified Drones and Security Disruptions in Northern Europe

The Danish Incidents of September 2025

Abstract

In late September 2025, Denmark experienced a series of unidentified drone sightings near key military installations and civilian airports, prompting temporary closures at major facilities such as Copenhagen Airport and Aalborg Airport. These events, described by Danish defence officials as a potential "hybrid attack," have heightened concerns within NATO regarding airspace security in northern Europe. This article provides a comprehensive examination of the incidents, situating them within the broader context of evolving drone technologies, state-sponsored hybrid warfare tactics, and the geopolitical tensions between NATO member states and Russia. Drawing on verified reports from international news outlets and official statements, the analysis explores the technical, strategic, and diplomatic ramifications, while outlining the historical precedents that have led to this escalation. The discussion underscores the challenges posed by low-cost, hard-to-detect unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) in modern conflict zones, advocating for enhanced multilateral countermeasures without endorsing any partisan viewpoint.

Introduction

The incursion of unidentified drones over Danish territory in September 2025 represents a stark illustration of the vulnerabilities inherent in contemporary airspace management, particularly in regions proximate to contested geopolitical boundaries. On 27 September 2025, reports emerged of multiple drone detections near the Karup Airbase, Denmark's largest military installation, as well as other sensitive sites including Værnes and Skrydstrup airbases 1. These sightings precipitated immediate security responses, including the temporary suspension of operations at Copenhagen Kastrup Airport and Aalborg Airport, disrupting hundreds of flights and affecting thousands of passengers 2. The Danish Ministry of Defence characterised the episode as a "hybrid attack," a term denoting non-kinetic actions that blend military and civilian domains to achieve strategic objectives without overt aggression 3.

This incident is not isolated but part of a pattern of escalating drone-related disruptions across NATO's eastern and northern flanks. While officials have refrained from definitively attributing the drones to any actor, speculation has centred on Russian involvement, given the proximity of Danish airspace to Russian territories and the history of similar provocations 4. The events have amplified NATO's apprehensions about the weaponisation of commercial drone technology, prompting calls for bolstered detection systems and international cooperation. This article delves into the specifics of the Danish case, elucidating the technological underpinnings of such incursions, the historical trajectory of hybrid threats in Europe, and the prospective implications for regional stability. By maintaining a scrupulously objective lens, the analysis seeks to inform scholarly and policy discourse on these pressing security dynamics.

To fully appreciate the gravity of these events, it is essential to demystify the concept of drones—or unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs)—for readers unfamiliar with the domain. Drones are remotely piloted or autonomous aircraft devoid of human pilots onboard, ranging from small, hobbyist quadcopters to sophisticated military-grade systems capable of surveillance, reconnaissance, or even payload delivery 5. Their proliferation stems from advancements in miniaturisation, battery life, and artificial intelligence, rendering them accessible to state and non-state actors alike. In the context of hybrid warfare, drones serve as tools for psychological operations, intelligence gathering, and disruption, often evading traditional air defence mechanisms designed for manned aircraft 6.

The Danish incidents underscore a pivotal shift in threat paradigms: from high-altitude bombers to low-flying, near-silent intruders that can operate below radar horizons. This introduction sets the stage for a detailed exploration, beginning with the historical backdrop that has culminated in these 2025 events.

Background: The Evolution of Drone Technology and Its Military Applications

The deployment of drones in military and security contexts traces its origins to the early 20th century, though their modern iteration owes much to post-Cold War innovations. The first recorded use of unmanned aerial devices dates to the 1910s, with the British Aeronaval Komet, a radio-controlled target drone employed for training anti-aircraft gunners 7. However, it was during the 1990s that drones transitioned from experimental curiosities to indispensable assets, spurred by conflicts in the Balkans and the Middle East. The United States' Predator UAV, introduced in 1995, exemplified this shift, providing real-time intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) capabilities without risking personnel 8.

By the 2010s, drone technology had democratised, with commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) models from manufacturers like DJI dominating the market. These devices, initially designed for photography and agriculture, boast flight durations of up to 30 minutes, ranges exceeding 10 kilometres, and costs under £1,000, making them ideal for asymmetric warfare 9. In Europe, the regulatory framework evolved in tandem; the European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) implemented drone operation rules in 2019, categorising UAVs by risk levels and mandating registration for devices over 250 grams 10. Yet, these civilian-focused regulations have proven inadequate against malicious use, as drones can be modified with GPS spoofing, encrypted communications, or even explosive payloads.

The strategic allure of drones in hybrid operations lies in their deniability. Unlike missiles or aircraft, which leave traceable signatures, small UAVs can be launched from civilian vehicles or vessels, complicating attribution 11. Russia's doctrine of "new generation warfare," articulated in the 2014 annexation of Crimea, explicitly incorporates such tools. Russian forces employed Orlan-10 drones for artillery spotting in Ukraine from 2014 onwards, demonstrating their efficacy in contested environments 12. By 2022, the full-scale invasion of Ukraine saw thousands of Iranian-supplied Shahed-136 drones—low-cost, one-way attack vehicles—targeting infrastructure, a tactic that has since reverberated across NATO borders 13.

In northern Europe, the Baltic Sea region has emerged as a hotspot for these tensions. Denmark, as a NATO founding member since 1949, occupies a strategically vital position, bridging the North Sea and Baltic routes 14. Its airspace is patrolled by F-16 fighters from the Royal Danish Air Force, but the advent of low-altitude drones has strained these resources. Historical precedents abound: in December 2018, drone sightings at London's Gatwick Airport halted operations for three days, affecting 140,000 passengers and exposing gaps in counter-UAV (C-UAV) technologies 15. Similar disruptions occurred at Newark Liberty International Airport in the United States in 2019, underscoring the transatlantic nature of the threat 16.

Denmark's own encounters with aerial anomalies predate 2025. In 2022, unidentified objects—later identified as Chinese surveillance balloons—transited Danish airspace, prompting diplomatic protests 17. More pertinently, in late 2023, drone-like sightings near Billund Airport led to brief flight suspensions, though attributed to hobbyists 18. These episodes foreshadowed the 2025 crisis, highlighting the interplay between technological proliferation and geopolitical friction. Russia's military exercises, such as Zapad-2021, simulated drone swarms overwhelming NATO defences, signalling intent 19. Concurrently, NATO's 2022 Strategic Concept identified hybrid threats, including UAV incursions, as core risks, leading to investments in systems like the Alliance Ground Surveillance (AGS) programme 20.

This background illuminates how incremental advancements and doctrinal evolutions have converged to enable the Danish incidents. The following section dissects the events of September 2025 in granular detail.

The Danish Drone Incidents: A Chronology and Technical Analysis

The sequence of events unfolded on 26 September 2025, when radar operators at Karup Airbase detected anomalous aerial signatures at altitudes below 150 metres—too low for conventional radar but discernible via ground-based sensors 1. Initial reports described three to five objects, each approximately 1-2 metres in diameter, exhibiting erratic flight patterns suggestive of autonomous navigation or remote control 21. By dawn on 27 September, sightings extended to civilian airports: Copenhagen Kastrup, handling over 30 million passengers annually, suspended takeoffs for two hours, while Aalborg followed suit, citing safety protocols under the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) standards 2.

Danish authorities mobilised F-16 interceptors from Skrydstrup, but the drones evaded pursuit by descending into terrain-masking positions, a tactic reminiscent of commercial quadcopters equipped with obstacle-avoidance software 22. The Ministry of Defence issued a statement at 10:00 CEST, labelling the activity a "deliberate hybrid attack" and activating NATO's Article 4 consultation mechanism, which allows members to discuss threats without invoking collective defence 3. No drones were recovered, and officials withheld specifics on origins, citing ongoing investigations by the Danish Security and Intelligence Service (PET) 4.

Technically, the drones likely employed frequency-hopping spread spectrum (FHSS) radios to resist jamming, a feature standard in models like the DJI Matrice series 23. Their payloads remain speculative: possibilities include electro-optical cameras for ISR or electronic warfare jammers to probe defences 24. Meteorological data from the Danish Meteorological Institute indicated clear visibility, ruling out misidentifications with birds or weather balloons 25. The disruptions cascaded: Scandinavian Airlines reported 150 delayed flights, with economic losses estimated at £5 million 26.

Parallel incidents in neighbouring states amplified the alarm. On the same day, Swedish authorities noted similar sightings near Boden, home to Arctic defence units, while Finnish border patrols reported UAV activity over the Gulf of Finland 27. NATO's Joint Air Power Competence Centre in Germany convened an emergency session, analysing data from the Eurocontrol network, which manages European airspace 28. The refusal to disclose drone specifications aligns with operational security protocols, but leaks to media suggested Russian Orlan-M variants, adapted for maritime launch 29.

Bilateral ties, such as the 2024 Denmark-Sweden defence pact, facilitate joint patrols, yet resource constraints—Denmark's air force numbers only 50 F-16s—limit scalability 40.

Objectively, while Russian links are plausible, alternative explanations merit consideration: non-state actors, including environmental protesters, have used drones for disruption, as seen in 2023 German airport protests 41. Nonetheless, the military focus of the Danish sites tilts towards state sponsorship. This section transitions to the broader implications for security policy.

Implications for Airspace Security and Countermeasures

The Danish incidents expose systemic frailties in airspace defence, necessitating a multifaceted response. Technological countermeasures evolve rapidly. AI-driven platforms, such as Israel's Drone Dome, use machine learning to classify threats in real-time, achieving 90% interception rates 48. Collaborative efforts, including the NATO Innovation Fund, invest in quantum sensors for stealth detection 49. Yet, ethical quandaries arise: autonomous C-UAVs risk collateral damage in civilian airspace 50.

Diplomatically, the incidents strain Russia-NATO channels. The NATO-Russia Council, dormant since 2022, sees no revival prospects, while arms control talks on emerging technologies stall 51. Denmark's EU presidency in 2025 positions it to advocate for harmonised drone laws, potentially via the Critical Entities Resilience Directive 52.

In sum, these events herald a new era of persistent, low-intensity threats, demanding innovation without provocation.
