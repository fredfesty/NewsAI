The Ceasefire Calculus

Europe’s Pivot to Peace on the Current Front Line

Tuesday, 21 October 2025 08:39

Abstract

A new diplomatic consensus has emerged among key Western allies, aligning with the United States' call for an immediate cessation of hostilities in Ukraine based on the existing line of contact. This shift, spearheaded by the US President, has prompted European capitals to prepare for a post-ceasefire reality, including the United Kingdom's commitment to deploy a costly multinational stabilisation force. The proposal, however, presents a profound challenge to Kyiv, which must reconcile its sovereign claim to all occupied territory with the immediate prospect of ending the bloodshed.

  • The US President spoke with Russian President Vladimir Putin for two hours by phone.
  • Russia currently occupies approximately 78 per cent of the Donbas region.
  • Russia fully occupies the Luhansk region and controls over 70 per cent of Donetsk.
  • The UK allocated a record £4.5 billion in military aid to Ukraine this year.

  • A joint statement supported the US President's call for an immediate ceasefire.
  • The UK committed 'well over £100m' to a post-ceasefire stabilisation force.
  • The 'Multinational Force Ukraine' will be led by the United Kingdom and France.
  • Over 200 military planners from 38-plus nations planned the multinational force.
  • The US President announced a bilateral summit with the Russian President in Budapest.

The Transatlantic Alignment on an Immediate Halt

A significant diplomatic shift in the approach to the war in Ukraine was formalised with a joint statement from European leaders, including those from Britain, France, and Germany, alongside Ukraine and the European Union. The statement declared strong support for the US President’s position that the fighting should cease immediately. Crucially, the leaders agreed that the current line of contact should serve as the starting point for negotiations. This consensus followed a period of intense diplomatic activity, including a meeting between the US President and Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy at the White House. The American leader had previously spoken with Russian President Vladimir Putin for two hours by phone. Following his meeting with the Ukrainian President, the US President took to social media to call for Kyiv and Moscow to “stop where they are” and end the war. He suggested that both sides should “claim Victory” and allow history to decide the outcome. The US President reiterated this position, stating that the war must be stopped immediately along the front line, wherever it lies, to prevent the conflict from becoming too complicated to resolve. He claimed to have delivered the same message to both the Ukrainian and Russian leaders. The US President’s proposal was framed as an attempt to replicate his recent success in brokering a ceasefire between Israel and Hamas. The diplomatic push comes more than three years after Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.

Kyiv’s Cautious Acceptance of a Starting Point

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy responded cautiously to the proposal for an immediate ceasefire along the existing battle lines. He acknowledged that the US President was correct in stating that the fighting must stop where it is, and that this should be followed by negotiations. The Ukrainian leader framed the proposal as a first step towards broader talks and a ‘just and lasting peace’. He also expressed a ‘positive outlook’ on the US President’s message, provided all parties understood the implications of the ‘line of contact’. However, the Ukrainian President has consistently rejected the idea of forfeiting any territory to Moscow. The US President’s call for a halt at the current lines implies Russia would retain control over the territory it currently occupies. This includes approximately 78 per cent of the Donbas region, which comprises the Donetsk and Luhansk territories. Russia fully occupies the Luhansk region and controls over 70 per cent of the Donetsk region. Reports indicated that the Russian President had offered a new ceasefire proposal during his phone call with the US President, which involved relinquishing relatively small territories in the southern Kherson and Zaporizhzhia regions in exchange for Kyiv yielding all the territory it still held in the Donbas region. The Ukrainian President stated that Russia had not yet communicated a clear position on this specific exchange option. The Ukrainian leader’s position remains that Russia is the aggressor and that pressure must be applied to Moscow, not Kyiv. He emphasised that Ukraine would grant the aggressor no gifts and forget nothing.

The European Security Commitment

In parallel with the diplomatic push for a ceasefire, the United Kingdom has committed to a substantial financial and military contribution to a post-conflict stabilisation effort. The UK Defence Secretary, John Healey, announced that the cost of Britain’s contribution to a post-ceasefire stabilisation force for Ukraine would be ‘well over £100m’. This figure is intended to cover an entry package, including the initial deployment of troops and aircraft. The Defence Secretary confirmed that he had already accelerated millions of pounds in spending to prepare for any possible deployment in the event of peace. The preparation is for a ‘Multinational Force Ukraine’ that would be led by the UK and France. This force is being developed by a ‘coalition of the willing’ of more than 30 nations, with over 200 military planners from 38-plus nations involved in detailed planning over the past six months. The multinational force’s principal components would include a NATO-style air policing operation to secure Ukraine’s airspace and a maritime security force to support an existing mine-clearing naval force in the Black Sea. The UK Defence Secretary stated that the force would be ready to deploy quickly if peace talks produce a ceasefire. The UK Prime Minister, Keir Starmer, has committed to ‘UK boots on the ground’ if necessary, though land forces would initially be limited to logistics, armament, and training. The Defence Secretary’s announcement was made during a speech in the City of London. The UK’s commitment to this stabilisation force is separate from the record £4.5 billion in military aid allocated to Ukraine this year.

The Diplomatic Road to Budapest

The prospect of a peace summit has intensified diplomatic maneuvering across Europe. The US President announced plans for a bilateral summit with the Russian President to be held in Budapest, Hungary, within the next few weeks. The choice of Hungary was reportedly made out of respect for Prime Minister Viktor Orbán and the country’s stability. The Ukrainian President is expected to join the talks remotely as a mediator. The prospect of the summit has caused disquiet among some European nations, particularly the Baltic and Eastern European states, who have rejected any deal that excludes Ukraine. The EU foreign policy chief, Kaja Kallas, stated that the only place for the Russian President in Europe should be in The Hague, in reference to the International Criminal Court arrest warrant against him. However, other European ministers have shown more flexibility. Germany’s Europe Minister, Gunther Krichbaum, called the meeting ‘good’ but cautioned about the risks of excluding Ukraine. The French Foreign Minister, Jean-Noël Barrot, called the American-Russian talks ‘useful’ but stressed that the Russian President’s presence on European Union soil only made sense if it led to an immediate and unconditional ceasefire. The Danish Foreign Minister, Lars Løkke Rasmussen, went further, calling the proposal for a ceasefire at current battle lines ‘an important step forward’. The diplomatic flurry underscores a fundamental rift in Europe over the US approach, with some leaders fearing it could undermine Western unity in supporting Ukraine. The Ukrainian President is scheduled to travel to Brussels to attend a summit of the European Council and to London for a meeting of the ‘coalition of the willing’ to win security guarantees for Kyiv.

Conclusion

The convergence of Western diplomatic positions on a ceasefire at the current line of contact marks a pivotal moment in the war, shifting the focus from territorial reclamation to immediate stabilisation. The joint European statement, which includes Kyiv’s allies, lends significant weight to the US President’s peace initiative, despite the inherent controversy of freezing the conflict with Russia in control of substantial Ukrainian territory. The commitment of the United Kingdom and its partners to a multinational stabilisation force, costing well over £100 million, demonstrates a pragmatic acceptance of a new reality on the ground, where military aid is now being complemented by detailed planning for a post-ceasefire security architecture. The challenge for the coming weeks lies in translating this diplomatic momentum into a concrete agreement in Budapest, one that satisfies the immediate humanitarian need to stop the killing while navigating the profound political and territorial concessions that a frozen conflict would impose on Ukraine’s sovereignty.
