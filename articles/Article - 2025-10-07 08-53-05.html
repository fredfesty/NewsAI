The Hull Lament

A British Industrial Titan Falters Under a Deluge of Chinese Imports

Article created and last updated on: Tuesday 07 October 2025 08:53

Abstract

The decision by the chemicals conglomerate Ineos to eliminate 60 skilled positions at its Hull facility has sent shockwaves through the British manufacturing sector. 6, 7, 8 This reduction, amounting to a fifth of the plant's workforce, has been attributed by the company to a confluence of soaring domestic energy costs and a flood of "dirt-cheap" petrochemical imports from China. 6, 7, 8 Ineos contends that these Chinese products, benefiting from less stringent environmental regulations and lower production costs, are being 'dumped' into the UK and European markets, particularly after facing tariff barriers in the United States. 6, 7, 8 The company has issued a stark warning that without governmental intervention in the form of protective tariffs, further job losses are inevitable across the wider UK chemical industry. 6, 7, 8 This event has ignited a broader debate about the competitiveness of British manufacturing, the efficacy of the government's industrial strategy, and the intricate dynamics of global trade in an era of increasing protectionism.

  • The Ineos Hull facility has long been a cornerstone of the regional economy.

  • Two-thirds of the British manufacturing sector has disappeared in the last 25 years.

Introduction

In early October 2025, the industrial landscape of East Yorkshire was dealt a significant blow with the announcement from Ineos, a global petrochemicals giant, that it would be cutting 20% of the workforce at its acetyls plant in Hull. 6 The loss of 60 skilled jobs is a stark reminder of the precarious state of the United Kingdom's manufacturing base. 6, 7 The company's rationale for this decision points to a perfect storm of adverse economic conditions: cripplingly high energy prices within the UK and what it describes as anti-competitive trade practices, specifically the "dumping" of cheaply produced goods from China onto European markets. 6, 16 This situation in Hull is not an isolated incident but rather a microcosm of the broader challenges confronting the British chemical industry and, by extension, the nation's entire manufacturing sector. The episode raises profound questions about the country's industrial policy, its vulnerability to global market fluctuations, and its capacity to compete in an increasingly complex and often protectionist international arena.

The Unravelling of a Key Regional Employer

The Ineos facility in Hull, a significant producer of acetic acid and its derivatives, has long been a cornerstone of the regional economy. 7, 8 These chemicals are fundamental to a vast array of modern products, from pharmaceuticals like aspirin and paracetamol to food preservation, adhesives, and industrial coatings. 6 The plant, described by the company as a "leading-edge, efficient and well-invested site" with a "highly skilled, professional, and dedicated" team, represents a significant capital investment and a repository of specialised expertise. 7, 8 The decision to reduce its workforce was, according to David Brooks, the Chief Executive of Ineos Acetyls, not taken lightly, with the company having explored every possible alternative. 7, 8 However, the "sustained pressure from energy costs, combined with unfairly low-cost imports into the UK and Europe," left them with no other choice. 7, 8 This move follows a pattern of retrenchment by Ineos in the UK, including the recent closure of its oil refinery in Grangemouth, Scotland, with the loss of approximately 400 jobs, and the shutdown of two plants in Germany, signalling a wider European crisis in the chemical sector. 8, 26, 27

The Twin Spectres of Energy Costs and Chinese Imports

At the heart of Ineos's predicament are two intertwined economic pressures: the high cost of energy in the UK and the influx of low-cost Chinese petrochemicals. The company has been vocal about the detrimental impact of "sky high" energy costs on its UK operations. 6, 8 British industrial energy prices are notoriously high, estimated to be 60% higher than those for their competitors in the European Union, and as much as four hundred percent higher than in the United States. 10, 31 This disparity places British manufacturers at a significant competitive disadvantage, eroding profit margins and limiting their capacity for reinvestment and innovation. 21 Ineos has previously warned that unless there is a significant turnaround in energy and carbon costs, the future of UK manufacturing is under threat. 12 The company has also highlighted the burden of carbon taxes, which it argues are squeezing the life out of the sector and, ironically, leading to the offshoring of manufacturing to countries with less stringent environmental regulations, thereby increasing global emissions. 9

Compounding the challenge of high domestic costs is the surge in "dirt-cheap carbon-heavy imports from China". 6, 8 Ineos alleges that these products are flooding the UK and European markets after being effectively blocked from the United States by tariffs. 6, 7, 8 The company claims that many of these Chinese competitor products are produced using coal and emit up to eight times more carbon dioxide than its own UK operations. 7, 8, 26 This practice, often referred to as 'dumping', involves exporting goods at a price lower than their normal value, often to gain market share and drive out domestic competition. The lack of similar trade barriers in the UK and Europe, Ineos argues, leaves its more environmentally conscious and higher-cost production exposed to unfair competition. 6 This sentiment is echoed by broader industry concerns about the impact of cheap imports on the UK chemical sector, which has seen its output fall significantly below pre-pandemic levels. 23

A Global Trade Imbalance with Local Consequences

The situation in Hull is a direct consequence of shifting global trade dynamics. The imposition of tariffs by the United States on a range of Chinese goods, including petrochemicals, has had a ripple effect across the globe. 13, 18 With their access to the lucrative US market restricted, Chinese producers have sought alternative outlets for their products, with Europe and the UK becoming prime targets. 13 This redirection of trade flows has intensified competition in these markets, putting immense pressure on domestic producers. 13 The global petrochemical market is already facing challenges of overcapacity, and the influx of Chinese products has exacerbated this issue. 13 Industry executives have warned that the disruption caused by tariffs could lead to a significant drop in global petrochemical trade. 13

The American Chemistry Council has been a vocal opponent of tariffs, arguing that the fallout from additional costs and shifting trade flows will have unintentional, long-term consequences that could threaten the growth of the US as a global supplier. 18 However, from a UK perspective, the absence of a robust response to this trade diversion leaves its domestic industry vulnerable. Ineos has explicitly called on the UK government and the European Commission to introduce urgent anti-dumping tariffs on Chinese and US importers to protect the chemicals sector. 6, 33 The company has warned that "more sites will close and thousands more jobs will be lost" across the UK and European chemical industry unless firm action is taken. 6, 7, 8 This call for protectionist measures highlights a growing tension between the principles of free trade and the desire to safeguard domestic industries and jobs.

The UK's Industrial Strategy Under the Spotlight

The job losses at Ineos have inevitably drawn attention to the UK government's industrial strategy and its effectiveness in supporting the manufacturing sector. In recent years, the government has articulated a vision for a modern industrial strategy aimed at boosting investment, productivity, and resilience across the economy. 11, 14 This strategy has identified key sectors with high growth potential, including advanced manufacturing and clean energy. 14, 25 It includes commitments to reduce electricity costs for energy-intensive industries, simplify regulation, and enhance support for investors. 14 A government spokesperson, responding to the Ineos announcement, acknowledged the "tough time for our chemicals industry" and pointed to the modern Industrial Strategy's aim of "slashing electricity costs". 29

However, critics argue that these measures are either insufficient or too slow to address the immediate crises facing industries like petrochemicals. Ineos itself has described the government's proposals on electricity prices as an "important first step" but has stressed that the chemical sector relies more heavily on gas, where UK prices remain among the highest in the world. 37 The company has warned that Britain is "sleepwalking into deindustrialisation" and that strategic foundation industries are "slowly bleed[ing] out" not because they cannot compete, but because UK policy makes them uncompetitive. 37 The Chemical Industries Association (CIA) has also expressed concerns, noting that the UK chemical sector is at a disadvantage compared to international competitors who benefit from lower energy costs and more consistent government backing. 20 The CIA's "Project 2035" report outlines the challenges ahead and calls for decisive intervention from both government and industry to reverse the sector's decline. 20, 32

The Broader Implications for British Manufacturing

The plight of the Ineos plant in Hull is symptomatic of a wider malaise affecting British manufacturing. The sector has been in long-term decline, with two-thirds of it disappearing in the last 25 years alone. 37 This deindustrialisation has significant economic and social consequences, including the loss of skilled jobs, a hollowing out of regional economies, and an increased reliance on imports. 9 The challenges are multifaceted, ranging from uncompetitive energy costs and stringent environmental regulations to skills shortages and the lingering impacts of Brexit. 10 The UK's departure from the European Union has introduced additional bureaucracy and trade barriers, further complicating the operating environment for manufacturers. 10

To survive and thrive in this challenging landscape, the UK chemical industry, and the manufacturing sector as a whole, must adapt and innovate. There is a growing recognition of the need to embrace "green chemistry" and bio-based chemicals, leveraging the UK's high skill levels to manufacture high-value, sustainable products. 15 Digitalisation and the adoption of 'fourth industrial revolution' technologies, such as artificial intelligence and the internet of things, are also seen as crucial for improving efficiency and competitiveness. 15 However, these transitions require significant investment, which is difficult to secure in an environment of high costs and intense international competition. 21

The Path Forward: A Call for Coordinated Action

The job cuts at Ineos in Hull serve as a stark wake-up call. They underscore the urgent need for a coherent and effective industrial strategy that addresses the structural weaknesses of the UK economy and provides a level playing field for its domestic industries. This will require a multi-pronged approach involving both government and industry.

From a governmental perspective, there is a clear need to tackle the issue of high energy costs. While the focus on reducing electricity prices is welcome, a more comprehensive energy strategy that addresses the cost of gas for industrial users is essential. 37 Furthermore, the government must consider its stance on trade policy. The call from Ineos for anti-dumping tariffs on Chinese imports cannot be dismissed out of hand. 6 While protectionism carries its own risks, there is a legitimate argument for measures to counter unfair trade practices and protect strategic industries from being undercut by competitors with lower environmental and labour standards. The UK's Trade Remedies Authority has already recommended extensive anti-dumping duties on Chinese biodiesel imports, setting a precedent for action in other sectors. 45, 48

For its part, the industry must continue to innovate and adapt. Investment in green technologies, digitalisation, and skills development will be crucial for long-term competitiveness. 10, 15 Collaboration between companies, universities, and research institutions can help to foster a culture of innovation and ensure that the UK remains at the forefront of technological advancement. 15 The Chemical Industries Association's "Project 2035" provides a roadmap for how the sector can transition to a more resilient and sustainable future, but this will require a concerted effort from all stakeholders. 20, 32

Conclusion

The loss of 60 jobs at the Ineos plant in Hull is more than just a local issue; it is a potent symbol of the profound challenges facing the United Kingdom's industrial base. The confluence of high domestic energy costs and a surge in low-cost imports from China has created a toxic environment for a sector that is vital to the nation's economic prosperity and security. 6, 7, 8 The episode has exposed the limitations of the current industrial strategy and has ignited a critical debate about the future of British manufacturing in an era of intense global competition. 37 Without a decisive and coordinated response from both government and industry, the lament of Hull could become a recurring theme across the country, with devastating consequences for jobs, communities, and the UK's standing as a leading industrial nation. The choices made in the coming months and years will determine whether the UK can reverse its industrial decline and forge a new path towards a prosperous and sustainable future, or whether it will continue to be buffeted by the winds of global economic change, with its industrial heartlands paying the heaviest price.
