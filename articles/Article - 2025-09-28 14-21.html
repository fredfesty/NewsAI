The Labour Party conference in Liverpool, spanning 21 to 25 September 2024, served as a pivotal moment for the newly elected government under Prime Minister Keir Starmer. Just two months after securing a landslide victory in the general election on 4 July 2024, Labour faced the dual task of consolidating its mandate and addressing mounting domestic challenges 1. Starmer's closing speech on 25 September 2024 was a cornerstone of the event, blending optimism about economic renewal with stern warnings against the resurgence of populist politics, particularly from Reform UK. In this address, Starmer directly challenged Reform UK's immigration proposals, describing them as not only impractical but also perilously divisive, with undertones that echoed accusations of racism in public discourse 2.

To understand the significance of these remarks, it is essential to contextualise them within the broader landscape of British politics. Immigration has long been a flashpoint in UK public life, influencing electoral outcomes and policy directions since the post-war era. Reform UK, led by Nigel Farage, emerged as a disruptive force in the 2024 election, capitalising on dissatisfaction with mainstream parties' handling of migration, the economy, and cultural changes. Starmer's critique was not merely rhetorical; it reflected Labour's strategic positioning to reclaim the centre ground while countering perceptions of weakness on border control 3.

Background

The roots of contemporary UK immigration debates trace back to the mid-20th century, when post-World War II reconstruction necessitated labour inflows from Commonwealth nations. The British Nationality Act 1948 granted citizenship rights to subjects of the Crown, facilitating migration from the Caribbean, South Asia, and Africa 4. This period, often romanticised as one of multicultural foundation-building, was marred by tensions, exemplified by events such as the 1958 Notting Hill race riots, which highlighted racial prejudices embedded in British society 5.

Subsequent decades saw legislative efforts to manage inflows. The Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962 and 1968 restricted entry, signalling a shift towards controlled migration amid rising public anxieties 6. The 1971 Immigration Act further delineated categories of entrants, distinguishing between those with right of abode and others requiring visas. These measures were responses to economic pressures and political rhetoric, with Enoch Powell's 1968 "Rivers of Blood" speech epitomising the inflammatory discourse that equated immigration with cultural erosion 7.

The late 1990s and 2000s under New Labour, led by Tony Blair, marked a liberalising phase. The Human Rights Act 1998 incorporated the European Convention on Human Rights, influencing asylum policies, while EU enlargement in 2004 prompted unrestricted access for Eastern European workers 8. Net migration surged, peaking at over 700,000 in 2022, driven by non-EU students, healthcare workers, and humanitarian routes 9. This era fostered economic growth but also fuelled Euroscepticism, culminating in the 2016 Brexit referendum, where immigration control was a central pledge 10.

Post-Brexit, the Conservative governments of 2019-2024 implemented the points-based system in 2021, aiming to prioritise skilled workers while curbing low-skilled entry 11. However, irregular crossings via the English Channel escalated, with over 45,000 arrivals in 2022 alone, prompting the controversial Rwanda deportation scheme 12. Public opinion polls consistently ranked immigration among top concerns, with 52% of voters in 2024 citing it as a priority 13.

Enter Reform UK, formerly the Brexit Party, which rebranded in 2021 under Farage's leadership. The party advocates stringent measures, including a migration cap, exit from the European Convention on Human Rights, and offshore processing 14. In the 2024 election, Reform secured 14.3% of the vote and five seats, outperforming expectations and fragmenting the right-wing vote 15. Critics, including Labour figures, have accused Reform of dog-whistle politics, where anti-immigrant rhetoric veils xenophobic undertones 16.

Labour's 2024 manifesto promised to "smash the gangs" behind Channel crossings, recruit 8,500 border force officers, and clear asylum backlogs, while maintaining the points-based system 17. Starmer's government, upon taking office, scrapped the Rwanda plan, redirecting resources to enforcement and international agreements 18. This backdrop framed the 2024 conference, where Starmer sought to assert Labour's credibility on immigration amid Reform's rising poll numbers, hovering around 20% in late 2024 19.

The conference itself occurred against a turbulent domestic scene. Labour's early decisions, such as means-testing winter fuel payments, sparked backlash, with membership dissatisfaction evident in internal ballots 20. A September 2024 poll by YouGov revealed that only 45% of Labour voters believed Starmer should lead into the next election, reflecting concerns over economic delivery 21. Internationally, Starmer's speech touched on alliances like AUKUS, a 2021 trilateral security pact with the US and Australia, focused on Indo-Pacific stability and nuclear submarine technology 22. Discussions with Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese underscored transatlantic solidarity, with Albanese publicly supporting Starmer amid UK party strife 23.

Starmer's Speech: Key Themes and Critiques of Reform UK

Keir Starmer's 25 September 2024 address lasted approximately 75 minutes, delivered to a packed auditorium in Liverpool's ACC Centre. The speech opened with tributes to Labour's historic win, attributing it to a rejection of 14 years of Conservative rule marked by austerity, Brexit fallout, and scandal 2. Starmer outlined a "mission-driven" government agenda, emphasising growth, clean energy, and NHS reform 24.

A significant portion addressed immigration, where Starmer positioned Labour as pragmatic reformers. He announced plans for a new Border Security Command, integrating intelligence and enforcement to dismantle smuggling networks 25. Starmer stressed that controlled migration benefits the economy, citing contributions from sectors like the NHS, where 20% of staff are foreign-born 26. However, he vowed to reduce net migration through measures targeting student dependents and care worker visas, responding to public demands without endorsing blanket restrictions 27.

The critique of Reform UK was incisive yet measured. Starmer described their policies as "the politics of the pub" – appealing in casual conversation but disastrous in practice 2. He specifically targeted Reform's call for a "freeze" on non-essential migration, arguing it would cripple industries reliant on international talent, such as technology and agriculture 28. Implicitly, Starmer linked Reform's rhetoric to far-right populism, warning that it "stokes division and hatred" without naming racism outright, though commentators interpreted it as such 29. This echoed broader accusations; for instance, Farage's past comments on immigration have been scrutinised by equality watchdogs 30.

Starmer's approach contrasted with Reform's absolutism. While Reform proposes withdrawing from international treaties, Labour favours bilateral deals with France and Albania to curb crossings 31. Polling post-speech indicated mixed reception: 55% of the public approved Labour's immigration stance, but Reform supporters remained unmoved, with 70% viewing Starmer as "out of touch" 32.

Domestic Political Challenges: Leadership Doubts and Party Unity

The conference was overshadowed by internal Labour frictions. Delegates debated the government's fiscal restraint, including the winter fuel cut affecting 10 million pensioners 33. Starmer dismissed leadership speculation as "distractions," focusing on long-term stability 34. A Labour Together poll in September 2024 showed 52% of party members reluctant to back Starmer for 2029, citing delivery shortfalls 21.

These doubts stemmed from early governance hurdles. The 5% public sector pay rise in July 2024, funded by tax hikes, drew union ire 35. Economically, GDP growth stalled at 0.6% in Q3 2024, with inflation at 2.2% 36. Starmer countered by pledging infrastructure investment, including Great British Energy 37.

Reform UK's threat amplified these pressures. Farage, attending the conference fringes, positioned his party as the true opposition, attracting disaffected Conservatives 38. Starmer's response was to frame Reform as a symptom of unresolved grievances, urging unity against "declinism" 2.

International Dimensions: AUKUS and Transatlantic Relations

Starmer's speech extended to foreign policy, highlighting AUKUS as a cornerstone of UK security 39. Initiated in September 2021, the pact commits the UK to sharing nuclear propulsion technology with Australia, enhancing deterrence against regional threats 22. In bilateral talks with Albanese on 26 September 2024, Starmer reaffirmed commitments, with Albanese praising Labour's "steady hand" amid domestic turbulence 23.

This alliance underscores post-Brexit realignments. AUKUS supplements the Five Eyes intelligence network and counters China's assertiveness in the South China Sea 40. For Labour, it balances progressive values with strategic imperatives, though critics question the £10 billion projected cost 41. Albanese's endorsement bolstered Starmer, contrasting with US uncertainties under a potential Trump return 42.

The conference also addressed Ukraine support and Gaza ceasefire calls, reflecting Labour's multilateralism 43. These elements reinforced Starmer's narrative of a "secure and fair" Britain.

Implications for UK Immigration Policy

Starmer's remarks signal a evolution in Labour's immigration framework. Historically, the party oscillated between openness and restriction; under Blair, inflows doubled, while Corbyn's era emphasised humanitarianism 44. The 2024 approach blends enforcement with integration, aiming for net migration below 300,000 annually 45.

Challenges persist. The asylum backlog exceeds 100,000 cases, straining resources 46. Reform's narrative exploits this, promising swift deportations 14. Labour's response involves legislative reforms, including the Border Security Bill slated for 2025 47.

Socially, immigration debates intersect with racism concerns. The Runnymede Trust reports persistent institutional biases, with hate crimes rising 25% post-Brexit 48. Starmer's implicit critique of Reform aligns with efforts to combat far-right extremism, as seen in the 2024 riots following misinformation 49.

Economically, migration sustains growth; the Office for Budget Responsibility estimates a 1% GDP boost from skilled inflows 50. Yet, regional disparities fuel resentment, with net migration concentrated in London and the South East 51.

Public and Media Reception

Media coverage of the speech was polarised. The Guardian lauded its "passion and purpose," while The Telegraph deemed it "short on specifics" 52. Public approval stood at 48%, per Ipsos, with immigration critiques resonating among Remain voters 53.

Reform UK retaliated, with Farage accusing Starmer of "virtue-signalling" 54. This exchange highlights deepening polarisation, with 40% of Britons viewing immigration as culturally threatening 55.

Long-Term Political Outlook

Looking ahead, Starmer's conference performance sets the tone for Labour's tenure. By 2029, success hinges on migration reductions and economic gains 56. Reform's momentum could erode Tory recovery, potentially leading to a fragmented right 57.

The fight against populism requires addressing root causes: inequality, housing shortages, and service strains 58. Labour's strategy emphasises evidence-based policy, contrasting Reform's emotive appeals.

Conclusion

Keir Starmer's 2024 Labour conference speech encapsulated the complexities of governing a divided nation. By critiquing Reform UK's immigration stance as divisive, he sought to reclaim narrative control, while navigating leadership doubts and international commitments. This moment underscores the enduring challenge of balancing openness with security in UK politics. As debates evolve, scholarly attention must focus on equitable solutions that honour Britain's multicultural heritage.
