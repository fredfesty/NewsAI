The Resumption of United Nations Sanctions on Iran

Historical Context, Mechanisms, and Implications

Article created and last updated on: Saturday 27 September 2025 14:50

Abstract

This article provides a comprehensive examination of the mechanisms and historical developments surrounding United Nations sanctions on Iran, with a particular focus on the potential resumption of measures originally imposed under Security Council resolutions. Drawing from the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) agreed in 2015, which led to the lifting of nuclear-related sanctions, the discussion explores the snapback provision that allows for the reinstatement of pre-2015 penalties. It analyses the geopolitical tensions, including efforts by Russia and China to delay such actions, Iran's diplomatic responses such as recalling envoys, and the broader implications for international non-proliferation efforts. The article situates these elements within the historical evolution of Iran's nuclear programme and sanctions regimes, ensuring a thorough understanding for readers unfamiliar with the subject. By integrating verified historical facts and recent diplomatic developments up to 2023, it underscores the ongoing challenges in achieving compliance with nuclear agreements amid shifting global alliances.

Introduction

The imposition and lifting of international sanctions represent a cornerstone of modern diplomatic efforts to address perceived threats to global security, particularly in the realm of nuclear proliferation. In the case of Iran, United Nations sanctions have played a pivotal role since the early 2000s, evolving in response to concerns over its nuclear activities. The year 2015 marked a significant turning point with the adoption of the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA), a multilateral agreement that curtailed Iran's nuclear programme in exchange for sanctions relief 1. However, the fragility of this arrangement became evident following the United States' withdrawal from the deal in 2018, leading to renewed tensions and questions about the potential reactivation of UN sanctions through the so-called snapback mechanism 2.

This article delves into the historical backdrop of these sanctions, the operational details of the JCPOA, and the procedural pathways for reinstating penalties. It examines key events, such as proposals to delay snapback actions by permanent Security Council members like Russia and China, Iran's retaliatory diplomatic measures including the recall of envoys from European capitals, and Tehran's assertions that any such resumption would be unlawful under international law 3. While the specific instance of a failed delay bid in late 2025 remains a hypothetical projection based on ongoing patterns, the analysis is grounded in verifiable historical precedents and current geopolitical realities as of 2023. The objective is to elucidate the complexities of this issue without endorsing any political stance, providing clarity on how multilateral institutions navigate disputes over nuclear compliance.

To fully appreciate the stakes involved, it is essential to trace the origins of Iran's nuclear programme and the international responses that shaped the sanctions landscape. This background not only contextualises recent developments but also highlights the interplay between technical nuclear safeguards, economic pressures, and diplomatic negotiations.

Background: The Origins of Iran's Nuclear Programme

Iran's engagement with nuclear technology dates back to the mid-20th century, initially under the auspices of the United States as part of the Atoms for Peace initiative. In 1957, Iran signed a civil nuclear cooperation agreement with the US, leading to the establishment of the Tehran Research Reactor in 1967, which was fuelled by highly enriched uranium supplied by American entities 4. This early phase reflected a period of alignment between Iran and Western powers during the reign of Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, who envisioned nuclear energy as a symbol of modernisation and technological advancement.

The 1979 Islamic Revolution dramatically altered this trajectory. The new regime under Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini initially suspended nuclear activities, viewing them as emblematic of Western influence. However, by the 1980s, amid the Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988), Iran revived its nuclear ambitions, seeking self-sufficiency in energy and defence capabilities. The war, which resulted in over a million casualties and widespread destruction of Iran's infrastructure, underscored the vulnerabilities of reliance on foreign powers 5. Post-war reconstruction efforts in the 1990s saw Iran expand its nuclear infrastructure, including the construction of facilities at Natanz and Arak, ostensibly for civilian purposes such as electricity generation and medical isotope production.

International suspicions arose in the early 2000s when revelations emerged about undeclared nuclear activities. In August 2002, the National Council of Resistance of Iran, an opposition group, disclosed the existence of two previously secret facilities: a uranium enrichment plant at Natanz and a heavy-water reactor at Arak 6. These disclosures prompted the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) to investigate, revealing that Iran had failed to report certain aspects of its programme in violation of its safeguards agreement under the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT), to which Iran is a signatory since 1970 7.

The NPT, established in 1968 and entering into force in 1970, forms the legal foundation for global non-proliferation efforts. It obliges non-nuclear-weapon states like Iran to forgo the development of nuclear weapons while permitting peaceful uses under IAEA oversight 8. Iran's non-compliance triggered a cascade of diplomatic measures, beginning with IAEA resolutions in 2003 calling for greater transparency. By 2006, escalating concerns led the UN Security Council to impose its first set of sanctions under Resolution 1737, targeting Iran's nuclear and missile programmes 9.

These initial sanctions prohibited the supply of nuclear-related materials and technology to Iran, froze assets of key individuals and entities involved in proliferation activities, and called for suspension of uranium enrichment. Subsequent resolutions, such as 1747 in 2007, 1803 in 2008, 1835 in 2008, 1929 in 2010, and 2231 in 2015, progressively intensified the measures, including arms embargoes, travel bans, and restrictions on Iran's access to international financial systems 10. The cumulative effect was a severe economic strain on Iran, with oil exports—its primary revenue source—plummeting and inflation soaring, exacerbating domestic hardships 11.

This sanctions regime was not unilateral but multilateral, involving coordination among the UN's P5 members (United States, United Kingdom, France, Russia, and China) alongside the European Union and other partners. However, divisions emerged early on. Russia and China, major trading partners with Iran, often advocated for restraint, vetoing harsher proposals while supporting measured escalations 12. These fissures foreshadowed the challenges in maintaining consensus, a theme that persists in discussions of sanctions resumption.

The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA): A Diplomatic Breakthrough

Negotiations to resolve the impasse culminated in the JCPOA, formally adopted on 14 July 2015 and endorsed by UN Security Council Resolution 2231 on 20 July 2015 13. The agreement was the result of over a decade of multilateral talks involving the E3+3 (United Kingdom, France, Germany, plus the US, Russia, and China, with the EU as coordinator) and Iran. Its core objective was to ensure Iran's nuclear programme remained exclusively peaceful, thereby averting the risk of weaponisation.

Under the JCPOA, Iran committed to significant limitations: reducing its operational centrifuges from nearly 20,000 to 5,060 at Natanz; capping its low-enriched uranium stockpile at 300 kilograms (far below the thousands needed for a bomb); redesigning the Arak reactor to prevent plutonium production suitable for weapons; and allowing unprecedented IAEA access, including continuous monitoring of declared sites and provisions for inspecting suspicious undeclared locations 14. In return, sanctions relief was phased in upon verification of compliance, lifting UN prohibitions on nuclear trade, ballistic missile activities (with a temporary eight-year restriction), and conventional arms (lifted after five years) 15.

The implementation of the JCPOA began on 16 January 2016, known as Implementation Day, following IAEA certification that Iran had met its initial commitments 16. This led to the termination of provisions in six previous UN resolutions (1696, 1737, 1747, 1803, 1835, and 1929), with remaining measures sunsetted over 10-15 years 17. Economically, Iran regained access to frozen assets estimated at over $100 billion and saw oil exports rebound to pre-sanctions levels by 2017 18.

Yet, the deal's architecture included safeguards against non-compliance. The snapback mechanism, embedded in Resolution 2231, allows any JCPOA participant to notify the Security Council of significant Iranian violations. This triggers a 30-day period during which the Council must vote to continue sanctions relief; absent such a vote (due to vetoes), pre-2015 sanctions automatically reinstate without further debate—a "snapback" 19. This provision was designed as a deterrent, ensuring Iran adhered to its obligations while providing an expedited enforcement tool.

The JCPOA's success was initially lauded as a model of diplomacy, with IAEA reports from 2016 to 2018 confirming Iran's compliance 20. However, domestic politics in key signatories introduced volatility. In the United States, President Donald Trump's administration viewed the deal as flawed, arguing it failed to address Iran's ballistic missiles, regional activities, or sunset clauses that would eventually restore enrichment rights 21.

United States Withdrawal and the Erosion of the JCPOA

On 8 May 2018, President Trump announced the US withdrawal from the JCPOA, reinstating unilateral sanctions under the maximum pressure campaign 22. This move isolated the US from its European allies, who sought to preserve the deal through mechanisms like the Instrument in Support of Trade Exchanges (INSTEX) to facilitate humanitarian trade with Iran bypassing US sanctions 23. Iran initially remained compliant but, facing economic strangulation— with oil exports dropping to under 300,000 barrels per day by 2019—began incremental breaches starting in May 2019 24.

These steps included exceeding the uranium stockpile limit, enriching uranium to 4.5% purity (beyond the 3.67% cap), and resuming advanced centrifuge operations 25. By 2020, Iran's stockpile reached 2,442 kilograms, enough for several bombs if further enriched 26. The COVID-19 pandemic and the assassination of Iranian nuclear scientist Mohsen Fakhrizadeh in November 2020 further complicated matters, prompting Iran to restrict IAEA access 27.

A pivotal moment came in August-September 2020 when the US, despite having withdrawn from the JCPOA, attempted to invoke the snapback mechanism 28. The Trump administration argued that its withdrawal did not preclude participation in Resolution 2231's enforcement provisions. However, this effort failed as France, Germany, the UK (E3), Russia, and China rejected the legitimacy of the US claim, leading to no Council action 29. UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned that snapback without consensus could undermine the Council's authority 30.

The Biden administration, taking office in January 2021, pursued indirect talks in Vienna to revive the JCPOA, but progress stalled over issues like Iran's demand for guarantees against future US withdrawals and the inclusion of non-nuclear matters 31. By 2022, IAEA Director General Rafael Grossi reported unresolved questions about undeclared nuclear material at three sites, heightening proliferation concerns 32. Iran's installation of advanced centrifuges and enrichment to 60% purity—near weapons-grade—escalated tensions, with the IAEA estimating in June 2023 that Iran possessed enough material for multiple warheads if weaponised 33.

In this context, discussions of sanctions resumption have intensified. European powers, frustrated by stalled negotiations, have signalled readiness to pursue snapback if Iran does not return to compliance 34. Russia and China, maintaining economic ties with Iran— including a 2022 strategic partnership agreement—have proposed extensions or delays to any snapback vote, arguing for renewed diplomacy 35. Such proposals echo historical patterns where these P5 members advocate for de-escalation to protect their interests, including Iran's role in countering Western influence in the Middle East.

Iran's Diplomatic Responses and Assertions of Illegality

Iran's reactions to potential sanctions resumption have consistently blended defiance with diplomatic outreach. Tehran maintains that its nuclear programme is for peaceful purposes, a right enshrined in Article IV of the NPT 36. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei has issued a fatwa against nuclear weapons, reiterated in 2022, though its binding nature is debated 37. In response to perceived aggressions, Iran has employed envoy recalls as a protest measure, a tactic seen in 2018 after US withdrawal and again in hypothetical escalations 38.

For instance, following the US "maximum pressure" sanctions, Iran reduced cooperation with the IAEA in 2021, leading to a censure resolution 39. Iranian officials, including Foreign Minister Hossein Amir-Abdollahian, have labelled snapback attempts as "illegal" and politically motivated, vowing non-compliance with reinstated measures 40. This stance aligns with Iran's broader narrative of sovereignty against "economic terrorism," a term used by President Ebrahim Raisi (in office 2021-2024) to describe sanctions 41.

Recalls of envoys from the UK, France, and Germany—key E3 members—serve as symbolic gestures, straining bilateral ties without severing them entirely. Historically, similar actions occurred in 2011 after EU sanctions, when Iran expelled European diplomats 42. These moves underscore Iran's strategy of multilateral engagement, including strengthening ties with non-Western powers through organisations like the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation, joined as a full member in 2023 43.

Tehran's vow against pursuing nuclear weapons, reiterated by officials in 2023, aims to assuage international fears while preserving leverage in negotiations 44. However, actions such as the 2023 unveiling of new enrichment facilities suggest a hedging strategy, maintaining breakout capability—the time to produce fissile material for a bomb, estimated at weeks by US intelligence in 2023 45.

The Snapback Mechanism: Procedural Details and Challenges

The snapback provision in Resolution 2231, operative until October 2025, is a procedural innovation unique to the JCPOA 46. Upon notification of a "significant non-performance," the complaining party refers the matter to a Joint Commission comprising JCPOA participants. If unresolved within 15 days, it escalates to ministers and then the UN Security Council 47. At the Council, a draft resolution to continue sanctions relief is tabled; if not adopted within 30 days (veto-proof due to the mechanism's design), sanctions snap back automatically 48.

This structure was intended to prevent veto paralysis, but its invocation remains untested successfully. The 2020 US attempt highlighted ambiguities: does withdrawal from the JCPOA forfeit snapback rights? Legal scholars argue that Resolution 2231 binds all UN members, but political consensus is requisite for legitimacy 49. Russia and China's delay proposals would likely involve amendments to extend the review period, requiring nine affirmative votes and no P5 vetoes 50.

Challenges include enforcement: reinstated sanctions would prohibit nuclear trade but lack the teeth of US secondary sanctions, which penalise third-party dealings 51. Moreover, Iran's potential withdrawal from the NPT, threatened in 2023 parliamentary debates, could unravel safeguards entirely 52. The mechanism's sunset in 2025 necessitates a new framework, with proposals for a follow-on resolution discussed in 2023 UN talks 53.

Geopolitical Implications and Regional Dynamics

The spectre of resumed UN sanctions reverberates across the Middle East, where Iran's nuclear ambitions intersect with proxy conflicts and great-power rivalries. Israel's longstanding opposition, viewing a nuclear Iran as an existential threat, has included covert operations like the Stuxnet cyberattack in 2010 and assassinations 54. Saudi Arabia and Gulf states, fearing regional destabilisation, have pursued their own nuclear hedging, with Riyadh signing cooperation deals with China in 2022 55.

US and European pushes for compliance stem from non-proliferation goals but also strategic containment of Iran's support for groups like Hezbollah and the Houthis 56. The 2023 Israel-Hamas war amplified concerns, with Iran-backed militias escalating attacks, prompting calls for snapback to curb Tehran's resources 57. Conversely, Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 drew Iran closer to Moscow, supplying drones and negotiating arms deals post-UN embargo lift in 2020 58. China, importing 10% of its oil from Iran in 2023 despite sanctions, uses economic leverage to advocate restraint 59.

Broader implications include erosion of multilateralism. Failed snapback bids could embolden proliferators, while success might isolate Iran further, potentially accelerating weaponisation 60. Economic fallout for Iran—GDP contraction of 7% in 2019 due to sanctions—highlights humanitarian costs, with UN experts warning of rights violations 61. Yet, resilience through smuggling and cryptocurrency evades some pressures 62.

Prospects for Resolution and Future Scenarios

Reviving the JCPOA or negotiating a successor remains viable but arduous. Vienna talks collapsed in 2022 over sequencing— Iran demands sanctions relief first, while the West insists on verifiable compliance 63. IAEA mediation and bilateral US-Iran channels offer glimmers of hope, but hardliners on both sides, including Iran's 2024 presidential transition following Raisi's death, complicate matters 64.

Future scenarios range from partial snapback, targeting specific entities, to full reinstatement if Iran enriches to 90% 65. A failed delay bid by Russia and China could unify the West but fracture the Council, echoing 2020 dynamics 66. Ultimately, sustainable resolution requires addressing root causes: mutual distrust, regional security dilemmas, and economic incentives for compliance.

This analysis demonstrates the intricate balance of law, power, and diplomacy in managing Iran's nuclear file. As global order evolves, the lessons from this saga inform broader non-proliferation strategies.
