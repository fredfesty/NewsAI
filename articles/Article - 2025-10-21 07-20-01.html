The Hawk's Ascent

Japan's First Female Prime Minister Takes Power Amid Crisis

Tuesday, 21 October 2025 07:23

Abstract

The election of Sanae Takaichi as Japan's first female Prime Minister on 21 October 20253,6 marks a historic moment, yet it is one defined by political fragility and a sharp ideological shift to the right6,7. The ultraconservative hawk, a protégé of the late Shinzo Abe3,14, secured the premiership only after a last-minute coalition deal with the right-wing Japan Innovation Party8,9,13, replacing the outgoing Shigeru Ishiba3,13. Her new minority government faces immediate, formidable challenges, including a domestic cost-of-living crisis5,16 and the high-stakes diplomatic test of hosting US President Donald Trump within days of taking office3,17.

Historical Context

  • Shigeru Ishiba was the outgoing Prime Minister.
  • Komeito party was allied with the LDP for 26 years.
  • Sanae Takaichi won the LDP leadership on 4 October 2025.
  • The LDP lost its coalition majority in both parliamentary chambers.
  • Takaichi is a protégé of the late Shinzo Abe.

  • Sanae Takaichi was chosen as PM on Tuesday, 21 October 2025.
  • LDP and Japan Innovation Party signed a coalition on 20 October 2025.

  • US President Donald Trump is scheduled to visit around 28 October 2025.
  • Satsuki Katayama is expected to be Japan’s first female Finance Minister.

A Fragile Mandate in the Diet

Sanae Takaichi, at 64 years old3,5, was formally chosen as the nation’s first female head of government by the Diet on Tuesday, 21 October 20253,7. The parliamentary vote confirmed her as the new Prime Minister, succeeding Shigeru Ishiba3,13, whose resignation followed bruising electoral losses for the Liberal Democratic Party (LDP)3,5. The LDP’s political dominance, which had been nearly uninterrupted for decades13, was severely weakened after the party lost its coalition majority in both parliamentary chambers3,8. Takaichi’s path to the premiership, despite winning the LDP leadership on 4 October 202512,15, was almost derailed when the LDP’s long-standing partner, the moderate centrist Komeito party, abandoned the coalition8,12. Komeito, which had been allied with the LDP for 26 years5,8, cited concerns over Takaichi’s ultraconservative politics and the LDP’s handling of corruption scandals as reasons for its departure5,8. This split forced Takaichi into a desperate search for a new partner to secure the necessary votes5. The solution arrived in the form of the right-wing Japan Innovation Party, known as Nippon Ishin no Kai or JIP8,9,11. The LDP and JIP signed a coalition agreement on the evening of Monday, 20 October 20259,11, just one day before the parliamentary vote7,13. The new alliance, however, is a minority government, holding a combined 231 seats in the powerful lower house, which is two votes short of an outright majority8,9,12. This precarious position means the Takaichi administration will be dependent on cooperation from other opposition groups to pass any legislation, including a supplementary budget5,8,12. The political instability is compounded by the fact that Takaichi is Japan’s fifth prime minister in as many years8,12, leading many observers to anticipate a short-lived government and an early election5.

The Ultraconservative Pedigree

Takaichi’s political identity is rooted firmly in the hard-line conservative wing of the LDP4,8,15. She is widely regarded as a protégé of the late former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe3,14, having served in senior roles during his tenure, including as Minister for Internal Affairs and Communications4. Her political views are often described as ultraconservative3,5 and nationalist3,4, with some within the LDP placing her on the far right of the party’s political spectrum4. Takaichi has openly cited former British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher as a role model3,4,15, earning her the nickname 'Iron Lady'4,22. Her policy platform is closely aligned with Abe’s vision, particularly on security and constitutional reform8,22. She is a member of the ultranationalist organisation Nippon Kaigi4 and has been a proponent of revising Article 9 of Japan’s pacifist postwar constitution4,8,22. This revision would formally recognise the role of the Japan Self-Defence Forces8. On historical issues, Takaichi has been described as a wartime history revisionist22, having argued that Japanese war crimes have been exaggerated4. Her regular visits to the controversial Yasukuni Shrine in Tokyo, which honours Japan’s war dead including convicted war criminals4,8,22, have consistently drawn protests from China and South Korea4,19. During her most recent LDP leadership campaign, however, she displayed a degree of pragmatism by avoiding comment on whether she would continue to visit the shrine as Prime Minister4,19 and skipping the autumn festival, a move interpreted as an attempt to avoid a diplomatic row with China16. Domestically, her social conservatism is equally pronounced4,8. She opposes same-sex marriage4,5,8 and has fought against a revision to the civil law that would allow married couples to use separate surnames3,4,8, arguing that such a change would undermine traditional family values18. She also supports the male-only succession tradition for the imperial family4,5,15. Takaichi has also tapped into populist sentiment with a 'Japan First' promise3 and a hard-line stance on immigration4,8,11,18, calling for restrictions on non-Japanese buying property18 and a cap on the number of foreign nationals in a government demographic strategy11.

The Economic Tightrope and 'Sanaenomics'

The most immediate domestic challenge for the new administration is navigating a cost-of-living crisis marked by rising prices and stagnant wages3,5,16. Takaichi’s economic vision is a continuation of the 'Abenomics' blueprint14, centred on fiscal stimulus and loose monetary policy14,19. She favours a 'high-pressure economy policy'10, which uses fiscal and monetary tools to maintain excess demand, thereby encouraging supply capacity expansion through investment10. Her approach, sometimes dubbed 'Sanaenomics'15, advocates for 'responsible proactive fiscal policy'10 and a willingness to issue deficit bonds when necessary10. To address public frustration with inflation, she has proposed a combination of tax cuts and subsidies14,19, focusing on supporting working-age households through measures like refundable tax credits10. This focus on increasing take-home pay through tax credits is preferred over setting numerical wage targets, which she believes are decisions for private companies10. The initial market reaction to her dovish monetary policy stance was a depreciation of the yen and a rally in stock prices10,19. However, the economic policy faces a significant hurdle in the form of the new coalition partner, the Japan Innovation Party16. While Takaichi is a fiscal expansionist, the JIP generally advocates for fiscal conservatism and small government5,8. The coalition agreement includes joint policy goals, but potential friction points remain, such as the JIP’s demands for a two-year suspension of the 8% consumption tax on food8,16 and a ban on corporate political donations16. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) has already urged the new government to avoid blanket subsidies or cutting the consumption tax, advising instead for targeted, temporary support for low-income households20. The LDP’s weakened minority position in the Diet19,20 means that Takaichi’s ability to push through structural reforms, which were the 'third arrow' of Abenomics19, will be severely constrained, risking a stall in legislation needed to address Japan’s chronic labour shortages and rapidly ageing population20.

The Diplomatic Baptism of Fire

Takaichi’s international debut is set to be a diplomatic baptism of fire, beginning with the scheduled visit of US President Donald Trump3,16. The US-Japan Summit is expected to take place around 28 October 202517, just days after her election3. This rapid turnaround is unusual for a new Japanese premier3, and Takaichi will have only three business days to prepare for the meeting with the unpredictable American leader3. The relationship, however, has a built-in icebreaker: Takaichi’s close alignment with the late Shinzo Abe3, who forged a strong personal friendship with President Trump during his first term3. Takaichi has pledged unwavering support for the US-Japan security alliance4,17, and her hawkish views on defence spending may align with President Trump’s desire for allies to take on a greater role in their national security3. Despite her 'Japan First' rhetoric3, she faces the complex task of balancing the demands of the Trump administration with her own nationalistic priorities3. Beyond the US, Takaichi’s foreign policy is defined by a tough stance on regional rivals3. She is considered a China hawk4,9,12 and is pro-Taiwan4. Her revisionist views on wartime history and past visits to the Yasukuni Shrine5,8 have the potential to imperil relations with both China and South Korea3,4. While she is expected to keep ties stable5, her long history of right-wing stances on historical issues is a cause for worry in Seoul19. Following the Trump visit, Takaichi is scheduled to make her international debut as Prime Minister at the Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) summit in South Korea8,16, where her diplomatic skills will be immediately scrutinised16. On trade, she supports the expansion of the Comprehensive and Progressive Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP)14 and has hinted at the possibility of renegotiating a trade and investment deal with the US if certain terms conflict with Japan’s national interests14.

The Gender Paradox

Takaichi’s election is a symbolic milestone, shattering the 'ultimate glass ceiling' in a country that consistently ranks poorly in global gender equality comparisons3,5,12,16. According to the World Economic Forum’s 2025 Global Gender Gap Report, Japan ranked 118 out of 148 countries12. However, the historic nature of her appointment is complicated by a profound gender paradox5. Takaichi is an ultraconservative figure who has actively opposed key measures for women’s advancement5. Her opposition to allowing married couples to use separate surnames3,4,8 and her support for male-only imperial succession4,5,15 are seen by many as stonewalling progress on gender issues5. Sociologist Chizuko Ueno noted that while Takaichi’s leadership might elevate Japan’s gender equality ranking, it does not necessarily mean Japanese politics will become 'kinder to women'5. Despite this, Takaichi has promised to appoint a cabinet with 'Nordic' levels of women12,16, a significant increase from the two female ministers under the outgoing Ishiba administration12. Early reports suggest that former minister Satsuki Katayama, who has a strong background in economics and finance12, will be appointed as Japan’s first female Finance Minister12,16. This move is seen as an attempt to make good on her promise and signal a commitment to female representation at the highest levels of government16. The paradox of a conservative hawk breaking the gender barrier underscores the complex, often contradictory, nature of political progress in Japan’s rigidly patriarchal society3,12.

Conclusion

Sanae Takaichi’s ascent to the premiership on 21 October 20253,6 represents a victory for the right wing of the Liberal Democratic Party18 and a historic moment for female representation in Japan3,16. Yet, the circumstances of her election—a last-minute coalition with the Japan Innovation Party9,11 following the loss of the LDP’s majority3,8—have created a government defined by instability and a lack of a clear mandate for radical change5,20. Her administration is immediately confronted by a volatile geopolitical landscape, symbolised by the imminent visit of US President Donald Trump3,17, and a pressing domestic cost-of-living crisis5,16. Takaichi’s commitment to the Abenomics blueprint14 and her hawkish stance on security and constitutional revision4,8 signal a continuation of the nationalist project championed by her mentor3,19. However, the need to coordinate with a fiscally conservative coalition partner8,16 and a fragmented legislature20 will test her pragmatic streak16. The long-term success of her premiership will depend less on the symbolism of her gender and more on her ability to forge a stable governing consensus while navigating the deep ideological and economic fissures that brought her to power5,19.
