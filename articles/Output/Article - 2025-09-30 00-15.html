Background

The Israeli-Palestinian conflict is one of the world's most enduring and intractable disputes, with roots stretching back to the late 19th and early 20th centuries. The establishment of the State of Israel in 1948, following the United Nations Partition Plan for Palestine, led to the displacement of a significant Palestinian population and marked the beginning of decades of conflict, occupation, and intermittent warfare 1011. Gaza, a densely populated Palestinian territory bordering Israel and Egypt, has been a focal point of this conflict, particularly since Hamas, an Islamist militant group, seized control in 2007 12. This takeover led to a blockade imposed by Israel and Egypt, severely restricting the movement of people and goods, and contributing to a dire humanitarian situation within the territory 13.

Numerous peace initiatives have been launched over the years, including the Oslo Accords in the 1990s, various US-led mediation efforts, and international conferences, none of which have resulted in a lasting resolution 1415. The core issues of the conflict typically include the status of Jerusalem, the right of return for Palestinian refugees, Israeli settlements in the West Bank, and security arrangements for Israel 16.

The Trump administration, which took office in January 2017, adopted a distinct approach to Middle East diplomacy, often diverging from established international consensus. Early in its tenure, the administration moved the US embassy in Israel to Jerusalem, a move that was widely condemned by the international community and seen as prejudging the final status of the city 17. The administration also significantly reduced funding to the United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees in the Near East (UNRWA) 18. In early 2020, the Trump administration unveiled its own peace proposal, dubbed "Peace to Prosperity," which was largely rejected by the Palestinians, who accused the US of bias towards Israel 19.

Against this backdrop, the events of late September 2025, involving the unveiling of a new peace plan for Gaza by President Trump and Prime Minister Netanyahu, represent a continuation of this administration's distinctive engagement with the conflict. The specific context for this new plan appears to be the ongoing hostilities and the urgent need for a resolution, including the release of hostages. The reported consideration of former British Prime Minister Tony Blair for a role in a potential postwar governing authority also signals a potential shift in the proposed framework for Gaza's future governance, moving beyond the immediate cessation of hostilities to longer-term stability 2021.

For any peace agreement to be sustainable, it must have the buy-in of all relevant parties. Hamas's position as the de facto authority in Gaza meant that their cooperation, or at least their acquiescence, would be essential for the implementation of any ceasefire, hostage release, or future governance arrangements. Without their direct involvement in the planning process, the plan risked being perceived as imposed rather than negotiated, significantly diminishing its chances of success 5.

The broader Palestinian political landscape also presented challenges. The Palestinian Authority (PA), based in the West Bank, has its own complex relationship with Hamas and a history of differing approaches to peace negotiations with Israel. Any plan that did not adequately address the concerns and aspirations of the Palestinian people as a whole, and potentially undermine the PA's legitimacy, would likely face significant opposition 1619.

Israel's internal political dynamics also played a crucial role. Prime Minister Netanyahu's government would need to maintain a strong coalition and secure the support of key security and political factions for the plan to be viable. The differing views within Israeli society regarding settlements, security, and the future of the Palestinian territories could create internal pressures that might affect the government's ability to commit to and implement the plan 31.

The international dimension was equally critical. While the US was a key proponent, securing broad international support from key players such as the European Union, key Arab states, and the United Nations would be vital for the plan's legitimacy and practical implementation 32. Past peace initiatives have often faltered due to a lack of unified international backing or conflicting agendas among global powers 14.

The prospect of a postwar governing authority, potentially involving international figures like Tony Blair, presented both opportunities and challenges 112021. While it could offer a path towards stability and reconstruction, it also raised questions about sovereignty, accountability, and the potential for external imposition. The success of such an authority would depend on its ability to gain local legitimacy and operate effectively within the complex realities of Gaza 24.

Despite these formidable challenges, the plan's focus on an immediate ceasefire and hostage release offered a potential pathway to de-escalation and humanitarian relief 14. If these immediate objectives could be achieved, they might create a more conducive environment for addressing the more complex political and governance issues in the longer term. However, the prospects for a lasting resolution remained uncertain, contingent on a delicate balance of political will, diplomatic skill, and a willingness to compromise from all parties involved.
