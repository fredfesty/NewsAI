Japan Is Set for Its First Female Prime Minister

A New Era in a Nation of Political Tradition

Article created and last updated on: Sunday 05 October 2025 00:15

Abstract

Japan stands on the precipice of a monumental political transformation with the election of Sanae Takaichi as the leader of the governing Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) 5. This development paves the way for her to become the nation's first female Prime Minister, a landmark event in a country with a deeply entrenched patriarchal political culture 3, 19. Takaichi, a 64-year-old political veteran known for her staunchly conservative and nationalist views, ascends to the leadership at a time of significant political instability and pressing domestic and international challenges 3, 4. Her victory over her more moderate rival, Shinjiro Koizumi, in a closely contested party election signals a potential shift to the right in Japanese politics 7, 32. This article provides a comprehensive analysis of the political context that led to Takaichi's rise, her political career and ideological underpinnings, her key policy positions, and the potential implications of her premiership for Japan's domestic and foreign policy landscape. It also examines the historical context of women in Japanese politics and the broader societal implications of this historic appointment.

Introduction

On 4 October 2025, the ruling Liberal Democratic Party of Japan elected Sanae Takaichi as its new president, a decision that has sent ripples across the nation and the international community 5, 7. As the leader of the majority party, Takaichi is all but certain to be confirmed as Japan's next Prime Minister by the National Diet 6, 7. This would mark a watershed moment in Japanese history, shattering the ultimate glass ceiling in a political arena long dominated by men 3, 18. The significance of this event cannot be overstated, particularly in a country that consistently ranks low in global gender equality indices 18, 19.

Takaichi's rise to power is not merely a symbolic victory for gender representation; it is also a reflection of the deep-seated political currents shaping contemporary Japan. Her election comes at a time of profound challenges for the nation, including a stagnant economy, a rapidly ageing population, and a complex and often tense geopolitical environment in East Asia 3, 14. The LDP itself has been grappling with declining public support and internal divisions, exacerbated by recent electoral setbacks 10, 16. Takaichi, a protégé of the late former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, is seen by many within the party as a figure who can galvanise the conservative base and provide strong leadership in uncertain times 3, 29.

This article seeks to provide a detailed and objective examination of the factors that have culminated in this historic moment. It will delve into the background of Japan's political landscape, tracing the trajectory of the LDP and the societal norms that have historically limited the political advancement of women. The article will then offer an in-depth profile of Sanae Takaichi, exploring her early life, her entry into politics, and the evolution of her political ideology. A critical analysis of her policy stances on key issues such as the economy, national security, and social affairs will follow. Finally, the article will assess the potential domestic and international ramifications of a Takaichi premiership, considering the opportunities and challenges that lie ahead for Japan's first female leader.

The Political Landscape of Post-War Japan and the Dominance of the LDP

To comprehend the significance of Sanae Takaichi’s ascendancy, it is essential to understand the political framework within which it has occurred. For the majority of the post-Second World War era, Japanese politics has been dominated by the Liberal Democratic Party. Formed in 1955 through a merger of two conservative parties, the LDP has governed Japan almost uninterruptedly, with only brief interludes in opposition 3. This long-standing dominance has created a stable, albeit often criticised, political system. The LDP's success has been attributed to a combination of factors, including its close ties with the business community and the bureaucracy, its ability to deliver economic growth during the post-war "economic miracle", and a relatively weak and fragmented opposition.

The process of selecting a Prime Minister in Japan is intrinsically linked to the leadership of the ruling party 23. Unlike in presidential systems, the Japanese public does not directly elect their head of government. Instead, the Prime Minister is chosen by the National Diet, Japan's bicameral legislature 21, 25. Given the LDP's historical majority in the powerful House of Representatives, the party's president has, by default, become the Prime Minister 13, 25. Therefore, the LDP leadership election is, in effect, the contest that determines the nation's leader 27. These internal party elections are often opaque and heavily influenced by factional politics within the LDP 27.

A Patriarchal Political System: The Historical Underrepresentation of Women

Despite Japan's status as a modern, developed democracy, its political landscape has remained stubbornly patriarchal. The underrepresentation of women in Japanese politics is a well-documented and persistent issue 18. For decades, the corridors of power in Nagatacho, Tokyo’s political heartland, have been overwhelmingly male-dominated. In the House of Representatives, the more powerful of the two parliamentary chambers, women comprise only about 15% of its members 18, 35. At the local level, the situation is equally stark, with only two of Japan’s 47 prefectural governors being women 18, 35.

Several cultural, social, and structural factors contribute to this gender disparity. Traditional gender roles, which place a strong emphasis on women's domestic responsibilities, have historically discouraged female participation in public life 22. The demanding nature of a political career in Japan, with its long hours and extensive networking requirements, can be particularly challenging for women who are still expected to be the primary caregivers. Furthermore, the LDP's "old boys' network" and its factional system have often been cited as significant barriers to women's political advancement. Critics argue that the party has been slow to embrace female candidates and has not done enough to create a supportive environment for women in politics 18. The election of Sanae Takaichi, therefore, represents a significant departure from this long-standing tradition.

The Preceding Political Instability

Takaichi's election as LDP leader follows a period of considerable political churn in Japan. The country has seen a succession of short-term prime ministers in recent years, a trend that has raised concerns about political stability and policy continuity 3. The resignation of Shigeru Ishiba as party president and Prime Minister in September 2025, after less than a year in office, precipitated the leadership contest that brought Takaichi to the forefront 3, 16. Ishiba's departure was prompted by the LDP's poor performance in the 2024 general election and the 2025 House of Councillors election, which resulted in the party losing its majority in both chambers of the Diet 16. This electoral decline reflected public dissatisfaction with the LDP over a range of issues, including economic stagnation and a series of political scandals 4, 10. In this context, the LDP was under pressure to select a leader who could restore public trust and navigate the party through a challenging political landscape 30.

Early Life and Education

Sanae Takaichi was born on 7 March 1961, in Nara Prefecture, located in the Kansai region of Japan 2. Unlike many of her political peers who hail from established political dynasties, Takaichi's background is more modest; her father was a manufacturing company employee and her mother was a police officer 15, 26. She graduated from Kobe University in 1984 with a degree in business 15. In a departure from the typical career path of a Japanese politician, Takaichi spent a year in the United States from 1987 to 1988 as a congressional fellow, working in the office of the late feminist Democratic Representative Patricia Schroeder 15, 26. This experience provided her with early exposure to the workings of a different political system. Before entering the political arena, Takaichi worked as a television presenter and author 2, 15. An unconventional figure in many respects, in her youth she was known to be a drummer in a heavy-metal band and a motorcycle enthusiast 18, 19.

Entry into Politics and Early Career

Takaichi's political career began in 1993 when she was elected to the House of Representatives as an independent 2, 9. She later joined the New Frontier Party before becoming a member of the Liberal Democratic Party in 1996 2, 24. Within the LDP, she became a protégé of the influential former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, with whom she shared a close political and ideological alignment 3, 29. Over the course of her long parliamentary career, Takaichi has held a series of prominent cabinet positions, demonstrating her political acumen and gaining extensive experience in government. Her ministerial portfolios have included Minister for Internal Affairs and Communications, Minister of State for Gender Equality, and most recently, Minister of State for Economic Security from 2022 to 2024 2, 3, 19. These roles have given her a broad understanding of domestic and security policy.

Political Ideology: A Staunch Conservative and Nationalist

Sanae Takaichi is widely regarded as one of the most conservative and nationalist figures within the LDP 3, 5. Her political philosophy is deeply rooted in a desire to promote a strong and assertive Japan on the world stage. She has often cited the former British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher as a role model, admiring her strong leadership and conviction 3, 18. Takaichi is a member of the influential and ultranationalist lobby group, Nippon Kaigi 2. Her political stances are often described as being on the far-right of the LDP's political spectrum 2.

Her nationalism is reflected in her views on Japan's wartime history. She is a known revisionist who has questioned the extent of Japan's war crimes and has been a frequent visitor to the controversial Yasukuni Shrine, which honours convicted war criminals among Japan's war dead 3, 18. These visits are a source of considerable diplomatic friction with China and South Korea, who view the shrine as a symbol of Japan's past militarism 18. Takaichi has also expressed opposition to the Kono and Murayama Statements, which offered official apologies for Japan's wartime aggression, including the issue of "comfort women" 2.

In terms of social policy, Takaichi holds deeply conservative views. She is a staunch opponent of same-sex marriage and has spoken out against proposals to allow married couples to use separate surnames, arguing that such a move would undermine traditional family values 2, 17. She also opposes any changes to the imperial succession law that would allow a woman to ascend to the Chrysanthemum Throne 2. These positions have drawn criticism from more progressive quarters of Japanese society and have led some to question whether her premiership will genuinely advance the cause of gender equality, despite her gender 14.

The 2025 Liberal Democratic Party Leadership Election

The 2025 LDP leadership election was a fiercely contested race, with five candidates vying for the top job 16, 34. The election was held on 4 October 2025, following the resignation of Prime Minister Shigeru Ishiba 16, 30. The main contenders were Sanae Takaichi and the more moderate Agriculture Minister, Shinjiro Koizumi, the son of the popular former Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi 5, 37. The campaign was dominated by debates over the future direction of the party and the country, with Takaichi positioning herself as a strong, decisive leader who could revive the LDP's fortunes and defend Japan's national interests 30.

The LDP leadership election is a two-stage process 27. In the first round, votes are cast by LDP members of the Diet and rank-and-file party members across the country 3. If no candidate secures an overall majority, a runoff is held between the top two candidates 27. In the 2025 election, no candidate achieved a majority in the first round 6. Takaichi came in first, securing 183 of the 589 votes, with Koizumi in second place with 164 votes 7, 16. In the subsequent runoff, which is weighted more heavily towards the votes of Diet members, Takaichi emerged victorious, securing 185 votes to Koizumi's 156 6, 7. Her victory was seen as a triumph for the conservative wing of the party and a testament to her effective campaigning.

Economic Policy: A Continuation of "Abenomics"

Sanae Takaichi's economic policy platform is largely seen as a continuation and reinforcement of "Abenomics", the signature economic policies of her mentor, Shinzo Abe 2. This approach is characterised by a three-pronged strategy of aggressive monetary easing, flexible fiscal policy, and structural reforms aimed at stimulating economic growth. Takaichi is a proponent of expansionary fiscal policy and has advocated for significant public spending to revitalise the Japanese economy 17. She has indicated that she would not be overly concerned about the country's large public debt in the short term, prioritising economic recovery above fiscal consolidation. In terms of monetary policy, her victory is expected to ensure the continuation of the Bank of Japan's ultra-loose monetary stance, making an imminent interest rate hike less likely 33.

Takaichi has also expressed a degree of economic nationalism. She has raised the possibility of renegotiating a trade and investment deal with the United States if it is deemed to be detrimental to Japan's national interests 17. This stance reflects her "Japan First" foreign policy approach, which prioritises the country's own economic well-being 3.

National Security and Foreign Policy: A Hawkish Stance

In the realm of national security and foreign policy, Sanae Takaichi is known for her hawkish and assertive views 3. A key plank of her platform is a commitment to strengthening Japan's defence capabilities. She is a strong advocate for increasing the country's defence budget and has expressed support for revising Japan's post-war pacifist constitution to clarify the legal status of the Self-Defence Forces 34. Her overarching goal is to enable Japan to play a more proactive role in regional security and to be less reliant on its security alliance with the United States.

Her stance on China is particularly firm. She views China as a significant strategic challenge and has called for a more robust response to its growing military and economic power 29. Takaichi has also been a vocal supporter of Taiwan and has advocated for closer ties with the self-governing island, a position that is likely to be a source of tension with Beijing 2, 8. She has reiterated Abe's assertion that a "Taiwan emergency is a Japan emergency" 2. Takaichi's foreign policy is expected to be characterised by a strong emphasis on the U.S.-Japan alliance, while also seeking to build a network of like-minded democracies in the Indo-Pacific region to counterbalance China's influence 3. However, her historical revisionism and nationalist rhetoric could complicate relations with South Korea, a key regional partner 3, 18.

Social and Immigration Policy: A Conservative Vision

Sanae Takaichi's social policy positions are deeply conservative and reflect her traditionalist worldview 22. As previously noted, she is opposed to same-sex marriage and the introduction of separate surnames for married couples 2, 17. She has also voiced her opposition to any changes to the imperial succession law that would permit a female to ascend the throne 2. These stances have drawn criticism from those who argue that they are out of step with a changing Japanese society. While she has pledged to increase the number of female ministers in her cabinet, some experts have expressed concern that her overall policy agenda may not lead to substantive progress in gender equality 18, 35.

On immigration, Takaichi has adopted a restrictive and cautious approach. She has called for tighter controls on immigration and has expressed concerns about the social impact of allowing in people from different cultural backgrounds 14. This position is seen as an attempt to appeal to conservative voters who are wary of the social changes that could result from increased immigration, a topic that has become more prominent in Japanese political discourse in recent years 17.

A Shift to the Right

The election of Sanae Takaichi as leader of the LDP and presumptive Prime Minister is widely expected to shift Japan's political centre of gravity to the right 3. Her staunchly conservative and nationalist views are a departure from the more moderate stances of her recent predecessors. This ideological shift could have significant implications for a range of domestic policy areas, from social issues to constitutional reform. Her supporters hope that her strong leadership will bring a renewed sense of purpose to the LDP and the country. However, her critics fear that her policies could deepen social divisions and alienate more liberal and centrist voters.

Challenges of Governing a Divided Nation

Takaichi will take the helm of a nation facing a multitude of domestic challenges. The Japanese economy continues to struggle with low growth and the pressures of a rapidly ageing and shrinking population 3, 14. The COVID-19 pandemic has exacerbated these economic woes, and rising prices are a growing concern for many households 3, 4. Takaichi's promise of increased public spending may offer some short-term relief, but she will also need to articulate a long-term vision for sustainable economic growth.

Furthermore, Takaichi inherits a deeply divided LDP and a fragmented political landscape 10, 11. The party's recent electoral setbacks have exposed internal rifts and a decline in public trust 16. Takaichi's immediate task will be to unite the party behind her leadership and to win back the support of the electorate 7. Given that the LDP and its coalition partner no longer hold a majority in either house of the Diet, she will need to engage in delicate negotiations with opposition parties to pass legislation 7, 14. This will require a degree of political skill and pragmatism that may be at odds with her strong ideological convictions.

The Symbolic Significance of a Female Prime Minister

The symbolic importance of Japan having its first female Prime Minister cannot be overlooked. In a society where gender inequality remains a significant challenge, Takaichi's appointment is a powerful statement about the changing role of women in Japanese society 3. It has the potential to inspire a new generation of women to pursue careers in politics and other leadership positions. However, the extent to which Takaichi's premiership will translate into tangible improvements in gender equality is a matter of debate. Her conservative social views and her opposition to policies aimed at promoting women's rights have led many feminists and gender equality advocates to be sceptical of her commitment to their cause 14, 22. They argue that true progress requires not just a female leader, but a leader who is actively committed to dismantling the structural barriers that hold women back.

Strengthening the U.S.-Japan Alliance

The U.S.-Japan alliance is the cornerstone of Japan's foreign and security policy, and it is expected to remain so under a Takaichi premiership. Takaichi is a firm believer in the importance of the alliance and has called for even greater cooperation between the two countries 3. She has expressed confidence in her ability to build a strong personal relationship with the current U.S. administration, citing a shared "America First" and "Japan First" approach to foreign policy 5, 33. Her focus on strengthening Japan's own defence capabilities is also likely to be welcomed by Washington, which has long encouraged Japan to take on a greater share of the security burden in the region.

Navigating Tense Relations with China and South Korea

One of the most significant foreign policy challenges facing Takaichi will be managing Japan's complex and often fraught relationships with its East Asian neighbours, China and South Korea. Her hawkish stance on China and her historical revisionism are likely to lead to increased tensions with Beijing 3, 32. The Chinese government has already responded to her election with a call for Japan to "honour its political commitments on major issues such as history and Taiwan" 8, 32. Takaichi will need to strike a delicate balance between standing firm on Japan's national interests and avoiding a dangerous escalation of tensions with its largest trading partner.

Relations with South Korea are also likely to be tested. Takaichi's visits to the Yasukuni Shrine and her views on wartime history are a source of deep resentment in South Korea, which suffered under Japanese colonial rule 18. Improving ties with Seoul, which is a key partner in dealing with the threat from North Korea, will require a high degree of diplomatic skill and a willingness to engage in dialogue, even on sensitive historical issues.

Japan's Role in the Indo-Pacific

Takaichi is expected to continue and strengthen Japan's engagement in the wider Indo-Pacific region. She is a proponent of the "Free and Open Indo-Pacific" concept, a strategic vision that was championed by Shinzo Abe 3. This strategy aims to promote a rules-based international order and to counter China's growing influence in the region. Takaichi is likely to seek closer security and economic ties with countries such as Australia, India, and the nations of Southeast Asia. Her leadership could see Japan taking on a more prominent role in regional security initiatives and in shaping the strategic architecture of the Indo-Pacific.

Challenges and Opportunities Ahead

Sanae Takaichi's premiership will be defined by her ability to navigate a series of formidable challenges and to seize the opportunities that lie before her. Her immediate priorities will be to unite a fractured LDP, restore public confidence in her party, and address the pressing economic concerns of the Japanese people 28, 31. She will also need to forge a working relationship with the opposition parties in the Diet to ensure the smooth passage of legislation 14.

On the international front, Takaichi will be immediately confronted with a complex geopolitical landscape. She will need to manage the delicate trilateral relationship between Japan, the United States, and China, while also dealing with the ongoing threat from North Korea 12. The upcoming APEC summit and a potential meeting with the U.S. President will be early tests of her diplomatic skills 3, 31. Her success will depend on her ability to balance her strong nationalist convictions with the pragmatic demands of international diplomacy.

Despite these challenges, Takaichi's premiership also presents a number of opportunities. Her election as Japan's first female Prime Minister is a historic moment that could usher in a new era of political engagement for women in Japan. Her strong leadership style could provide the decisiveness that many feel has been lacking in Japanese politics in recent years. If she is able to successfully navigate the challenges she faces, she has the potential to leave a lasting mark on Japanese politics and society.

Conclusion

The election of Sanae Takaichi as the leader of Japan's ruling Liberal Democratic Party and the country's presumptive first female Prime Minister is a development of profound historical significance. It represents a dramatic break with centuries of tradition in a nation where political power has long been the exclusive domain of men. Takaichi's rise is a testament to her own political skill and determination, as well as to the shifting political tides within the LDP and the country as a whole.

Her premiership is poised to usher in a period of significant change for Japan, both at home and abroad. Her staunchly conservative and nationalist ideology suggests a move to the right in Japanese politics, with potential implications for a wide range of policy areas, from the economy and national security to social and immigration issues. She inherits a country facing a daunting array of challenges, and her ability to provide effective leadership in these uncertain times will be the ultimate measure of her success.

The world will be watching closely as Sanae Takaichi takes her place on the global stage. Her leadership will not only shape the future of Japan but will also have a significant impact on the geopolitical dynamics of the Indo-Pacific region. Whether she will be a transformative leader who can revitalise Japan and enhance its standing in the world, or a divisive figure who deepens social and political divides, remains to be seen. What is certain, however, is that her premiership marks the beginning of a new and uncertain chapter in the history of modern Japan.
