Background

The History and Significance of Oktoberfest

To fully comprehend the gravity of the threat against Oktoberfest, it is essential to understand the festival's deep-rooted cultural and historical significance. The origins of Oktoberfest date back to 12 October 1810, with the celebration of the marriage of Crown Prince Ludwig of Bavaria, who would later become King Ludwig I, to Princess Therese of Saxe-Hildburghausen. 1, 4 The citizens of Munich were invited to attend the festivities, which were held on the fields in front of the city gates. 4 These fields were subsequently named Theresienwiese ("Therese's meadow") in honour of the Crown Princess, a name that is still used today, often abbreviated to "Wiesn". 3, 5 The inaugural event concluded with a horse race, which was so popular that the decision was made to repeat it the following year, thus establishing the annual tradition of Oktoberfest. 2, 4

Over the ensuing two centuries, Oktoberfest evolved from a royal wedding celebration into the world's largest folk festival. 5 In its early years, an agricultural fair was added to promote the Bavarian economy. 2 The first carousel and swings appeared in 1818, marking the beginning of the festival's transformation into an amusement fair. 3 While beer stands were present in the early gatherings, it was in 1896 that they were replaced by the large, brewery-sponsored beer tents that are now a hallmark of the event. 2 The festival has been cancelled on numerous occasions throughout its history, primarily due to wars and pandemics. 1, 3 In 1980, Oktoberfest was the target of a deadly far-right terrorist attack, when a pipe bomb killed 13 people and injured over 200. 2 This event remains a dark chapter in the festival's history and has had a lasting impact on security considerations.

Today, Oktoberfest is a global phenomenon, attracting approximately six million visitors annually. 5 The festival is a vital part of Bavarian culture and a significant contributor to the local economy. The event is a celebration of Bavarian traditions, featuring traditional music, parades, and cuisine. 2 The threat of violence against such a cherished and iconic event, therefore, represents not only a danger to public safety but also an assault on cultural identity.

The Events of 1 October 2025

The Domestic Incident in Lerchenau

The events that led to the closure of Oktoberfest began in the early hours of 1 October 2025, in the Lerchenau district of northern Munich. At approximately 5 a.m. local time, residents reported hearing loud bangs and seeing flames at a residential property. 4 A 57-year-old German man, whose name has not been released by the authorities, initiated a violent attack on his family. 2 He used a self-made weapon to open fire on his parents, killing his 90-year-old father and injuring his 81-year-old mother. 2 The perpetrator's 21-year-old daughter was also injured in the attack. 2, 3

Following the shootings, the man set fire to the house, which he had booby-trapped with explosives, reportedly grenades attached to tripwires. 2, 3 He also set fire to three vehicles outside the property, including his own van. 2 The first responders, who arrived at the scene shortly after the initial reports, were faced with a complex and dangerous situation. The house was engulfed in flames, and the presence of explosives hampered their efforts to access the property and recover the body of the deceased. 2, 3 The perpetrator's mother was found injured in the garden, while his daughter was rescued by firefighters from the first floor of the burning building. 2

The Bomb Threat and the Closure of Oktoberfest

After the attack on his family, the perpetrator fled the scene. During his rampage, he had dropped a letter into a neighbour's letterbox. 1 This letter contained a "non-specific" bomb threat directed at the Oktoberfest. 4 The discovery of this note prompted an immediate and decisive response from the authorities. The Lord Mayor of Munich, Dieter Reiter, confirmed that there was a "verified bomb threat" against the festival. 2

Given the credible nature of the threat, the decision was made to close and evacuate the Theresienwiese, the grounds where Oktoberfest is held. 2 This was a significant undertaking, as the festival attracts hundreds of thousands of visitors each day. The closure was announced at approximately 10 a.m. local time, and the festival grounds were cleared of all visitors and staff. 4 A massive security operation was launched, involving over 500 police officers, who conducted a thorough search of the entire festival area for explosives. 4 Sniffer dogs and other specialised equipment were deployed to ensure that the grounds were safe. 5

The Suspect's Suicide and the Reopening of the Festival

While the security sweep was underway at the Theresienwiese, a manhunt was in progress for the suspect. A police helicopter spotted the man in the garden of the family home before he fled. 2 After a brief pursuit, he was cornered by police near Lake Lerchenau, where he took his own life. 2, 3 The suspect was carrying a backpack containing an explosive device, which had to be defused by a bomb squad. 2, 4

After a seven-hour closure, during which the police conducted a meticulous search of the Oktoberfest grounds and found no explosives, the festival was declared safe and reopened to the public at 5:30 p.m. local time. 1 The Lord Mayor of Munich, Dieter Reiter, expressed his relief and thanked the emergency services for their swift and professional response. 1

The Perpetrator and Potential Motives

The perpetrator of these acts was a 57-year-old German citizen who resided in Starnberg, a town southwest of Munich. 1, 4 He worked as a caretaker, craftsman, and garden designer. 3 According to the Bavarian Interior Minister, Joachim Herrmann, there was no indication of a political or religious motive for the man's actions. 2 The incident appears to have been rooted in a "family matter". 2 It was reported that the suspect had questioned the paternity of his 21-year-old daughter, although a test had confirmed that he was the father. 1 This suggests that the perpetrator may have been suffering from severe psychological distress, which ultimately manifested in a violent and destructive outburst. The investigation into the precise motives for his actions is ongoing.

Security at Major Public Events in Germany

The incident at the 2025 Oktoberfest has once again brought the issue of security at major public events to the forefront of public discourse in Germany. The country has a robust and multi-layered approach to event security, which has been continually refined in response to past incidents, both domestically and internationally.

The security concept for Oktoberfest, for example, is developed in close cooperation between the city of Munich and the police. 4 The festival grounds are fenced off, and visitors are subject to security checks at the entrances. 4 Bags and rucksacks are restricted in size, and their contents are inspected. 4 The entire Theresienwiese is monitored by a network of over 50 cameras, and a public address system is in place to communicate with the crowd in case of an emergency. 4 A dedicated police station is located on the festival grounds, and around 600 officers are deployed to ensure the safety of visitors. 4

In recent years, security measures at public events across Germany have been heightened in response to a number of violent attacks. 2 These include the use of concrete barriers to prevent vehicle-ramming attacks, an increased police presence, and enhanced surveillance. 2 However, the incident at Oktoberfest highlights the challenge of protecting against threats that originate from individuals with no known links to terrorist organisations. The so-called "lone wolf" attacker, motivated by personal grievances or psychological issues, poses a different and often more unpredictable threat than organised terrorist groups.

Firearms Legislation in Germany

The fact that the perpetrator of the Munich attack used a self-made weapon raises questions about firearms legislation in Germany. The country has some of the strictest gun laws in Europe. 3 The German Weapons Act (Waffengesetz) regulates the acquisition, possession, and carrying of firearms. 3 To legally own a gun, a German citizen must obtain a firearms ownership license (Waffenbesitzkarte), for which they must meet a number of strict criteria. 6 These include being at least 18 years of age, passing a background check, demonstrating a credible need for the weapon (such as for hunting or sport shooting), and proving their expertise in handling firearms. 2, 6

The use of firearms for self-defence is highly restricted. 3 The law makes a clear distinction between owning a firearm and carrying one in public. A separate and even more difficult to obtain license is required to carry a loaded weapon. 2 The illegal possession of firearms is a serious offence, punishable by imprisonment. 4

Despite these strict regulations, the Munich attacker was able to construct and use a deadly weapon. This highlights the challenge of preventing individuals who are determined to cause harm from obtaining or manufacturing weapons. The incident will likely lead to a review of existing laws and a discussion about what more can be done to prevent such tragedies in the future.

Conclusion

The temporary closure of the 2025 Munich Oktoberfest was a stark reminder of the vulnerability of even the most well-secured public events to acts of violence. The incident, which stemmed from a tragic domestic dispute, demonstrated the devastating consequences of one individual's psychological distress. The swift and professional response of the German authorities undoubtedly prevented a greater tragedy and allowed the festival to resume with minimal disruption. However, the event raises important questions about public safety, mental health, and the effectiveness of existing security measures and firearms legislation. As Germany and other nations continue to grapple with the threat of lone-wolf attacks, the lessons learned from the 2025 Oktoberfest incident will be invaluable in shaping future strategies for protecting public spaces and preventing similar tragedies.
