The naming convention serves as a simple yet effective tool for conveying the seriousness of an approaching weather event to the public and emergency services alike.

The formation of extratropical cyclones, such as Storm Amy, is a natural phenomenon driven by temperature gradients and atmospheric instabilities. The path and intensity of these storms are influenced by large-scale atmospheric circulation patterns, including the position and strength of the jet stream. Variations in these patterns, often associated with phenomena like the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO), can lead to periods of increased storminess or relative calm in the region.

In recent decades, scientific research has increasingly focused on the potential influence of anthropogenic climate change on extreme weather events. While attributing any single storm event directly and solely to climate change is complex and requires extensive attribution studies, scientific consensus suggests that climate change may be altering the characteristics of storms in various ways. For extratropical cyclones in the North Atlantic, projections from climate models, as synthesised by bodies such as the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), suggest potential shifts in storm tracks and a possible increase in the intensity of the strongest storms. Warmer sea surface temperatures in the Atlantic could provide more energy for storm development, potentially leading to higher wind speeds and greater precipitation. Furthermore, a warmer atmosphere holds more moisture, which can contribute to heavier rainfall events when storms occur.

The observed impacts of Storm Amy, such as extreme wind speeds and heavy rainfall, are consistent with the types of hazards that could be exacerbated by a changing climate.
