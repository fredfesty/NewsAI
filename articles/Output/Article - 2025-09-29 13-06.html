Navigating the Polycrisis

Rachel Reeves, the New Fiscal Reality, and the Remaking of British Economic Policy

Article created and last updated on: Monday 29 September 2025 12:57

Abstract

This article provides a comprehensive analysis of the keynote address delivered by the Chancellor of the Exchequer, Rachel Reeves, at the Labour Party conference in Liverpool on 29 September 2025. The speech is identified as a pivotal moment in the trajectory of the Labour government, elected in a landslide victory in July 2024. It marked a significant departure from the party's pre-election fiscal promises, most notably the pledge not to increase the headline rates of income tax, national insurance, or Value Added Tax. The Chancellor’s declaration that "the world has changed" served as the intellectual cornerstone for this policy pivot, with the rationale attributed to a confluence of external pressures including persistent global conflicts, the imposition of protectionist tariffs by the United States, and a sustained environment of elevated borrowing costs. This article deconstructs the speech, examining its three core components: the articulation of a new, more stringent fiscal reality necessitating "hard choices"; the political repositioning of the government against the perceived primary electoral threat from the Reform UK party; and the call for a methodological review of the forecasting models employed by the Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR). The analysis is situated within the broader economic and political context of the United Kingdom in 2025, including a sluggish domestic economy, challenging inflationary pressures, and the profound political shadow cast by the market turmoil that followed the Truss administration's 2022 mini-budget. Furthermore, the article explores the intra-party dynamics revealed by the Chancellor's address, particularly the implicit critique of policy proposals associated with the Mayor of Greater Manchester, Andy Burnham, which underscores a tension between centralised fiscal discipline and demands for greater public expenditure. The implications of this policy shift are examined in detail, considering the potential trajectory of UK tax policy, the reaction of financial markets, and the fundamental reshaping of the Labour Party's economic identity in government. The article concludes that the speech represents a critical juncture where the rhetoric of the 2024 election campaign confronted the unyielding constraints of governance, setting the stage for a period of fiscal consolidation and defining the economic and political landscape for the remainder of the parliamentary term.

Introduction

The annual party conference season in the United Kingdom has long served as a critical forum for political deliberation, policy pronouncements, and the strategic positioning of the main political actors. On 29 September 2025, the Labour Party conference in Liverpool became the stage for what may be retrospectively viewed as one of the most consequential speeches by a modern Chancellor of the Exchequer. In her keynote address, Rachel Reeves articulated a stark and unvarnished assessment of the United Kingdom's economic position, signalling a definitive end to the fiscal framework that had underpinned Labour's successful 2024 general election campaign. The central thesis of her speech—that a changed world demanded a change in approach—served as a prelude to the explicit warning of "hard choices" and the abandonment of a core pledge not to raise major taxes. This moment represented not merely a policy adjustment, but a fundamental recalibration of the government's relationship with the electorate, the financial markets, and its own political promises.

The purpose of this article is to conduct a thorough and multi-faceted analysis of Chancellor Reeves's speech, its antecedents, and its far-reaching implications. It seeks to move beyond the immediate headlines to provide a scholarly examination of the complex interplay of economic constraints, political strategy, and institutional pressures that culminated in this significant policy pivot. The analysis will argue that the speech cannot be understood in isolation, but rather as the logical outcome of a series of developments stretching back to the economic inheritance bequeathed to the Labour government, the enduring legacy of the 2022 mini-budget, and the rapidly evolving political landscape of post-2024 Britain.

To achieve this, the article will first establish the necessary context by tracing the path from Labour's electoral triumph to the challenging realities of its first fifteen months in office. This will involve an examination of the party's 2024 manifesto commitments, the state of the UK economy upon their assumption of power, and the self-imposed fiscal rules designed to restore economic credibility. Subsequently, the core of the article will provide a granular deconstruction of the Chancellor's address, dissecting its key arguments concerning the new fiscal reality, the political threat posed by Reform UK, and the proposed reforms to the Office for Budget Responsibility. The internal dynamics of the Labour Party will also be scrutinised, with a particular focus on the subtle but significant tensions between the Treasury and other powerful figures within the party, such as Andy Burnham. Finally, the article will synthesise these elements to explore the profound economic and political consequences of this watershed moment, considering the future of taxation, the government's ongoing quest for market credibility, and the very definition of the Labour Party's economic philosophy in the mid-2020s. Through this comprehensive approach, the article aims to provide a definitive academic account of a speech that set the defining economic and political narrative for the Starmer government.

Background: The Road to September 2025

The 2024 General Election and Labour's Ascent to Power

The political context for Chancellor Reeves's 2025 conference speech was forged in the dramatic electoral upheaval of the previous year. The general election held on 4 July 2024 resulted in a landslide victory for the Labour Party, led by Keir Starmer, bringing an end to fourteen years of Conservative-led government.1432 The scale of the victory was historic; Labour secured 411 seats in the House of Commons, a net gain of 209, affording the new Prime Minister a substantial working majority of 174.914 Conversely, the Conservative Party suffered its worst defeat in modern history, collapsing to just 121 seats.1445 The election also saw the Liberal Democrats re-establish themselves as the third party in Parliament with 72 seats, while in Scotland, the Scottish National Party's dominance was severely curtailed as their representation fell from 48 to just nine seats.914

A crucial outcome of the 2024 election was the parliamentary breakthrough of Reform UK. Led into the election by Nigel Farage, the party secured 14.3% of the national vote, a share greater than that of the Liberal Democrats, and won five seats, including Farage's own in Clacton.141723 This result marked an unprecedented achievement for a party of its populist, right-wing orientation and established it as a significant disruptive force in British politics.1739

The Labour Party's campaign was predicated on a message of change, stability, and fiscal responsibility, designed to draw a sharp contrast with the perceived chaos of the preceding Conservative administrations. The party's 2024 manifesto, titled "Change", was central to this strategy.4 Economically, it was built around a promise to "restore economic stability with tough new spending rules".4 A key component of this was a widely publicised "tax lock for working people", which contained an explicit pledge not to raise the rates of the three largest revenue-generating taxes: income tax, national insurance, and Value Added Tax (VAT).41234 The manifesto also committed to capping the headline rate of corporation tax at its existing level of 25% for the duration of the parliament to provide certainty for businesses.434

These pledges were underpinned by a set of "non-negotiable" fiscal rules, which were carried into government.536 The two primary rules stipulated that, first, the current budget must be on a path to balance, ensuring day-to-day costs are met by revenues, and second, that national debt must be falling as a share of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) by the fifth year of the forecast period.2436 This framework was designed to project an image of prudence and to reassure financial markets that a Labour government would not engage in unfunded spending commitments. The manifesto also contained a promise to strengthen the role of the Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR), ensuring every significant fiscal event would be subject to its independent forecast, a direct response to the OBR having been sidelined during the 2022 mini-budget.12 This pre-election platform, therefore, was one of cautious ambition, prioritising stability and credibility above radical fiscal expansion, and it was this platform upon which Rachel Reeves entered Number 11 Downing Street as Chancellor.

The Economic Inheritance: A Landscape of Constraints

Upon taking office in July 2024, the new Labour government was immediately confronted with a deeply challenging economic inheritance. The rhetoric from the new administration was that the outgoing Conservatives had left a significant fiscal "black hole", a claim used to manage public expectations and prepare the ground for difficult decisions.36 The objective economic data painted a picture of a high-debt, low-growth economy grappling with persistent inflationary pressures.

In her first budget, delivered on 30 October 2024, Chancellor Reeves began to address this inheritance.2036 Despite the manifesto's tax lock, the event included what was widely described as a "big overall tax rise", primarily achieved through measures that did not technically breach the letter of the pledge, such as an increase in employers' National Insurance contributions.213 This initial fiscal event left the Chancellor with very little room for manoeuvre against her own "iron clad" fiscal rules.2 The new rules were formally adopted, with the target for balancing the current budget set for the 2029/30 fiscal year.24

Throughout late 2024 and into 2025, the economic environment remained unforgiving. Economic growth proved to be sluggish.13 The Office for Budget Responsibility, in its March 2025 Economic and Fiscal Outlook, forecast GDP growth of just 1.0% for 2025.27 By the time of the Spring Statement on 26 March 2025, the OBR had downgraded its 2025 growth forecast from 2% (as of October 2024) to 1%, citing global uncertainty and a contraction in the economy in January 2025.41 Data from the second quarter of 2025 showed GDP growing by a modest 0.3% compared to the first quarter.27

Inflation, while having receded from the double-digit peaks of previous years, remained stubbornly above the Bank of England's 2% target. The Consumer Prices Index (CPI) stood at 2.6% in March 2025, rising to 3.6% in June and 3.8% in July.2737 The Bank of England's August 2025 Monetary Policy Report noted that CPI inflation had averaged 3.5% in the second quarter of the year, higher than previously anticipated.18 In response to these persistent pressures, the Bank had been cautious in easing monetary policy, with the Bank Rate standing at 4.0% in August 2025 after a series of gradual cuts.1827

Crucially for the Chancellor, the cost of government borrowing remained elevated, reaching levels not seen in decades and reportedly higher than any other G7 economy.2 This directly increased the cost of servicing the UK's substantial national debt, which at the end of July 2025 stood at 96.1% of GDP.27 Government borrowing in the first four months of the 2025/26 financial year was £6.7 billion higher than in the same period a year earlier.27 This combination of weak growth, persistent inflation, high debt, and expensive borrowing created a formidable set of constraints. By September 2025, reports were circulating that the OBR was likely to downgrade its growth forecasts once again, a move that would further shrink the Chancellor's already narrow fiscal headroom and increase the pressure to find additional revenue simply to remain compliant with her own fiscal rules.2

The "Trussonomics" Legacy and the Pursuit of Stability

It is impossible to comprehend the political and economic philosophy of the Starmer-Reeves administration without understanding the profound and lasting impact of the "mini-budget" presented by Chancellor Kwasi Kwarteng on 23 September 2022, under the brief premiership of Liz Truss.6 This fiscal event, officially titled "The Growth Plan", represented the largest package of tax cuts in the UK in fifty years, including the abolition of the top rate of income tax and reversals of planned increases in corporation tax and National Insurance.622 These measures, amounting to an estimated £45 billion, were to be funded entirely by a massive increase in government borrowing.22 Critically, the government chose not to commission an independent forecast from the Office for Budget Responsibility to accompany the announcement.6

The reaction from financial markets was immediate and catastrophic. The value of the pound sterling plummeted, at one point reaching an all-time low against the US dollar.619 The cost of government borrowing, as measured by gilt yields, soared. The turmoil was so severe that the Bank of England was forced to launch an emergency intervention, buying billions of pounds of government debt to prevent a collapse in the pensions sector.19 The crisis precipitated a humiliating series of U-turns, the dismissal of Kwarteng, and ultimately the resignation of Liz Truss after just 49 days in office. The Resolution Foundation later calculated that the unfunded tax cuts and the subsequent rise in interest rates and borrowing costs had created a fiscal hole of approximately £30 billion.6

The episode, often referred to as the "Trussonomics" crisis, left a deep scar on the British body politic. It served as a brutal demonstration of the power of financial markets to discipline governments that deviate from perceived fiscal orthodoxy. For the then-opposition Labour Party, it was a formative political experience. The crisis powerfully reinforced the strategic imperative of demonstrating iron-clad fiscal credibility. The pursuit of stability became the central, non-negotiable tenet of their economic platform, shaping the cautious manifesto of 2024 and the stringent fiscal rules adopted upon entering government. The legacy of 2022 created a political environment in which any policy, particularly one involving taxation or borrowing, was measured against its potential reception by the bond markets.29 This fear of provoking a negative market reaction became a dominant, if often unspoken, constraint on the new government's actions, dictating every step and creating the intellectual backdrop against which Chancellor Reeves would have to frame her necessary, but politically perilous, pivot on tax policy in September 2025.29

Deconstructing the Chancellor's Address: A Triptych of Challenges

The Fiscal Pivot: "The World Has Changed"

The centrepiece of Chancellor Reeves's address was the carefully constructed abandonment of the 2024 manifesto's tax lock. This was not presented as a matter of choice, but of necessity, dictated by a fundamental shift in the global environment. The phrase "the world has changed" was the rhetorical anchor for this pivot, designed to frame the decision as a pragmatic response to external shocks rather than a breach of faith with the electorate. The Chancellor articulated a trio of interconnected global challenges that, she argued, had rendered the pre-election fiscal stance untenable.

First, the persistence of global conflicts was cited as a primary driver of economic instability. While the speech did not detail specific conflicts, the ongoing war in Ukraine and renewed hostilities in the Middle East following the breakdown of a ceasefire in March 2025 provided a clear and plausible context.15 Such conflicts invariably disrupt supply chains, fuel energy price volatility, and necessitate increased defence and security expenditure, placing direct and indirect burdens on the national finances. This narrative positioned the UK as being subject to geopolitical forces beyond its immediate control, which carried unavoidable economic consequences.

Second, the Chancellor pointed to the rise of protectionism, with a specific reference to US tariffs. This alluded to a potential shift in American trade policy following the 2024 US presidential election, moving towards a more insular and less free-trading stance. For an open, trading economy like the United Kingdom, the imposition of tariffs by its largest single trading partner would represent a significant headwind, dampening export growth and negatively impacting the overall economic outlook. This argument served to underscore the vulnerability of the UK economy to the policy decisions of its allies and the fragility of the post-war consensus on global trade.

Third, and most immediately impactful for the Treasury, was the sustained environment of higher borrowing costs. As previously established, UK government gilt yields had risen to near three-decade highs by the latter half of 2025, significantly increasing the cost of servicing the national debt and issuing new debt to cover the budget deficit.2 This was not a uniquely British problem, but part of a global trend of monetary tightening to combat inflation. However, the Chancellor's emphasis on this point served as a direct link between global financial conditions and the constraints on domestic policy. The higher interest bill on the national debt directly reduced the amount of money available for public services or investment, creating a stark trade-off that the government could no longer ignore.

By weaving these three factors together, Reeves constructed a compelling narrative of a "polycrisis" in which geopolitical, trade, and financial pressures were converging to create a fiscal reality far harsher than that anticipated in 2024. This narrative was essential to justify the politically damaging decision to signal tax rises. The speech hinted that the forthcoming November 2025 budget would contain specific measures, while also suggesting that VAT would not be one of the taxes to be increased, thereby honouring at least one part of the original tax lock.13 The pivot was thus framed not as a failure of planning, but as a responsible and necessary adaptation to a world that had become more dangerous and economically volatile.

The Political Realignment: Targeting Reform UK

Beyond the fiscal message, the speech represented a significant strategic realignment in the Labour government's political posture. Chancellor Reeves explicitly identified Nigel Farage and the Reform UK party as "the single greatest threat" to the livelihoods of people in the United Kingdom. This declaration was a formal recognition of a political reality that had been solidifying for over a year. Following the Conservatives' collapse in 2024, Reform UK had successfully positioned itself as the primary vehicle for opposition to the Labour government, capitalising on widespread public discontent.17 By September 2025, opinion polls consistently showed Reform UK with a substantial lead over Labour, with one Ipsos poll giving them a 12-point advantage (34% to 22%).8 Some MRP polling models even suggested Reform was within touching distance of being the largest party in a hypothetical general election.25 The party's support was drawn heavily from former Conservative voters but was also making inroads with a segment of the 2024 Labour electorate.839

Reeves's strategy was to frame the political battle not as a traditional left-versus-right contest, but as a choice between economic stability and economic recklessness. She did this by directly linking Reform UK to the legacy of the 2022 mini-budget, highlighting the party's support for the tax-cutting agenda of Liz Truss. This was a potent line of attack, designed to paint Reform UK not as a credible party of government, but as proponents of a dangerous economic ideology that had already been tried and had failed catastrophically. By invoking the memory of the market crash, soaring mortgage rates, and the threat to pensions that followed the 2022 event, the Chancellor aimed to define her opponents as a risk that the country could not afford to take.

This framing allowed Labour to position itself as the party of fiscal orthodoxy and economic security, the true inheritor of the mantle of stability that the Conservative Party had abdicated. It was a direct appeal to the "silent majority" of voters concerned with economic competence and predictability. In this narrative, Labour's "hard choices" and potential tax rises were presented as the actions of a responsible government cleaning up a fiscal mess and safeguarding the economy from future shocks, while Reform's policies were caricatured as a return to the failed "fantasy economics" that had caused the 2022 crisis. This political repositioning was a crucial element of the speech, attempting to turn a moment of fiscal weakness—the admission that taxes must rise—into a moment of political strength by defining the terms of the new political dividing line.

The Institutional Reform: Questioning the OBR

The third key component of the Chancellor's address was a call for changes to the way the Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) forecasts the performance of the economy. This was a nuanced but highly significant intervention, reflecting a growing debate within economic policy circles about the role and methodology of the UK's independent fiscal watchdog. The OBR was established in 2010 to provide independent and authoritative analysis of the UK’s public finances. However, its forecasting record and its influence over policy have been subject to increasing scrutiny.11

The Chancellor's critique was not a frontal assault on the OBR's independence, which the Labour manifesto had pledged to uphold.12 Instead, it was a more subtle questioning of its analytical framework. The context for this was a widespread frustration that the OBR's models were perceived as being too static, potentially underestimating the positive growth effects (or "dynamic" benefits) of certain government policies, particularly those related to investment and supply-side reform. This debate had been ongoing for some time, with an external review of the OBR in late 2024 calling for clearer criteria on its use of dynamic scoring.1131

The timing of Reeves's comments was critical. With the OBR reportedly poised to downgrade its growth forecasts for the UK economy—a move that would tighten the fiscal straitjacket on the government—the Chancellor's intervention can be seen as a pre-emptive attempt to manage the narrative.2 By questioning the OBR's methodology, she was subtly suggesting that the official forecast might be overly pessimistic and might not fully capture the potential of the government's own growth agenda. This could provide a degree of political cover for a difficult set of fiscal numbers in the upcoming November budget.

Furthermore, the call for reform resonated with earlier criticisms, including from the head of the OBR, Richard Hughes, who had previously lamented that government failure to provide detailed spending plans had forced the OBR to produce forecasts based on "questionable assumptions," which he suggested were akin to a "work of fiction".21 While Reeves's critique came from a different angle, it tapped into a broader sense that the forecasting process was imperfect and perhaps not fit for the challenges of a post-pandemic, high-inflation economy. The Chancellor's proposal, therefore, was not merely a technical suggestion but a political act. It was an attempt to regain a degree of policy autonomy from what some see as an overly powerful "fiscal technocracy" and to create more space for the government's own economic strategy, even as she was forced to bow to the immediate fiscal realities.16

Intra-Party Dynamics and the Burnham Subtext

While much of the Chancellor's speech was focused on external economic pressures and the political opposition, it also contained a significant, albeit veiled, message directed at internal party dynamics. The address included passages emphasising the paramount importance of fiscal discipline and the rejection of unfunded spending commitments, which were widely interpreted as a thinly veiled criticism of the policy agenda being advanced by Andy Burnham, the influential and popular Mayor of Greater Manchester.7

Since the 2024 election, and with increasing intensity in the weeks leading up to the 2025 conference, Burnham had carved out a role as the most prominent internal critic of the government's direction.40 He had spoken publicly about a "climate of fear" within the Labour Party that stifled open debate and had called for a more ambitious policy agenda to address the cost of living crisis and reconnect with voters who were becoming disillusioned with the government.2330 His interventions were seen by many as positioning for a future leadership challenge, offering a more radical, devolutionist alternative to the perceived centralism and caution of the Starmer-Reeves axis.747 Burnham's agenda included calls for course corrections on sensitive issues such as lifting the two-child benefit cap and a broader belief in using public control over essential services like housing, energy, and rail to improve living standards.740

In this context, Reeves's stern emphasis on fiscal rectitude and the necessity of making "hard choices" served as a direct rebuttal to the Burnhamite wing of the party. Her message was clear: in the current economic climate, ambitious spending plans that were not fully costed were a luxury the country—and the government—could not afford. By implicitly linking such proposals to the kind of fiscal laxity that the government was defining itself against, she was reinforcing the Treasury's authority as the ultimate arbiter of policy feasibility. The speech acted as a reassertion of central control, a warning that the path to electoral credibility and economic stability required unity around a disciplined fiscal framework, not a proliferation of uncosted regional or ideological projects. This subtext revealed the underlying tensions within a party of government, balancing the pragmatic constraints of the Treasury against the political pressures for a more expansive and interventionist state to deliver on the promise of "change".

The Broader Context: Protests and Public Sentiment

The formal proceedings of the Chancellor's speech were momentarily punctuated by an interruption from a pro-Palestine heckler. While brief, the incident served as a potent reminder of the complex and often volatile landscape of public sentiment and the array of non-economic pressures facing the government. The protest did not occur in a vacuum but was rooted in the government's evolving foreign policy and the sustained public activism surrounding the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.

In the weeks prior to the conference, in September 2025, the Labour government had fulfilled a long-standing manifesto promise by formally recognising the state of Palestine.2846 This historic foreign policy shift was confirmed by Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper in her own conference speech, where she described statehood as the "inalienable right of the Palestinian people".38 This decision followed a period of intense international diplomacy and growing pressure from within the Labour Party and the wider public, as the humanitarian crisis in Gaza continued following the resumption of conflict in March 2025.1546

However, for many activists, recognition was seen as a necessary but insufficient step. Pro-Palestinian campaign groups continued to call for more concrete actions, such as the cessation of UK arms exports to Israel and the consideration of trade sanctions.328 The heckler's interruption, therefore, reflected this ongoing sentiment that the government, despite its formal recognition of Palestine, was not doing enough to address the immediate conflict and humanitarian suffering. The incident highlighted the fact that, even as the Chancellor sought to focus the nation's attention on domestic fiscal challenges, the government's agenda was also being shaped by deeply felt global issues. It underscored the difficult balancing act for the Starmer administration, navigating the demands of its domestic economic programme while also managing the passionate and often conflicting expectations of the public on matters of international justice and foreign policy.

Economic and Political Implications

The Future of UK Taxation

The Chancellor's definitive pivot away from the 2024 tax lock opens a critical new chapter in UK fiscal policy. By preparing the ground for tax rises in the November 2025 budget, the speech has inevitably focused attention on which specific levies are likely to be targeted. The explicit suggestion that VAT would be spared indicates a desire to maintain at least a semblance of the original pledge and to avoid a tax that is perceived as regressive, disproportionately affecting those on lower incomes.35 This leaves a range of other major taxes as potential candidates for increases.

While the manifesto also pledged not to raise the headline rates of income tax and National Insurance for "working people", the Chancellor's new framing of a changed world may be used to justify a revision of this commitment.1335 However, a more politically palatable route may be to continue the policy of "fiscal drag" by keeping personal tax allowances and thresholds frozen.3541 As wages rise with inflation, this policy automatically pulls more people into the tax system and pushes others into higher tax brackets, raising significant revenue without altering the headline rates.

Other areas are likely to come under intense scrutiny. The taxation of wealth, as opposed to income, presents a range of options. Reforms to Capital Gains Tax (CGT), potentially aligning its rates more closely with those of income tax, have long been debated as a means of addressing perceived unfairness in the system.2 Similarly, changes to Inheritance Tax (IHT) could be considered.13 The Labour manifesto had already committed to closing the loophole on the taxation of carried interest for private equity managers and abolishing the "non-dom" tax status, but the revenue from these measures is insufficient to fill the larger fiscal gap.12 Therefore, more substantial reforms, possibly including changes to property taxes such as council tax or stamp duty, may now be on the table.13 The Chancellor's challenge will be to design a package of tax increases that raises the necessary revenue to satisfy her fiscal rules and reassure markets, while also being perceived as fair and politically defensible against the inevitable attacks from the opposition.

Market Reactions and Credibility

A central objective of the Chancellor's speech was to manage the reaction of financial markets to the news of a significant policy shift. The entire economic strategy of the Labour government has been conditioned by the memory of the 2022 mini-budget, where unfunded fiscal promises triggered a collapse in market confidence.29 Reeves's approach in September 2025 can be seen as the antithesis of Kwasi Kwarteng's in 2022. Where Kwarteng's announcements were a surprise "shock and awe" package, Reeves's speech was a carefully choreographed exercise in expectation management.

By signalling the necessity of tax rises well in advance of the November budget, the Chancellor aimed to pre-empt any negative market shock. The strategy is one of transparency and predictability: to explain the rationale for the difficult decisions before announcing the specific measures. The narrative of a changed global environment and the unwavering commitment to the government's own fiscal rules are intended to frame the tax rises not as a sign of fiscal profligacy, but as a mark of fiscal responsibility. The intended message to the bond markets is that this is a government that understands the constraints it faces and is willing to take unpopular decisions to ensure the public finances remain on a sustainable path.

The success of this strategy will be judged by the movement in UK gilt yields and the value of sterling in the aftermath of the speech and in the run-up to the budget. The government's hope is that, by contrasting its measured and transparent approach with the chaotic unilateralism of 2022, the markets will react with understanding, if not enthusiasm. A calm market reaction would be seen as a vindication of the Chancellor's strategy and would cement her reputation for credibility. Conversely, any significant spike in borrowing costs would indicate that the markets remain nervous about the UK's fiscal position and could severely constrain the government's policy options moving forward.

The Remaking of the Labour Party's Economic Identity

The speech at the 2025 conference marks a crucial moment in the evolution of the Labour Party's economic identity under Keir Starmer and Rachel Reeves. The pre-election phase was dominated by what could be termed "vow of silence" economics, where the primary goal was to neutralise Conservative attacks by making binding commitments to fiscal restraint and ruling out major tax rises. This was a strategy born of political necessity, designed to detoxify the Labour brand after the 2019 election and to win the trust of a sceptical electorate and watchful financial markets. The "iron-clad" fiscal rules and the tax lock were the key symbols of this approach.

Reeves's speech signifies the transition from this campaign-oriented identity to a governing one. It represents the point at which the rigid promises required to win power collide with the complex and fluid realities of managing a G7 economy in a turbulent world. The new economic identity emerging from this collision is one of pragmatic, and perhaps reluctant, fiscal consolidation. It is an identity that remains committed to the language of stability and rules-based policy, but which now acknowledges that stability requires higher taxes, not just controlled spending.

This shift redefines "Reevesian" economics. If the initial phase was about demonstrating that Labour could be trusted with the public finances, the second phase is about defining what the party will do with that trust when faced with hard choices. It is a move away from the reassuring simplicities of the manifesto towards a more complex and potentially less popular narrative about shared sacrifice and the limits of the state in a low-growth, high-debt environment. How the party manages this transition, and whether it can carry the public with it, will be the defining economic and political challenge of its first term in office.

The Electoral Calculus

The decision to abandon a core manifesto pledge on taxation carries significant electoral risks for the Labour government. It provides a powerful and simple line of attack for opposition parties, who will accuse the government of breaking its promises and misleading the electorate. Reform UK, already identified by the Chancellor as the main threat, is certain to seize upon the issue, framing the tax rises as a betrayal of "working people" and a consequence of Labour's economic mismanagement. This narrative could resonate powerfully with the voters Labour needs to retain, particularly those in former "Red Wall" constituencies who lent their support to the party in 2024.

However, the government's strategy is based on a calculated risk. The hope is that by framing the decision as a necessary response to a deteriorating global situation, and by demonstrating a commitment to fiscal responsibility, voters will see the government as pragmatic and competent, even if they dislike the specific policies. The memory of the 2022 mini-budget is a key asset in this regard; the government can argue that while tax rises are painful, the alternative—the fiscal chaos championed by their opponents—is far worse.

The success of this electoral calculus will depend on several factors. First, the state of the economy itself: if voters begin to feel an improvement in their living standards, they may be more forgiving of the tax increases. Second, the perceived fairness of the tax changes: the government will need to ensure that the burden is seen to be distributed equitably. Third, the effectiveness of the government's communication strategy in persuading the public that there was no viable alternative. The speech in Liverpool was the opening move in this high-stakes political battle. It has set the terms of the debate for the coming years and has placed a wager on the idea that, in an age of insecurity, voters will ultimately reward perceived competence and responsibility over populist promises.

Conclusion

The address delivered by Chancellor Rachel Reeves at the 2025 Labour Party conference was far more than a routine political speech. It was a moment of profound political and economic significance, marking a definitive turning point for the Labour government and for the United Kingdom's fiscal trajectory. In abandoning the central tax pledge of the 2024 election manifesto, the Chancellor initiated a necessary but perilous transition from the reassuring rhetoric of an election campaign to the unyielding realities of governing in an era of global polycrisis. The speech represented a direct confrontation with a landscape of sluggish growth, persistent inflation, and the enduring shadow of past fiscal misadventures.

The analysis presented in this article has demonstrated that the Chancellor's policy pivot was the culmination of a confluence of pressures. The challenging economic inheritance, characterised by high debt and low growth, provided little room for manoeuvre from the outset. The legacy of the 2022 "Trussonomics" crisis created an overarching imperative for fiscal credibility, making adherence to strict fiscal rules a non-negotiable aspect of the government's strategy. When this domestic fragility was met with the external shocks of global conflict, rising protectionism, and elevated borrowing costs, the pre-election fiscal framework became untenable. The Chancellor's speech was an acknowledgement of this reality.

Simultaneously, the address was a masterclass in political strategy. It sought to turn a moment of weakness into one of strength by redefining the central dividing line in British politics as a contest between Labour's perceived responsible stewardship and the "fantasy economics" attributed to its primary challenger, Reform UK. The nuanced critique of the Office for Budget Responsibility and the implicit rebuttal of internal party critics further served to consolidate the Treasury's authority and frame the government's difficult choices as the only responsible path forward.
