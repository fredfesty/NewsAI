Background

The conflict between Israelis and Palestinians is one of the most enduring and complex geopolitical challenges of the modern era, rooted in competing national aspirations, historical narratives, and territorial claims. Understanding the developments of October 2025 necessitates a comprehensive review of the historical trajectory and salient features of this protracted struggle, particularly as it pertains to the Gaza Strip.

Historical Roots of the Conflict

The origins of the modern Israeli-Palestinian conflict can be traced back to the late 19th and early 20th centuries, with the emergence of Zionist and Arab nationalist movements. Zionist aspirations sought to establish a Jewish homeland in Palestine, then a part of the Ottoman Empire, while Arab nationalism asserted the rights of indigenous Arab populations. British administration of Palestine under a League of Nations Mandate following World War I, from 1920 to 1948, exacerbated tensions as both communities intensified their claims and sought international recognition 23.

Mass Jewish immigration to Palestine, particularly during the interwar period and in the wake of the Holocaust, led to increased demographic shifts and land disputes. The United Nations Special Committee on Palestine (UNSCOP) proposed a partition plan in 1947, envisioning separate Arab and Jewish states with Jerusalem under international administration. This plan was accepted by the Jewish leadership but rejected by Arab states and Palestinian representatives, who viewed it as a dispossession of their ancestral lands 24.

The termination of the British Mandate in May 1948 and the subsequent declaration of the State of Israel immediately precipitated the 1948 Arab-Israeli War. This conflict resulted in Israel establishing control over a significant portion of mandated Palestine, while Jordan annexed the West Bank and East Jerusalem, and Egypt took administrative control of the Gaza Strip. Hundreds of thousands of Palestinians became refugees, an event they refer to as the 'Nakba' (catastrophe), profoundly shaping Palestinian identity and national aspirations thereafter 25.

Evolution of Israeli-Palestinian Relations

Subsequent decades were marked by further wars and periods of intense conflict. The 1956 Suez Crisis, the 1967 Six-Day War, and the 1973 Yom Kippur War reshaped the regional map. In the Six-Day War, Israel occupied the West Bank, East Jerusalem, the Gaza Strip, the Golan Heights, and the Sinai Peninsula 26. This occupation placed a large Palestinian population under Israeli military rule, leading to the establishment of Israeli settlements in the occupied territories, which remain a major point of contention and are considered illegal under international law by most of the international community 27.

The Palestinian Liberation Organisation (PLO), founded in 1964, emerged as the primary representative of the Palestinian people, initially advocating for the liberation of all of Palestine through armed struggle. Over time, particularly after the First Intifada (Palestinian uprising) from 1987 to 1993, the PLO, under Yasser Arafat, began to engage in diplomatic efforts, culminating in the Oslo Accords in the mid-1990s 28. These accords established the Palestinian Authority (PA) with limited self-governance in parts of the West Bank and Gaza Strip, and laid the groundwork for a potential two-state solution, although a permanent peace agreement remained elusive 29.

The Gaza Strip: From Occupation to Blockade

The Gaza Strip, a small, densely populated coastal enclave, has a unique and particularly tragic history within the broader conflict. Administered by Egypt from 1948 to 1967, it came under Israeli occupation in the Six-Day War. For decades, Gaza was characterised by refugee camps, poverty, and sporadic resistance against Israeli rule 30.

A pivotal moment occurred in 2005 when Israel unilaterally disengaged from Gaza, withdrawing its troops and dismantling its settlements. This move was framed by Israel as an effort to improve its security and disengage from direct control over the Palestinian population 31. However, after the Israeli withdrawal, the political situation in Gaza rapidly deteriorated. In 2006, Hamas, an Islamist militant and political organisation, won the Palestinian legislative elections, defeating the Fatah party, which dominated the PA in the West Bank 32. A year later, in 2007, Hamas seized complete control of the Gaza Strip in a violent internecine conflict with Fatah, effectively creating two separate Palestinian political entities – Hamas controlling Gaza and the Fatah-led PA governing parts of the West Bank 33.

In response to Hamas's takeover, Israel, with Egyptian cooperation, imposed a comprehensive blockade on the Gaza Strip, severely restricting the movement of goods and people in and out of the territory 34. This blockade, which remains largely in effect as of October 2025, has had a devastating impact on Gaza's economy and humanitarian situation, leading to widespread poverty, high unemployment, and a collapsing infrastructure. Critics argue that the blockade amounts to collective punishment of Gaza's two million residents, while Israel asserts it is a necessary security measure to prevent Hamas from importing weapons and materials for military purposes 35. The humanitarian conditions have been consistently decried by international organisations, with warnings of impending famine and a dire lack of essential services and medical supplies 4, 10, 19.

Major Escalations and Operations Leading to October 2025

The period following Hamas's takeover of Gaza and the imposition of the blockade has been punctuated by multiple large-scale military confrontations between Israel and Hamas, often involving other Palestinian militant groups such as Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ). These conflicts have resulted in thousands of casualties, predominantly Palestinian, and extensive destruction within the Gaza Strip.

Key operations include:

  • Operation Cast Lead (2008-2009): A major Israeli air and ground offensive launched in response to rocket fire from Gaza.
  • Operation Pillar of Defence (2012): Primarily an aerial campaign targeting Hamas military infrastructure.
  • Operation Protective Edge (2014): A prolonged conflict involving significant ground incursions and widespread destruction in Gaza.
  • Cycles of violence in 2018, 2021, and 2022: Characterised by rocket exchanges and Israeli airstrikes.

The conflict dramatically escalated on 7 October 2023, when Hamas launched a surprise, large-scale attack on southern Israel, breaching the border, firing thousands of rockets, and infiltrating Israeli communities. This assault resulted in the killing of more than 1,300 Israelis and the taking of hundreds of hostages, an event that profoundly shocked Israel and the international community 11, 19. In response, Israel declared war on Hamas and launched a massive military campaign, including aerial bombardments and ground operations across the Gaza Strip, with stated objectives of dismantling Hamas's military and governance capabilities and securing the release of all hostages 19.

This ensuing "Gaza war," as it came to be known, continued for nearly two years. By October 2025, the humanitarian crisis in Gaza had reached catastrophic levels, with over 67,000 Palestinians killed and 169,165 wounded since October 2023 9, 11, 13. Thousands more were believed to be buried under rubble 11. Amnesty International reported "catastrophic mass displacement" and the "obliteration" of Gaza City due to intensified Israeli operations since mid-August 2025, deploying tactics to sow panic and forcibly displace residents into unsafe, ill-equipped areas 10. Israel's military offensive to seize control of Gaza City, referred to as "Operation Gideon's Chariots II" or "B," formally began its first stages on 20 August 2025, with an expanded main offensive commencing on 15 September 2025 5. By 30 September, approximately 1,250 buildings in Gaza City were reported destroyed 5.

International Diplomacy and Prior Peace Proposals

Throughout the decades of conflict, numerous international efforts have been made to broker peace, establish ceasefires, and outline pathways for a lasting resolution. These have ranged from bilateral negotiations to multilateral initiatives involving the United States, the United Nations, the European Union, Egypt, and Qatar. Key diplomatic milestones and proposals include:

  • Madrid Conference (1991): Initiated multilateral negotiations following the Gulf War.
  • Oslo Accords (1990s): Led to the creation of the Palestinian Authority.
  • Camp David Summit (2000) and Taba Summit (2001): Unsuccessful attempts to reach a final status agreement.
  • Roadmap for Peace (2003): Proposed by the Quartet (US, UN, EU, Russia), envisioning a two-state solution.
  • Annapolis Conference (2007): Resumed peace talks between Israeli and Palestinian leadership.
  • Various ceasefire efforts: Often mediated by Egypt and Qatar after major escalations. For instance, in January 2025, a ceasefire mediated by the US, Egypt, and Qatar was announced to end fifteen months of war, but Israel renewed its offensive in March 2025 19.

These efforts have consistently faced formidable obstacles, including deep distrust between the parties, internal political divisions within both Israeli and Palestinian camps, the expansion of Israeli settlements, the continued blockade of Gaza, and unresolved core issues such as borders, the status of Jerusalem, and the right of return for Palestinian refugees. The failure of successive peace initiatives has contributed to a sense of disillusionment and a perception that a comprehensive resolution remains distant. This historical context of recurrent conflict and stalled diplomacy forms the essential backdrop against which the Trump peace initiative of October 2025 must be understood.

The Current Context: October 2025 Developments

The early days of October 2025 marked a pivotal, yet contentious, phase in the ongoing conflict in the Gaza Strip. A combination of external pressure from the United States and the ongoing humanitarian catastrophe on the ground seemingly compelled a strategic shift in Israel's military posture, coupled with a renewed push for a comprehensive peace plan.

The Israeli Military Campaign in Gaza City

Prior to 4 October 2025, Israeli military operations in the Gaza Strip, particularly in Gaza City, had been intense and sustained. The campaign, which Israel referred to as "Operation Gideon's Chariots II" or "B", commenced its preliminary stages on 20 August 2025, with an explicit aim to seize control of Gaza City from Hamas 5. This operation intensified into a main offensive starting on 15 September 2025, following a plan approved by Israel's security cabinet on 8 August 2025 5. The scope of the offensive was significant, with Israel calling up 60,000 reservists for the operation 5. Ahead of the offensive, Israel announced plans to relocate Palestinian civilians in Gaza City to the southern Gaza Strip, setting a deadline for evacuation by 7 October 2025, and escalated bombardments on Gaza City neighbourhoods such as Zeitoun, Sabra, Rimal, and Tuffah 5.

The consequences of this military campaign were severe and widely reported. By 30 September 2025, approximately 1,250 buildings were reported to have been destroyed in Gaza City, with several residential areas levelled by Israeli bombings 5. Amnesty International reported on 3 October 2025, that the intensification of Israel's military offensive in Gaza City since mid-August had unleashed a "catastrophic wave of mass forced displacement," forcing hundreds of thousands of Palestinians, many already displaced multiple times, into overcrowded enclaves in southern Gaza lacking basic necessities 10. Amnesty highlighted tactics such as unlawful mass displacement orders, the destruction of multi-story residential buildings sheltering families, and the use of explosive remote-controlled vehicles for demolitions, alongside intensified aerial bombardments of crowded areas 10. These actions, Amnesty asserted, were designed to sow panic and forcibly displace residents into unsafe areas 10. The human toll was staggering, with the Gaza Health Ministry reporting that the death toll in the nearly two-year Israel-Hamas war had surpassed 67,000 Palestinians by 4 October 2025, with over 169,000 wounded 9, 13.

The Order to Halt and Reduce Operations

On Saturday, 4 October 2025, a significant development emerged from Israeli media: the Israeli military had received orders from its political leadership to halt its offensive campaign in Gaza City 4, 6, 8, 12, 13, 16, 22. Israeli army radio, a primary source for these reports, stated that operations in Gaza would be reduced to "the minimum," with troops instructed to carry out only defensive strikes 4, 6, 8, 12, 13, 16, 22. This decision effectively blocked and halted the operation to conquer Gaza City, at least for the time being 12. The order to scale down operations and switch to solely defensive manoeuvres was reportedly issued after overnight talks between Israeli and American officials 6.

This strategic adjustment was directly linked to the broader diplomatic efforts surrounding US President Donald Trump's peace plan. The Israeli military, in a statement, confirmed it was directed to "advance readiness" for the first part of the Trump plan 4, 8, 22. Similarly, the Israeli Prime Minister's Office issued a statement indicating that "in light of Hamas's response, Israel is preparing to immediately implement the first phase of Trump's plan for the immediate release of all hostages," and pledged to "continue to work in full cooperation with the president and his team to end the war in accordance with the principles set forth by Israel that are consistent with President Trump's vision" 22.

Statements from the Israeli Military and Political Leadership

While the political leadership issued the order for a halt, the messaging from various Israeli entities indicated a complex, and at times contradictory, situation on the ground. Despite the directive to cease offensive operations, reports on Saturday, 4 October 2025, confirmed that Israel continued striking the Gaza Strip and besieging Gaza City 4, 13. Gaza's civil defence agency stated that Israel carried out "dozens of air strikes and artillery shelling on Gaza City and other areas in the Strip," despite President Trump's appeal to halt the bombing 9, 13. These strikes reportedly killed at least six Palestinian civilians, including two children, in Gaza City and a tent housing displaced people in al-Mawasi, with drones targeting a gathering near a bakery in central Gaza City, killing "dozens" 4, 8, 13, 21.

The Israeli military's Arabic-language spokesperson, Avichay Adraee, cautioned residents not to return to northern Gaza or Gaza City, explicitly stating that Israeli troops were still operating there and that returning would be "extremely dangerous" 4, 8, 9. This warning, issued on the very day of the reported halt order, underscored the ongoing presence and activity of Israeli forces in areas designated for a halt in offensive action. An Israeli official, speaking anonymously to the Associated Press, did state that Israel had moved to a defensive-only position and would not actively strike, though no forces had been removed from the strip 13. This seemingly contradictory information highlights the potential for a phased or partial implementation of the halt, or a disconnect between policy directives and immediate operational realities, perhaps justified by the military as defensive responses to perceived threats. Israel's Army Radio subsequently reported that the military was holding its positions along captured lines, not advancing or retreating, and justified recent airstrikes as aimed at removing threats to its forces and warning civilians against returning north 20.

On the political front, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's office reiterated Israel's commitment to ending the war in Gaza, aligning with Trump's vision 9, 22. Opposition politician Yair Lapid welcomed the Trump plan as a "genuine opportunity to release the hostages and end the war" and pledged political support to Netanyahu for further negotiations 8, 14. This suggests a degree of internal political consensus within Israel regarding the desirability of pursuing the Trump plan, at least in its initial phase, particularly concerning the release of hostages. However, some segments of the political spectrum, represented by figures like National Security Minister Itamar Ben Gvir and Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich, were anticipated to potentially "sabotage" a hostage deal, indicating internal resistance to certain aspects of the plan 21.

International Reactions to the Order

The international community's response to these developments was largely one of cautious optimism, particularly concerning the potential for an end to the nearly two-year conflict and the release of hostages. US President Donald Trump, having publicly welcomed Hamas's partial acceptance of his plan and demanding Israel cease its bombing campaign, expressed appreciation for Israel's "temporarily stopped the bombing" to facilitate the deal 7, 9, 13, 14, 17, 18, 21. He also issued a stern warning to Hamas to "move quickly, or else all bets will be off," asserting that he would not tolerate delay or any outcome where Gaza continues to pose a threat 21.

Leaders from across the globe voiced their support for the peace initiative. UN rights chief Volker Turk described the Trump plan as a "vital opportunity" to halt bloodshed and misery in the Palestinian territory "once and for all" 9. UN Secretary-General António Guterres was "encouraged" by Hamas's statement and urged all parties to seize this opportunity 18. Statements of support came from French President Emmanuel Macron, British Prime Minister Keir Starmer, and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz, all of whom highlighted the urgency of seizing this moment for a ceasefire, hostage release, and humanitarian aid 9, 11. South Africa also welcomed Hamas's response and urged reciprocal action from Israel regarding Palestinian prisoners 21.

Qatar and Egypt, key mediators in the conflict, confirmed their commitment to implementing the peace plan. A senior Hamas official indicated that Egypt was organising a conference for Palestinian factions to deliberate on the post-war future of the Gaza Strip, reflecting Hamas's stated intention to hand over administration to Palestinian technocrats 4, 9, 17, 18, 22. This broad international endorsement underscored the widespread desire to de-escalate the conflict and find a resolution to the dire humanitarian situation in Gaza.

The Proposed "Trump Plan" for Gaza

The "Trump Plan" for Gaza, formally unveiled by President Donald Trump on 29 September 2025, alongside Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, represents a comprehensive diplomatic initiative aimed at resolving the protracted conflict in the Gaza Strip and fostering broader regional peace 15. This proposal, distinct from an earlier plan made in February 2025, comprises twenty specific points designed to address the immediate cessation of hostilities, the humanitarian crisis, security concerns, and the future governance and development of the territory 15.

Genesis and Objectives of the Plan

The genesis of this plan lies in the recognition of the catastrophic and unsustainable nature of the nearly two-year war in Gaza, which escalated dramatically after the 7 October 2023 attacks 11, 19. Previous ceasefire efforts, including one mediated in January 2025, had collapsed, leading to a renewed Israeli offensive in March 2025 15, 19. The immense human cost, with over 67,000 Palestinian deaths by October 2025, coupled with widespread destruction and famine-like conditions, created an urgent imperative for a renewed and robust peace effort 9, 13, 19.

President Trump's stated objective for this plan extended beyond merely ending the Gaza war. He articulated a vision for it to form the basis for a comprehensive regional peace and an expansion of the Abraham Accords, the diplomatic normalisation agreements between Israel and several Arab countries that he had brokered during his first term 14. The plan's core aims include securing a complete surrender from Hamas, the release of all hostages, dismantling Hamas's military capabilities, and establishing a transitional governance structure in the Gaza Strip 5, 15. Ultimately, it sought to end the war, facilitate hostage returns, allow a surge of humanitarian aid, and initiate the reconstruction of the devastated enclave 4, 8, 15.

Phase One: Details and Implementation Modalities

Hamas's conditional acceptance on 3 October 2025, signalled a critical step towards the implementation of the plan, with the Israeli military being ordered to advance readiness for its first phase 4, 8, 15, 22. The details of this initial phase are multifaceted and interconnected:

  1. Immediate End to Fighting: The plan explicitly calls for an immediate cessation of hostilities in Gaza 4, 8, 15. This was the explicit demand made by President Trump to Israel on 3 October 2025, after Hamas's response 4, 7, 8, 9, 13, 14, 17, 18.
  2. Hostage Release and Prisoner Exchange: A central component of Phase One is the release of all remaining 48 Israeli hostages held in Gaza 4, 8, 15, 17, 19. This is to occur within 72 hours, in exchange for the release of more than 1,000 Palestinian political prisoners held in Israeli jails 4, 8, 15, 17, 19. Hamas agreed to this specific term, indicating its willingness to release all hostages, both living and deceased 15, 19. This aspect of the plan is widely seen as a crucial humanitarian imperative and a significant confidence-building measure.
  3. Gradual Withdrawal of Israeli Forces: The plan stipulates a gradual withdrawal of Israeli forces from the Gaza Strip 4, 8. While specific timelines for this withdrawal in Phase One are not explicitly detailed in all reports, the overall intent is to reduce Israel's military presence and allow for the return of displaced Palestinians and the commencement of humanitarian and reconstruction efforts.
  4. Surge of Humanitarian Aid and Reconstruction: Recognising the severe humanitarian crisis, including famine-like conditions caused by the Israeli blockade, the plan mandates a surge of aid to Gaza and the initiation of reconstruction efforts for the devastated strip 4, 8, 15, 18. The UN had previously prepared approximately 170,000 metric tonnes of food, medicine, shelter, and other vital supplies, poised for entry into Gaza, conditional on the opening of Israeli-controlled crossings and safe movement for aid workers and civilians 18.
  5. Disarmament of Hamas (Conditional): While the broader Trump plan calls for the complete disarmament of Hamas and its exclusion from future Gaza governance 4, 8, 14, 15, 17, reports suggest internal disagreements within Hamas regarding this specific demand 21. Hamas's political leadership was reportedly open to accepting the proposal with certain amendments, but its leader in Gaza, Izz al-Din al-Haddad, was reportedly willing to surrender offensive weapons but desired to retain "defensive" weapons such as assault rifles 15, 21. This point remains a significant area for negotiation.
  6. Transitional Governance Structure: The plan envisions a transitional authority for the Gaza Strip, led either by President Trump himself or by a body of independent Palestinian technocrats under international supervision 4, 8, 14, 15, 17, 22. Hamas agreed to hand over the administration of the Gaza Strip to such a Palestinian body, contingent on "Palestinian national consensus and Arabic and Islamic support" 18, 22. The plan further suggests that the West Bank-based Palestinian Authority would eventually govern Gaza, with Hamas having no role in its future administration 14.

Anticipated Outcomes and Challenges of Phase One

The anticipated outcomes of a successful Phase One implementation are profound. Foremost among them is an immediate cessation of the most destructive aspects of the conflict, bringing relief to the civilian population in Gaza. The release of all hostages would address a primary Israeli war objective and a profound societal wound. The influx of humanitarian aid and the commencement of reconstruction are critical for preventing further loss of life and alleviating immense suffering.

However, significant challenges loom. The most immediate is the inconsistency between the order to halt offensive operations and the reported continuation of Israeli strikes on Gaza City on 4 October 2025 4, 8, 9, 13, 21. This disparity raises questions about command and control, the precise interpretation of "defensive strikes," or a potential strategy to maintain pressure while peace talks proceed. Furthermore, the complexities of disarming Hamas and establishing an acceptable transitional governance structure present formidable hurdles, given the deep-seated mistrust and competing interests among Palestinian factions. Hamas's conditional acceptance implies that critical details still require intensive negotiation, and previous ceasefire efforts have often collapsed at this stage 4, 14. The deadline set by Trump for Hamas's full acceptance by 5 October 2025 also adds a layer of urgency and potential for renewed escalation if unmet 15, 19.

Broader Vision of the Trump Plan

Beyond Phase One, the Trump plan's broader vision, as outlined on 29 September 2025, includes several ambitious long-term goals. It calls for the demilitarisation of Gaza, the deployment of an international stabilisation force, and a conditional pathway towards Palestinian statehood 15. The plan also seeks to foster broader economic development and security across the region. President Trump articulated his hope that ending the Gaza war would pave the way for a comprehensive regional peace and an expansion of the Abraham Accords, viewing the initiative as "not about Gaza alone" but about "long sought PEACE in the Middle East" 14. The international support garnered from numerous countries, including major global and regional powers, suggests a widespread desire for the plan to succeed in bringing stability to a deeply volatile region 15.

Implications and Analysis

The developments of early October 2025, centred on Israel's military posture in Gaza City and the proposed Trump peace plan, carry profound implications across humanitarian, political, and geopolitical spheres. A thorough analysis reveals both opportunities for de-escalation and formidable challenges that could undermine the nascent peace efforts.

Impact on the Humanitarian Situation in Gaza

The immediate and most significant implication of a halt to offensive operations and a surge in humanitarian aid, as stipulated by Phase One of the Trump plan, directly addresses the catastrophic humanitarian crisis in Gaza. For nearly two years, the Gaza Strip has endured immense suffering, exacerbated by an Israeli blockade that has severely restricted the flow of essential goods 4, 19. Reports of famine-like conditions, the destruction of critical infrastructure, and an overwhelmed healthcare system have been persistent 4, 10, 19. The death toll exceeding 67,000 Palestinians and 169,000 wounded underscores the dire urgency of the situation 9, 11, 13.

A genuine and sustained cessation of hostilities, coupled with the unhindered entry of humanitarian aid, could offer immediate relief to the civilian population. The release of hostages would alleviate a major source of trauma and political pressure on Israel, potentially fostering an environment more conducive to dialogue. However, the reported continuation of Israeli strikes on 4 October 2025, despite the order to halt offensive operations, casts a shadow over these prospects 4, 8, 9, 13, 21. These ongoing strikes, which reportedly caused additional civilian casualties, highlight the precariousness of the situation and the inherent difficulties in transitioning from intense conflict to a full ceasefire 4, 8, 13, 21. Effective implementation of aid delivery would require safe corridors, reliable distribution mechanisms, and the lifting of restrictions that have previously hampered humanitarian efforts 18. The United Nations has stressed the necessity of opening Israeli-controlled crossings, ensuring safe movement for civilians and aid workers, unrestricted entry of goods, and the revival of the private sector to address the dire needs 18.

The long-term humanitarian impact also depends on the successful implementation of the reconstruction phase. With thousands of buildings destroyed in Gaza City alone 5, and extensive damage across the Strip, a massive international effort would be required to rebuild homes, hospitals, schools, and essential infrastructure. Without this, even with a ceasefire, the population would remain in a state of profound deprivation.

Political Ramifications within Israel

The Israeli political landscape surrounding the Trump plan is complex and fraught with internal divisions, despite the declared readiness to advance its first phase. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's government, having overseen a nearly two-year war, faces immense domestic pressure from multiple directions. The families of the remaining 48 hostages have been vocal in demanding their return, making the hostage release component of the plan a paramount concern for the Israeli public and a crucial objective for the government 4, 8, 15, 17, 19, 21. The agreement by Hamas to release all hostages is therefore a significant political victory for Netanyahu, allowing him to claim progress towards a core war aim 4, 8, 15, 17, 19, 22.

However, the broader terms of the Trump plan, particularly those concerning the disarmament of Hamas and the future governance of Gaza, present potential challenges to Netanyahu's right-wing coalition. Hardline elements within his government, such as National Security Minister Itamar Ben Gvir and Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich, have historically adopted maximalist positions regarding Palestinian territories and security, potentially resisting any concessions that could be perceived as empowering Palestinian entities or compromising Israeli security 21. Their potential to "sabotage" a deal is a notable concern, indicating that internal political wrangling could complicate the negotiation process 21.

Conversely, the widespread international support for the Trump plan, coupled with calls from opposition figures like Yair Lapid for unity in pursuing the deal, provides Netanyahu with a mandate and political cover to engage in negotiations 8, 9, 14, 15. The perceived opportunity for an end to the war and regional normalisation, as envisioned by Trump, could strengthen Netanyahu's position in the short term, but long-term political stability will depend on the successful management of internal dissent and the tangible benefits derived from the peace process.

Reactions from Palestinian Factions (Hamas, PA)

The response from Palestinian factions is critical for the success of any peace initiative. Hamas's conditional acceptance of the Trump plan, specifically its agreement to release all remaining hostages and surrender governing power of the Gaza Strip, marks a significant shift 4, 8, 9, 14, 15, 17, 18, 19, 22. This willingness to hand over administration to a Palestinian body of independent technocrats, based on "Palestinian national consensus and Arabic and Islamic support," could pave the way for a unified Palestinian administration, a long-standing international goal 18, 22.

However, the "conditions" attached to Hamas's acceptance and reported internal disagreements within the group present formidable challenges. While the political wing of Hamas was reportedly open to the proposal, its military wing expressed reservations, particularly regarding complete disarmament. The leader in Gaza, Izz al-Din Haddad, reportedly indicated willingness to surrender offensive weapons but desired to retain "defensive" ones 21. This internal division could complicate negotiations, as the complete disarmament of Hamas is a core tenet of the Trump plan and a key Israeli demand 4, 8, 14, 15, 17. The history of collapsed ceasefire efforts even after initial momentum suggests that the negotiation phase remains highly vulnerable 4, 14.

The involvement of Egypt in organising a conference for Palestinian factions to decide on Gaza's post-war future is a positive step towards fostering intra-Palestinian dialogue and potentially overcoming these divisions 4, 9, 17. The support of Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) for Hamas's response is also notable, indicating a degree of unified militant front in engaging with the plan 4. The role of the Palestinian Authority (PA) in Ramallah is also crucial. The Trump plan envisions the PA eventually governing Gaza 14. Bridging the long-standing rift between Hamas and Fatah, the dominant party in the PA, would be essential for establishing a legitimate and effective unified Palestinian administration. This would require substantial political will and international facilitation.

Regional Responses and Geopolitical Shifts

The Trump plan has garnered widespread international support, including from key regional and global powers such as France, Germany, Russia, Spain, the UAE, Egypt, Turkey, Qatar, Jordan, Indonesia, Pakistan, and the United Kingdom 15. This broad endorsement reflects a collective desire to end the prolonged conflict and address its destabilising effects on the wider Middle East.

For regional actors like Egypt and Qatar, who have historically played mediating roles, the plan offers an opportunity to assert their diplomatic influence and contribute to regional stability 18. The potential for an expansion of the Abraham Accords, which aim to normalise relations between Israel and more Arab states, is a significant geopolitical incentive for many, particularly Gulf states 14. Such an expansion could reshape regional alliances, further isolating Iran and its proxies, and fostering new economic and security cooperation frameworks.

However, the plan's success hinges on its ability to address core Palestinian grievances in a meaningful way. While conditional statehood is mentioned in the plan 15, its specifics and viability remain to be seen. If the plan is perceived as sidelining Palestinian rights or failing to deliver a just and equitable resolution, it could face rejection from elements of the Arab street and lead to renewed instability. The ongoing humanitarian crisis and the reported continuation of Israeli strikes, despite the halt order, could also complicate regional buy-in, as Arab states may face domestic pressure if the plan does not visibly improve the lives of Palestinians. The involvement of regional and international forces in a stabilisation mission, as suggested by the plan's demilitarisation aspect 15, would require significant diplomatic heavy lifting and consensus.

Challenges to Long-Term Stability and Peace

The journey from a ceasefire to lasting peace is fraught with immense challenges. The historical record of failed peace processes underscores the difficulty of translating initial agreements into durable political settlements.

Firstly, the core issues of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict – borders, settlements, Jerusalem, refugees, and security arrangements – remain deeply contentious. While the Trump plan addresses immediate concerns like hostage release and governance, the long-term political framework, including the nature of conditional Palestinian statehood, will require meticulous negotiation and compromises from all sides 15.

Secondly, the internal political divisions within both Israel and Palestine pose significant threats. Any agreement reached would need to command sufficient domestic support to be implementable and sustainable. The fragmentation of Palestinian leadership and the influence of hardline factions on both sides could easily derail the process.

Thirdly, security guarantees for both Israelis and Palestinians are paramount. The demilitarisation of Gaza and the disarmament of Hamas, as envisioned by the plan, are non-negotiable for Israel, but these are highly sensitive issues for Palestinian militant groups 15, 21. Establishing an effective international stabilisation force and robust verification mechanisms would be essential but politically challenging to deploy.

Finally, the reconstruction of Gaza, while critical, is a monumental undertaking. It requires not only financial resources but also a stable political and security environment. Without addressing the underlying causes of conflict and ensuring a viable future for Palestinians in Gaza, any reconstruction efforts risk being undone by renewed cycles of violence. The emphasis on a "lasting PEACE" and expanding the Abraham Accords indicates an ambitious scope for the Trump plan 14. However, achieving such an outcome demands an unprecedented level of commitment, flexibility, and good faith from all parties involved, supported by sustained and coordinated international pressure and assistance.

Conclusion

The strategic recalibration by the Israeli military in Gaza City on 4 October 2025, to halt offensive operations and transition to a defensive posture, in conjunction with preparations for the first phase of the United States President Donald Trump's peace plan, marks a highly significant and potentially transformative juncture in the nearly two-year war in the Gaza Strip. This development, confirmed by Israeli army radio and corroborated by numerous international news organisations, emerged amidst a backdrop of severe humanitarian catastrophe, characterised by immense civilian casualties and widespread destruction 4, 9, 10, 13. The order, issued by Israel’s political leadership, directly responds to President Trump’s demands for an immediate cessation of bombing after Hamas conveyed its conditional acceptance of the peace proposal 4, 7, 8, 9, 13, 14, 17, 18, 22.

The Trump plan, a comprehensive 20-point initiative unveiled in late September 2025, offers a multifaceted framework for resolution. Its initial phase focuses on an immediate ceasefire, the release of the remaining 48 Israeli hostages in exchange for Palestinian prisoners, a gradual withdrawal of Israeli forces, a critical surge of humanitarian aid to the besieged territory, and the commencement of reconstruction efforts 4, 8, 15, 17, 19. Longer-term aspirations include the disarmament of Hamas, the establishment of a transitional Palestinian technocratic authority for Gaza, overseen by international supervision, and a conditional pathway towards Palestinian statehood, all aimed at fostering broader regional peace and expanding the Abraham Accords 4, 8, 14, 15, 17, 22.

While Hamas’s agreement to the core tenets of hostage release and relinquishing governance represents a crucial step forward, internal divisions within the group regarding complete disarmament, coupled with the reported continuation of Israeli strikes despite the halt order, underscore the profound fragilities inherent in the nascent peace process 4, 8, 9, 13, 21. The humanitarian situation remains critical, demanding immediate and unimpeded aid delivery, and the prospects for long-term stability hinge on successful and sensitive navigation of complex issues such as borders, settlements, and the political future of a unified Palestinian administration.

The widespread international endorsement of the Trump plan, from the United Nations to major global and regional powers, reflects a collective global imperative to end the devastating conflict and alleviate the suffering of the Palestinian people 9, 15, 18. However, translating this international consensus into a durable peace will necessitate exceptional political will, sustained diplomatic engagement, and an unwavering commitment to compromise from all parties. The coming period, particularly with Egypt facilitating intra-Palestinian dialogue, will be instrumental in determining whether the momentum generated in early October 2025 can be successfully leveraged to pave the way for a lasting resolution to one of the world's most intractable conflicts, or if it will falter, leading to renewed cycles of violence and humanitarian despair. The situation remains highly fluid, demanding ongoing scrutiny and rigorous analysis to comprehend its evolving trajectory and profound geopolitical implications.
