Japan Is Set for Its First Female Prime Minister

Article created and last updated on: Sunday 05 October 2025 00:19

Abstract

On the 4th of October 2025, Japan's ruling Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) elected Sanae Takaichi as its new leader, a decision that has positioned her to become the nation's first female prime minister 11, 12, 13. This landmark event in Japanese political history occurred amidst a period of considerable political instability, with the LDP having lost its majority in both houses of the Diet for the first time since its formation in 1955 5, 31. Takaichi, a 64-year-old hard-line conservative and a protégé of the late Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, secured the party presidency in a run-off election against the more moderate Shinjirō Koizumi 3, 26, 41. Her victory signifies a notable shift to the right for the LDP and presents a complex array of challenges and opportunities for Japan. This article provides a comprehensive analysis of Sanae Takaichi's ascent to power, examining the intricate political, social, and economic context that precipitated this historic moment. It delves into her political career and ideological underpinnings, particularly her conservative stances on social issues, her hawkish foreign and security policy, and her proposed economic agenda. The article further explores the profound significance of Japan having its first female premier in a traditionally male-dominated political landscape, while also critically assessing the potential limitations of her leadership in advancing gender equality. Finally, it considers the formidable challenges that await the Takaichi administration, including the need to navigate a minority government, address deep-seated economic issues, and manage increasingly complex geopolitical relationships in the Indo-Pacific region.

Introduction

The election of Sanae Takaichi as the president of the Liberal Democratic Party on the 4th of October 2025, represents a watershed moment in the political annals of Japan 11, 12, 13. In a nation where political leadership has been overwhelmingly male for centuries, the prospect of a female prime minister has long been a subject of speculation and debate. Takaichi's victory, therefore, is not merely a change in leadership but a symbolic shattering of one of the most resilient glass ceilings in the developed world 14. This event, however, is not without its paradoxes. Takaichi, while breaking new ground for women in Japanese politics, is an avowed conservative who has consistently opposed policies aimed at promoting gender equality, such as allowing married couples to have separate surnames and recognising same-sex marriage 3, 16, 37. Her rise to power, therefore, prompts a nuanced discussion about the nature of female leadership and its impact on feminist progress.

The political context of Takaichi's election is one of profound uncertainty for the LDP. The party, which has dominated Japanese politics almost uninterruptedly since its inception in 1955 2, 4, 8, is currently in a precarious position. Following significant losses in the October 2024 House of Representatives election and the July 2025 House of Councillors election, the LDP and its coalition partner, Komeito, no longer command a majority in either chamber of the Diet 5, 15, 22, 27. This new reality of a minority government will necessitate a different style of leadership, one that is adept at negotiation and coalition-building with opposition parties 12, 34, 41. The resignation of her predecessor, Shigeru Ishiba, after a short and tumultuous tenure, underscores the immense pressure facing any leader of the embattled LDP 5, 15, 31.

Takaichi's policy platform, deeply rooted in the conservative ideology of her mentor, the late Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, signals a significant rightward shift for the LDP 17, 40. Her hawkish stance on foreign and security policy, including her advocacy for increased defence spending and a revision of Japan's pacifist constitution, is likely to create new dynamics in East Asia, particularly in relations with China and South Korea 12, 37, 40. On the economic front, she has championed a return to the principles of "Abenomics," with its focus on aggressive monetary easing and fiscal stimulus, despite growing concerns about Japan's colossal public debt and persistent economic stagnation 34. Her social conservatism, as mentioned, stands in stark contrast to the liberalising trends seen in many other advanced economies.

This article aims to provide a comprehensive and scholarly analysis of Sanae Takaichi's rise to the pinnacle of Japanese politics. It will begin by providing a detailed background on the political landscape of Japan in the years leading up to the 2025 LDP leadership election, with a particular focus on the challenges that have beset the LDP. It will then examine Takaichi's political career, her ideological convictions, and the key policy proposals that she has articulated. A significant portion of the article will be dedicated to exploring the multifaceted implications of Japan having its first female prime minister, considering both the symbolic and practical consequences of this development. The article will also offer a forward-looking assessment of the challenges and prospects for the Takaichi administration, both domestically and internationally. In doing so, this article seeks to provide a thorough and objective understanding of this pivotal moment in Japanese history and its potential ramifications for the future of the nation.

The Enduring Dominance and Recent Decline of the Liberal Democratic Party

To fully comprehend the significance of Sanae Takaichi's election, it is essential to understand the political entity she now leads: the Liberal Democratic Party (LDP). Formed in 1955 through the merger of two conservative parties, the Liberal Party and the Japan Democratic Party, the LDP has been the dominant force in Japanese politics for the vast majority of the post-war era 2, 4, 19. This period of near-unbroken rule is often referred to as the "1955 System" 2. The LDP's success can be attributed to several factors, including its close ties with the business community and the bureaucracy, its ability to deliver economic growth during Japan's "economic miracle" of the 1960s and 1970s, and its alignment with the United States in its foreign policy 2, 4, 8. The LDP is not a monolithic entity but rather a broad coalition of factions, or "habatsu," which have historically played a crucial role in determining the party's leadership and policy direction 7, 18, 23. These factions are not strictly based on ideology but are often formed around personal loyalties to influential politicians 4, 7, 25.

The LDP's long reign has not been without its challenges. The party has been plagued by a series of corruption scandals over the years, which have periodically eroded public trust 4. The bursting of Japan's asset price bubble in the early 1990s ushered in a period of prolonged economic stagnation, often referred to as the "lost decades," which further tested the LDP's ability to govern effectively 6, 36, 43. The party did experience a brief period in opposition between 1993 and 1994, and again from 2009 to 2012, when the Democratic Party of Japan (DPJ) came to power 4, 30. However, the DPJ's time in government was marred by internal divisions and policy missteps, ultimately leading to the LDP's return to power under Shinzo Abe in 2012 36.

The years leading up to the 2025 LDP leadership election have been particularly turbulent for the party. A major political fundraising scandal involving several LDP factions, most notably the one formerly led by Shinzo Abe, came to light in late 2023 and early 2024, severely damaging the party's reputation 7, 10, 25. The scandal led to the dissolution of several major factions and prompted calls for significant political reform 7, 25. Public dissatisfaction with the LDP's handling of the scandal, coupled with persistent economic anxieties, contributed to the party's poor performance in the 2024 general election and the 2025 House of Councillors election 5, 10, 22, 27. These electoral defeats resulted in the LDP losing its majority in both houses of the Diet, a historic setback for the party 5, 31. The ensuing political instability led to the resignation of Prime Minister Shigeru Ishiba in September 2025, paving the way for the leadership contest that would ultimately see Sanae Takaichi emerge as the victor 5, 15, 31.

The Political Trajectory of Sanae Takaichi

Sanae Takaichi's journey to the leadership of the LDP has been a long and eventful one, marked by a steadfast commitment to her conservative principles. Born in Nara Prefecture in 1961, Takaichi's early career was not in politics. After graduating from university, she worked as a researcher at a think tank and later as a television journalist 3, 14. She first entered the political arena in 1993, when she was elected to the House of Representatives as an independent 3, 14. She joined the LDP in 1996 and quickly established herself as a rising star within the party's conservative wing 3.

A pivotal moment in Takaichi's career came with her association with Shinzo Abe. She became a close ally and protégé of the future prime minister, and her political fortunes rose in tandem with his 3. During Abe's first stint as prime minister from 2006 to 2007, Takaichi served in several cabinet posts, including Minister of State for Okinawa and Northern Territories Affairs, Minister of State for Science and Technology Policy, and Minister of State for Gender Equality 3, 16, 42. When Abe returned to power in 2012, Takaichi was once again appointed to senior positions, most notably as Minister for Internal Affairs and Communications and later as Minister of State for Economic Security 3, 14.

Takaichi has made several bids for the LDP leadership. In 2021, she ran to succeed Yoshihide Suga, but was eliminated in the first round of voting, placing third 3. She ran again in 2024, this time coming in first in the first round but narrowly losing to Shigeru Ishiba in the run-off 3, 10. Her successful campaign in 2025 was her third attempt at the party's top job 3, 14. Her persistence and her ability to build a strong support base among the party's rank-and-file members were key to her eventual victory 41.

Takaichi is known for her unabashedly conservative and nationalist views 3, 40. She is a regular visitor to the Yasukuni Shrine, which honours Japan's war dead, including convicted war criminals, a practice that has drawn criticism from China and South Korea 37, 40. She is a strong advocate for revising Japan's post-war constitution to clarify the legal status of the Self-Defence Forces and to remove its pacifist clauses 8. In terms of social policy, she is a staunch traditionalist, opposing same-sex marriage, dual surnames for married couples, and the idea of a female emperor 3, 16, 37, 40. Her economic policies are heavily influenced by "Abenomics," favouring bold monetary and fiscal stimulus to revitalise the Japanese economy 34, 40. She has often cited the former British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher as a role model, although her emphasis on fiscal expansion differs significantly from Thatcher's focus on fiscal discipline 11, 14, 16.

The State of Women in Japanese Politics

The election of Japan's first female prime minister is a development that cannot be fully appreciated without considering the broader context of women's participation in Japanese politics. Japan has consistently ranked low among developed nations in terms of gender equality in the political sphere 9, 24, 32. Despite women gaining the right to vote in 1945, their representation in the National Diet has remained stubbornly low 9, 24. As of 2025, women held only about 15% of the seats in the powerful House of Representatives 16, 21. The World Economic Forum's 2025 Global Gender Gap Report ranked Japan 118th out of 148 countries, with a particularly poor performance in the area of political empowerment 32.

Several factors have been cited to explain the underrepresentation of women in Japanese politics. Traditional gender roles, which often place a heavy burden of domestic and caregiving responsibilities on women, can make it difficult for them to pursue a demanding political career 9. The male-dominated culture of Japanese political parties, particularly the LDP, has also been identified as a significant barrier 16, 42. Female politicians have often reported facing sexism and a lack of support from their male colleagues 16. The electoral system has also been seen as a contributing factor, with some arguing that the single-member district system disadvantages female candidates 23.

Despite these challenges, there have been some signs of progress in recent years. The number of female candidates running for office has been gradually increasing, and several women have been elected to prominent positions, such as Yuriko Koike, the governor of Tokyo, and Renho Murata, the former leader of the opposition Democratic Party 9. The Japanese Communist Party elected its first female leader, Tomoko Tamura, in 2024 21. The election of Sanae Takaichi as LDP leader and prime minister-in-waiting is undoubtedly the most significant milestone to date in the journey towards greater female representation in Japanese politics. However, as will be discussed in a later section, the extent to which her premiership will translate into tangible progress for women's rights and gender equality remains a subject of considerable debate.

The 2025 Liberal Democratic Party Leadership Election

The 2025 LDP presidential election was held on the 4th of October and was a direct consequence of the resignation of Prime Minister Shigeru Ishiba on the 7th of September 2025 5, 15, 31. Ishiba's decision to step down was prompted by the LDP's historic losses in the 2024 general election and the 2025 House of Councillors election, which left the party without a majority in either house of the Diet 5, 27, 31. The leadership contest was seen as a crucial moment for the LDP, a chance to select a new leader who could potentially reverse the party's declining fortunes and navigate the challenging political landscape of a minority government.

The election saw five candidates vying for the party's top post: Sanae Takaichi, former Minister of State for Economic Security; Shinjirō Koizumi, Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries; Yoshimasa Hayashi, Chief Cabinet Secretary; Takayuki Kobayashi, former Minister of State for Economic Security; and Toshimitsu Motegi, former LDP Secretary-General 5, 33, 41. The campaign was dominated by discussions on how to revitalise the Japanese economy, address the country's demographic challenges, and manage its increasingly complex foreign policy relationships 33, 40. The LDP's recent fundraising scandal and the need for party reform were also key issues 13.

The LDP leadership election is a two-stage process 5, 28, 29, 38. In the first round, votes are cast by both LDP Diet members and rank-and-file party members across the country 5, 28. If no candidate wins an outright majority in the first round, a run-off is held between the top two candidates 28, 38. In the run-off, only Diet members and the 47 prefectural LDP chapters cast votes 10, 28, 38.

In the first round of the 2025 election, no candidate secured a majority. Sanae Takaichi emerged as the frontrunner, receiving 31.07% of the vote 3, 5. Shinjirō Koizumi came in second with 27.84% of the vote, thus advancing to the run-off against Takaichi 3, 5. The results of the first round highlighted Takaichi's strong support among the LDP's grassroots members, a crucial factor in her eventual victory 41.

In the run-off election, Takaichi defeated Koizumi, securing 185 votes to Koizumi's 156 26. Her victory was attributed to her ability to consolidate the support of the party's conservative wing, as well as her popularity with the LDP's regional chapters 41. The result was a redemption for Takaichi, who had narrowly lost the 2024 leadership election to Shigeru Ishiba in a run-off 3, 10, 40. Her election as the LDP's first female president has set the stage for her to be confirmed as Japan's first female prime minister in a parliamentary vote expected to be held in mid-October 5, 12, 14.

Sanae Takaichi's Political Ideology and Policy Platform

Sanae Takaichi's political ideology can be best described as a form of conservative nationalism that is deeply influenced by the political philosophy of her mentor, Shinzo Abe. Her policy platform reflects a commitment to a strong and assertive Japan, both domestically and internationally. This section will provide a detailed analysis of her key policy positions, covering her views on the economy, foreign and security policy, and social issues.

Economic Policy: A Return to 'Sanaenomics'

Takaichi's economic agenda, which has been dubbed "Sanaenomics," is largely a continuation of the "Abenomics" policies pursued by Shinzo Abe 34. The core of her economic strategy is to combat deflation and stimulate growth through a combination of aggressive monetary easing, flexible fiscal policy, and structural reforms 34. She has advocated for increased public spending to boost the economy, particularly in areas such as infrastructure and technological innovation 40. This emphasis on fiscal stimulus sets her apart from more fiscally conservative members of the LDP and has raised concerns about Japan's already enormous public debt, which is approaching 250% of GDP 34, 43.

Takaichi has also expressed a degree of scepticism about the benefits of globalisation and has called for a review of some of Japan's trade agreements. She has raised the possibility of renegotiating a trade and investment deal with the United States, in which former President Donald Trump agreed to lower tariffs on Japanese automobiles in exchange for significant Japanese investment in the US 17, 40. Her focus on economic security, a portfolio she held in a previous cabinet, suggests that she will prioritise protecting Japan's critical industries and supply chains from external threats 3, 14.

However, Takaichi's economic policies face significant challenges. Japan is grappling with a number of deep-seated structural problems, including a declining and ageing population, low productivity growth, and stagnant wages 6, 35, 36, 43, 44. Critics of Abenomics argue that it failed to address these fundamental issues and instead exacerbated wealth inequality 34. It remains to be seen whether "Sanaenomics" will be able to deliver the sustainable economic growth that has eluded Japan for so long.

Foreign and Security Policy: A Hawkish Stance

In the realm of foreign and security policy, Takaichi is known for her hawkish and nationalist views 12, 41. She is a strong proponent of strengthening Japan's defence capabilities and has called for a significant increase in the defence budget, potentially to 2% of GDP or even higher 34. A central plank of her security agenda is the revision of Article 9 of the Japanese Constitution, the so-called "peace clause," which renounces war as a sovereign right of the nation 8. Takaichi, like Abe, believes that the constitution should be amended to explicitly recognise the legitimacy of the Self-Defence Forces and to allow Japan to play a more active role in international security affairs.

Takaichi's stance on China is particularly firm. She has been a vocal critic of Beijing's assertive behaviour in the East and South China Seas and has called for a more robust response to China's growing military power 40. Her views on Taiwan are also noteworthy; she has expressed support for the island's democracy and has advocated for closer ties between Japan and Taiwan. Her revisionist views on Japan's wartime history, including her regular visits to the Yasukuni Shrine, are likely to be a source of friction in Japan's relations with China and South Korea 37, 40.

Takaichi is a staunch supporter of the US-Japan security alliance, which she sees as the cornerstone of Japan's foreign policy. However, her nationalist inclinations and her willingness to challenge existing trade agreements could potentially lead to a more transactional and less predictable relationship with the United States, particularly if Donald Trump were to return to the presidency 11, 15.

Social Policy: A Bastion of Conservatism

Takaichi's social conservatism is one of the most defining aspects of her political identity. She is a staunch defender of traditional family values and has consistently opposed progressive social reforms 3, 16, 37, 40. She is a vocal opponent of same-sex marriage and has argued that allowing married couples to use separate surnames would undermine the family unit 3, 40. Her views on gender equality are also deeply rooted in traditionalism. While she has spoken of the need to support women in the workplace, she has also emphasised the importance of their roles as mothers and wives 16, 37.

One of the most contentious issues in Japanese social policy is the question of imperial succession. Under current law, only males in the paternal line of the imperial family can ascend to the Chrysanthemum Throne. With a dwindling number of male heirs, there have been calls to amend the law to allow for a female emperor or for the throne to be passed down through the maternal line. Takaichi is a firm opponent of any such changes, arguing that the male-only succession tradition must be preserved 3, 16, 37.

Takaichi has also taken a hard line on immigration. In a country that has historically been reluctant to accept large numbers of foreign workers, Takaichi has called for stricter controls on immigration and a crackdown on illegal immigration 13, 40. Her rhetoric on this issue is seen as an attempt to appeal to voters who have been drawn to the nationalist and anti-immigrant message of some of Japan's emerging right-wing populist parties 13, 40.

The Significance of Japan's First Female Prime Minister

The ascent of Sanae Takaichi to the premiership of Japan is an event laden with historical and symbolic significance. In a society that has long been characterised by its patriarchal structures and its underrepresentation of women in positions of power, the image of a woman leading the government is a powerful one. This section will explore the multifaceted implications of this development, examining its potential impact on gender equality, women's political participation, and the broader social and political landscape of Japan.

A Symbolic Breakthrough with Complex Implications

The most immediate and undeniable significance of Takaichi's premiership is its symbolic power. For generations of Japanese women who have faced systemic barriers to their advancement, the sight of a female prime minister is a powerful symbol of change and possibility 14. It challenges the long-held notion that the highest echelons of political power are the exclusive domain of men. This symbolic breakthrough has the potential to inspire more women and girls to pursue careers in politics and other leadership roles, and to foster a greater sense of political efficacy among female citizens.

However, the nature of Takaichi's political ideology complicates any straightforward celebration of her premiership as a victory for feminism. As has been detailed, Takaichi is a social conservative who has consistently opposed policies that are widely seen as central to the advancement of gender equality 3, 16, 37. Her opposition to dual surnames for married couples, a policy that would primarily benefit women who wish to retain their own names, and her resistance to same-sex marriage, a key issue for LGBTQ+ rights, place her at odds with many feminist and progressive movements in Japan.

This paradox has led to a nuanced and at times critical reaction to her rise to power. While some have hailed it as a historic achievement, others have expressed concern that her policies could actually set back the cause of women's rights 16, 17, 37. They argue that her focus on traditional family values and her emphasis on women's roles as mothers and wives could reinforce outdated gender stereotypes and undermine efforts to achieve genuine gender equality 16, 37. This debate highlights a broader question that is relevant in many parts of the world: is the mere presence of a woman in a position of power sufficient to advance the interests of women as a group, or is the content of her policies and her commitment to feminist principles the more important factor?

Potential Impact on Women's Political Participation

One of the key questions surrounding Takaichi's premiership is whether it will lead to a tangible increase in the number of women participating in Japanese politics. The "role model effect" is often cited as a potential benefit of having a female leader. The visibility of a woman in the highest office could inspire other women to enter the political arena, and it could also help to normalise the idea of female leadership in the minds of voters and party officials.

Takaichi herself has made some gestures towards promoting women in politics. During her leadership campaign, she pledged to increase the number of female ministers in her cabinet and to promote women to senior positions within the LDP 16, 32, 37. If she follows through on these promises, it could have a significant impact on the gender balance of power in Japanese politics. However, there are also reasons to be sceptical. The LDP remains a deeply male-dominated party, and there is likely to be considerable resistance to any radical changes to the status quo 16, 42. Takaichi's ability to effect meaningful change will depend on her political will, her ability to build coalitions of support, and the extent to which she is willing to challenge the entrenched interests of the party's male establishment.

Furthermore, some analysts have argued that Takaichi's success may be an anomaly, a product of her unique political skills and her close ties to the powerful Abe faction, rather than a sign of a broader shift in the political landscape 33. They suggest that the structural barriers that have long hindered women's political participation in Japan are still very much in place and that it will take more than one female prime minister to dismantle them.

Navigating a Male-Dominated Political Culture

As Japan's first female prime minister, Sanae Takaichi will inevitably face a level of scrutiny and a set of challenges that her male predecessors did not. She will have to navigate a political culture that is still deeply ingrained with patriarchal norms and expectations. The media is likely to pay close attention to her appearance, her personal life, and her leadership style in ways that it would not for a male leader. She may also face resistance and condescension from some of her male colleagues, both within her own party and in the opposition.

Takaichi's own personal style may be an asset in this regard. She has cultivated an image as a tough and determined politician who is not afraid to stand her ground. Her hobbies, which include playing the drums in a heavy metal band and riding motorcycles, also contribute to her unconventional image and may help to subvert traditional gender stereotypes 14, 37, 40. However, she will also have to be careful to avoid being seen as either too "masculine" or too "feminine," a double bind that many female leaders have had to navigate.

Ultimately, Takaichi's success as prime minister will be judged not on her gender but on her ability to govern effectively. If she is able to deliver on her policy promises, revive the economy, and enhance Japan's security, she will be seen as a successful leader, regardless of her gender. If she fails, her gender may be unfairly used as a scapegoat. The challenges she faces are immense, and her performance will be closely watched, both in Japan and around the world, as a test case for female leadership in one of the world's most powerful nations.

Challenges and Prospects for the Takaichi Administration

Sanae Takaichi assumes the leadership of Japan at a time of profound domestic and international challenges. Her administration will be confronted with a complex and demanding agenda, from navigating the intricacies of a minority government to addressing deep-seated economic problems and managing an increasingly volatile geopolitical environment. This final section will provide a forward-looking analysis of the key challenges and prospects for the Takaichi administration.

The Challenge of Governing Without a Majority

The most immediate and pressing challenge for Takaichi will be to govern effectively without a majority in either house of the Diet 12, 34, 41. This is a new and unfamiliar situation for the LDP, which has long been accustomed to wielding absolute power. To pass legislation and implement her policy agenda, Takaichi will need to build coalitions with opposition parties. This will require a level of political skill, pragmatism, and willingness to compromise that has not always been a hallmark of LDP leadership.

The most likely coalition partner for the LDP is Nippon Ishin no Kai (Japan Innovation Party), a conservative opposition party that shares some common ground with Takaichi on issues such as constitutional reform and a strong national defence 34. However, any coalition agreement will inevitably involve policy concessions, which could alienate some members of Takaichi's own party, particularly those on the far right. The process of coalition-building will be a delicate balancing act, and any missteps could lead to political gridlock and instability.

The prospect of a "revolving door" of prime ministers, which plagued Japanese politics between 2006 and 2012, is a real concern 11, 34. If Takaichi is unable to forge a stable governing coalition and deliver on her promises, her premiership could be short-lived. Her ability to unite her own fractured party and to reach out to the opposition will be crucial to her political survival.

Tackling Japan's Economic Malaise

Revitalising the Japanese economy will be another major test for the Takaichi administration. Japan has been struggling with low growth, deflation, and a declining population for decades 6, 35, 36, 43, 44. The COVID-19 pandemic has exacerbated these problems, and the country is now facing rising inflation and stagnant wages 6, 34, 43. Takaichi's proposed solution, a return to the expansionary policies of "Abenomics," is a gamble. While fiscal and monetary stimulus may provide a short-term boost to the economy, they are unlikely to address the underlying structural issues that are holding Japan back.

A key challenge will be to find a sustainable solution to Japan's demographic crisis. With a rapidly ageing population and a declining birth rate, Japan is facing a shrinking workforce and a growing burden on its social security system 6, 35, 43. Takaichi's opposition to large-scale immigration will make it difficult to address the country's labour shortages. Finding a way to boost productivity and encourage greater female participation in the workforce will be essential, but these are long-term challenges that will not be easily solved.

The issue of Japan's massive public debt will also loom large over the Takaichi administration. Her commitment to increased public spending will further strain the country's finances and could lead to a loss of confidence in the Japanese government bond market. Balancing the need for economic stimulus with the imperative of fiscal consolidation will be a formidable challenge.

Navigating a Perilous Geopolitical Landscape

The international environment that Takaichi inherits is more complex and dangerous than at any time since the end of the Cold War. The rise of China, the war in Ukraine, and the potential for a more isolationist United States have created a new era of geopolitical uncertainty. Takaichi's hawkish foreign and security policy is a response to these challenges, but it also carries risks.

Her firm stance on China and her revisionist views on history could lead to increased tensions with Japan's most important neighbour. While a stronger Japan may be welcomed by some in the region, it could also trigger a new arms race in East Asia. Managing the relationship with China will require a delicate mix of deterrence and diplomacy, and it is not yet clear whether Takaichi has the diplomatic skills to navigate this complex relationship.

The future of the US-Japan alliance will also be a key concern. While Takaichi is a strong supporter of the alliance, her nationalist rhetoric and her willingness to challenge existing agreements could create friction with Washington. The outcome of the 2028 US presidential election will be a crucial factor in shaping the future of the alliance. A return to the "America First" policies of Donald Trump could put significant strain on the relationship and force Japan to take on a greater share of the burden for its own defence.

In conclusion, Sanae Takaichi's premiership marks a new and uncertain chapter in Japanese politics. Her historic ascent to power is a testament to her political skill and determination, but she faces a daunting array of challenges. Her ability to navigate the treacherous waters of a minority government, to revitalise a moribund economy, and to steer Japan through a perilous geopolitical landscape will determine the success or failure of her administration. The world will be watching to see whether Japan's first female prime minister can rise to the occasion and lead her country into a new era of strength and prosperity.
