A New Era in Japanese Politics

The Ascent of Sanae Takaichi and the Implications of a First Female Premiership

Article created and last updated on: Sunday 05 October 2025 00:14

Abstract

This article provides a comprehensive analysis of the political ascent of Sanae Takaichi to the leadership of Japan's Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) on the 4th of October 2025, a development that positions her to become the nation's first female prime minister 2, 4, 6. It examines the intricate political dynamics that precipitated this historic event, including the resignation of her predecessor, Shigeru Ishiba, amidst declining public support and internal party turmoil 4, 5. The article delves into the multifaceted political persona of Takaichi, scrutinising her ideological underpinnings as a hard-line conservative and nationalist, and her close political alignment with the late former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe 4, 35. A detailed exploration of her policy platform is undertaken, encompassing her hawkish stance on national security and foreign relations, particularly with China and South Korea, her advocacy for expansionary economic policies reminiscent of 'Abenomics', and her socially conservative positions on matters such as imperial succession and same-sex marriage 4, 15, 35. The analysis extends to the broader implications of her premiership, considering the monumental significance of a woman attaining the highest political office in a society marked by a persistent gender gap in politics, while also acknowledging the critiques of her record on gender equality issues 7, 15, 32. The article further assesses the formidable challenges that await her administration, including the imperative of unifying a fractured LDP, navigating a precarious parliamentary situation where the ruling coalition lacks majorities, addressing pressing economic concerns, and managing complex international relationships 12, 14. Through a detailed and objective examination, this article aims to offer a scholarly perspective on a pivotal moment in contemporary Japanese political history.

Introduction

The election of Sanae Takaichi as the president of the Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) on the 4th of October 2025 represents a watershed moment in the political landscape of post-war Japan 2, 6. As the leader of the nation's predominant political party, she is all but certain to be confirmed as Japan's first female prime minister, shattering a significant barrier in a society that has historically exhibited a pronounced gender disparity in political representation 4, 7, 20. This development, while monumental in its symbolism, is embedded in a complex web of political, social, and economic factors that have shaped the trajectory of Japanese governance in recent years. The significance of Takaichi's rise extends far beyond the realm of gender politics; it signals a potential ideological shift within the ruling party and portends significant implications for Japan's domestic policy agenda and its posture on the international stage 4.

This article will provide an in-depth, scholarly examination of the rise of Sanae Takaichi and the multifaceted context surrounding her impending premiership. It will begin by elucidating the political background that led to the LDP leadership contest, including the factors contributing to the resignation of Prime Minister Shigeru Ishiba and the state of the LDP itself 4, 5, 19. It will then offer a detailed biographical sketch of Takaichi, tracing her political career, her ideological evolution, and her key policy stances that have defined her as a prominent figure on the conservative wing of the LDP 3, 25. A critical analysis of her proposed policies will be undertaken, covering the domains of economic strategy, national security, foreign relations, and social issues. The article will also explore the profound paradox of her ascent: a female leader who has not historically championed feminist causes, and what this might mean for the future of gender equality in Japan 4, 15, 32. Finally, it will assess the formidable challenges and opportunities that will confront the Takaichi administration as it takes the helm of the world's fourth-largest economy at a time of considerable domestic and international uncertainty 4, 12.

The Political Decline of the Liberal Democratic Party and the Preceding Administrations

The political climate leading to the October 2025 Liberal Democratic Party leadership election was one of significant instability and public discontent for the long-governing party 12. The LDP, which has dominated Japanese politics almost uninterruptedly since its formation in 1955, found itself in a precarious position 11, 22, 26. The preceding years had been marked by a series of challenges that eroded public trust and led to significant electoral setbacks.

The premiership of Fumio Kishida, who served from October 2021 to August 2024, was beset by difficulties 28. His administration grappled with the fallout from political funding scandals, most notably a slush fund controversy involving unreported income from fundraising parties that implicated several LDP factions 23, 31. These revelations, coupled with public scrutiny over the party's historical ties to the controversial Unification Church following the assassination of former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe in July 2022, caused Kishida's approval ratings to plummet to record lows 9, 31. Facing immense pressure and the prospect of leading a deeply unpopular party into future elections, Kishida announced his resignation on the 14th of August 2024, stating it was necessary to show the public a "reborn LDP" 9, 23, 24.

His successor, Shigeru Ishiba, who took office in September 2024, inherited a deeply divided party and a disillusioned electorate 28. Ishiba's tenure was brief and fraught with challenges 19. He struggled to unify the LDP's factions and failed to reverse the party's declining fortunes 30. The LDP-led coalition suffered damaging defeats in parliamentary elections, losing its majority in both the upper and lower houses of the Diet 4, 12. This created a "twisted Diet" scenario, where the government would need cooperation from opposition parties to pass legislation, leading to political gridlock and further instability 2, 14. Less than a year into his term, facing continued low approval ratings and the inability to effectively govern, Ishiba announced he would step down, triggering the LDP leadership contest of October 2025 4, 6. The political turmoil reflected a broader public frustration with the LDP's perceived inability to address core economic challenges, such as rising prices and stagnant wages, alongside the persistent stain of political corruption 5, 12. This context of crisis and the urgent need for the LDP to present a new face to the public set the stage for a leadership election that would ultimately lead to a historic outcome.

The 2025 Liberal Democratic Party Leadership Election

The leadership election for the Liberal Democratic Party held on the 4th of October 2025 was a keenly contested affair that culminated in a runoff vote 2. Five candidates initially entered the race, but the contest ultimately narrowed to a head-to-head battle between Sanae Takaichi and the Agriculture Minister, Shinjiro Koizumi, son of the popular former Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi 2, 6. The election was not merely a contest of personalities but represented a struggle for the ideological soul of the party, which was reeling from internal divisions and a loss of public confidence 12.

In the first round of voting, which involved LDP parliamentarians and rank-and-file party members, none of the candidates managed to secure an outright majority, necessitating a second-round ballot between the top two contenders 2, 14. Takaichi emerged as the frontrunner in the initial phase 2. The runoff vote was restricted to Diet members and representatives of the LDP's prefectural chapters. Takaichi ultimately secured victory with 185 votes to Koizumi's 156 2. Her success was attributed to a strong showing among the party's lawmakers, where she garnered 149 votes, supplemented by 36 votes from the prefectural chapters 2. Koizumi, conversely, received 145 votes from Diet members and only 11 from the regional chapters 2.

Takaichi's victory marked the culmination of her third bid for the party leadership, demonstrating her persistence and growing influence within the LDP 2, 4. Her campaign successfully mobilised the conservative wing of the party, appealing to members who felt the LDP had become too moderate under previous leaders 12. After her election, Takaichi acknowledged the challenging road ahead, stating, “I'm feeling how tough it's going to be from here on, rather than feeling happy” 2. She also emphasised the need for party unity, calling on "everyone, and of all generations" to help rebuild the LDP 2. The election result was a clear indication that a significant portion of the LDP sought a leader with a strong, conservative vision to navigate the party through its period of crisis.

Early Life and Entry into Politics

Sanae Takaichi was born on the 7th of March 1961, in Nara Prefecture 3, 25. Raised in a conservative household, her father was a factory worker and her mother a policewoman 17. She is a self-made politician, the first in her family to be elected to public office 4, 17. Her early life reveals a non-traditional path for a future conservative leader; during her university years at Kobe University, where she studied business management, she was a drummer in a heavy metal band and an avid motorcycle rider 4, 17, 32.

After graduating, Takaichi's interest in politics led her to the United States, where she worked from 1987 to 1989 as a congressional fellow for Democratic Representative Patricia Schroeder 8, 17. This experience provided her with firsthand exposure to the workings of a different political system. Upon her return to Japan, she worked as a television presenter and commentator before launching her political career 17.

Takaichi was first elected to the House of Representatives in the 1993 general election, initially as an independent candidate 3, 25. Her early political affiliations were fluid; she briefly joined the minor 'Liberals' party, which then merged into the New Frontier Party 3. In 1996, she was re-elected under the New Frontier Party banner, but later that year, she made the pivotal decision to join the Liberal Democratic Party, responding to an invitation from the then LDP Secretary-General Koichi Kato 3. This move marked the beginning of her long and influential career within Japan's dominant conservative party.

Ministerial Career and Political Ideology

Throughout her career in the Liberal Democratic Party, Sanae Takaichi has consistently positioned herself on the party's right-wing, establishing a reputation as a hard-line conservative and nationalist 3, 4. She is a long-standing member of the ultranationalist organisation Nippon Kaigi 3. Her political ascent was significantly bolstered by her close association with former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, who served as her mentor and appointed her to several key positions during his tenures in office 3, 17, 35.

Takaichi has held numerous high-profile ministerial posts, demonstrating a breadth of experience in government. Her cabinet roles have included Minister for Internal Affairs and Communications, Minister of State for Gender Equality, and most recently, Minister of State for Economic Security from 2022 to 2024 under the Kishida administration 3, 4, 17. She also served as the chairperson of the LDP's influential Policy Research Council, a position considered a significant stepping stone for party leadership 3, 17.

Her political ideology is deeply rooted in a conservative vision for Japan. She is a proponent of revising Japan's post-war pacifist constitution, a long-held goal of the LDP's conservative wing 2. On social issues, her views are staunchly traditionalist. She opposes the legalisation of same-sex marriage and has resisted legislative changes that would permit married couples to use separate surnames, arguing such a move would undermine traditional family values 3, 15, 35. Furthermore, she is a firm supporter of maintaining the male-only line of succession for the Imperial Throne 15. Takaichi has often cited former British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher as a political role model, aligning herself with a brand of strong, conservative leadership 3, 12, 15. Her consistent and unapologetic articulation of these conservative principles has made her a rallying figure for the hawkish faction of the LDP 4. During her 2025 leadership campaign, she attempted to soften her image by describing herself as a "moderate conservative," a move seen as an effort to broaden her appeal within a divided party 2, 17.

Economic Policy: A Continuation of Abenomics

Sanae Takaichi's economic philosophy is heavily influenced by the policies of her mentor, Shinzo Abe, often referred to as 'Abenomics' 25. This approach is characterised by a combination of aggressive monetary easing, significant fiscal spending, and structural reforms to stimulate economic growth. Takaichi has been a vocal supporter of this expansionary framework and has criticised the Bank of Japan's more recent moves towards interest rate hikes 25, 34. Her premiership is expected to signal a departure from the more fiscally hawkish stance of her immediate predecessor, Shigeru Ishiba 34.

Equity markets may welcome her emphasis on fiscal expansion, though some economists and investors have expressed concern about Japan's already substantial national debt, which is one of the largest in the world 25, 34. During the leadership campaign, Takaichi moderated some of her more radical economic proposals, such as a cut to the consumption tax, in an effort to present a more pragmatic platform 34. However, her core belief in using government spending to boost the economy remains central to her agenda. One of the immediate challenges for her government will be to address public frustration over rising prices and stagnant wages, which were key factors in the LDP's recent unpopularity 5. Takaichi has also raised the possibility of renegotiating a trade and investment deal with the United States, particularly if the Trump administration is perceived as pursuing policies unfair to Japan 25, 35.

National Security and Foreign Policy

Sanae Takaichi is known for her hard-line, nationalist stance on national security and foreign policy, which is likely to define her premiership and could lead to significant shifts in Japan's international relations 4. A key element of her platform is a "Japan First" foreign policy, which, while affirming the importance of the security alliance with the United States, prioritises Japan's national interests 4. She has expressed confidence in her ability to build a strong personal relationship with U.S. President Donald Trump, seeing a parallel between her "Japan First" and his "America First" approaches 34. She is a strong advocate for increasing Japan's defence budget and strengthening its military capabilities to counter perceived threats in the region 2.

Her position on China is particularly hawkish 2, 35. While she has acknowledged China as an "important neighbor" and stressed the need for dialogue, her overall approach is one of deep skepticism and a desire for Japan to adopt a more robust posture 2, 4. This aligns with her repetition of Shinzo Abe's statement that a "Taiwan emergency is a Japan emergency," signaling a firm stance on regional security 3. This position is likely to create friction with Beijing.

Relations with South Korea are also expected to be complex under a Takaichi administration. Her revisionist views on Japan's wartime history are a significant point of contention. She has repeatedly visited the controversial Yasukuni Shrine, which honours Japan's war dead, including convicted Class A war criminals from the Second World War 4, 17, 35. These visits are seen by China and South Korea as a symbol of Japan's past militarism and a lack of remorse for its historical actions 4, 10. While she was circumspect about her intentions to visit the shrine as prime minister during the election campaign, her past actions and statements suggest a potential for renewed diplomatic tensions 10, 17. She takes a negative view of the Kono and Murayama Statements, which offered apologies for Japan's past aggressions, including the issue of "comfort women" 3. Such stances are likely to complicate recent efforts to improve ties between Tokyo and Seoul 4.

Immigration and Social Policy

In the realm of social policy, Sanae Takaichi's positions are consistently and staunchly conservative, reflecting the traditionalist wing of the Liberal Democratic Party 3, 15. A prominent feature of her 2025 leadership campaign was a strong focus on immigration 14, 35. In what was seen as an attempt to win back voters from newer, more radical right-wing parties like Sanseito, she has called for stricter controls on immigration and expressed concerns about allowing "people with completely different cultures and backgrounds" into Japan 14, 35. This rhetoric marks a significant politicisation of immigration, an issue that had not previously been a major feature of national election campaigns 12.

On family and gender issues, Takaichi's views are firmly rooted in the preservation of traditional social structures. She is a vocal opponent of legalising same-sex marriage and has consistently resisted efforts to amend the 19th-century civil code to allow married couples to retain separate surnames 3, 15, 32. She argues that such reforms would "destroy the social structure based on family units" 3. Her stance on gender equality is paradoxical; while poised to become the first female prime minister, she is not known as an advocate for gender equality and has supported the LDP's traditional view of women's roles as being primarily centered on being good mothers and wives 4, 15, 32. She also supports maintaining the male-only succession law for the imperial family, opposing any change that would allow a woman to ascend to the Chrysanthemum Throne 3, 15. These positions place her in direct opposition to many gender equality advocates in Japan.

The First Female Prime Minister: Symbolism and Substance

The impending inauguration of Sanae Takaichi as Japan's first female prime minister is an event of immense symbolic importance in a country that has consistently ranked low in global indices of political gender parity 6, 20. For decades, Japanese politics has been a male-dominated sphere, with women holding a small fraction of seats in the Diet and occupying few senior leadership positions 7, 21. In 2025, female lawmakers held only 15.7% of seats in the powerful House of Representatives 7. The ascension of a woman to the nation's highest political office inevitably shatters a powerful glass ceiling and provides a potent symbol of change 4, 13.

However, the substance of Takaichi's political platform raises significant questions among gender equality advocates about whether her premiership will translate into tangible progress for Japanese women 4, 14. Critics point to her long-standing opposition to policies aimed at advancing women's rights and promoting gender equality, such as allowing separate surnames for married couples and legalising same-sex marriage 15, 32. Her alignment with the traditional LDP view of women's roles within the family unit is seen by many as antithetical to the goals of modern feminism 32. Yuki Tsuji, a professor specialising in politics and gender, has stated that Takaichi "has no interest in women's rights or gender equality policies" 14.

During her 2025 campaign, Takaichi did make some overtures towards addressing gender disparity, promising to appoint a cabinet with female representation comparable to that of Nordic countries 13, 36. She has also recently acknowledged her own struggles with menopausal symptoms and highlighted the need for greater understanding of female health issues in the workplace 15, 32. Despite these pledges, there is concern that as leader, her loyalty to the influential male heavyweights within the LDP could constrain her ability to enact meaningful reforms 15. Therefore, while her rise is a historic milestone, its substantive impact on the broader landscape of gender politics in Japan remains a subject of considerable debate and scepticism.

Challenges Facing the Takaichi Administration

Sanae Takaichi will assume the premiership at a moment of profound political and economic challenge, facing a series of formidable hurdles both domestically and internationally 4, 12. Her ability to navigate these issues will determine the longevity and success of her administration.

Domestic Challenges

One of the most immediate tasks for Takaichi will be to unify a deeply fractured Liberal Democratic Party 2, 12. The leadership election exposed significant divisions within the party, and her victory, while decisive in the runoff, was not overwhelming. She must mend these internal rifts and rebuild public trust in the LDP, which has been severely damaged by scandals and electoral defeats 12, 14. Compounding this is the precarious parliamentary situation. The LDP-Komeito ruling coalition has lost its majority in both houses of the Diet, meaning Takaichi's government will need to seek cooperation from opposition parties to pass legislation 2, 14. This could lead to policy compromises and potential gridlock. She has indicated a willingness to expand the ruling coalition, but any new partner would need to align with the LDP on fundamental issues such as constitutional revision 2.

On the economic front, she inherits a nation grappling with the rising cost of living, an ageing population, and the long-term challenges of a stagnant economy 4, 14. The Japanese public is frustrated with the LDP's inability to deliver sustained wage growth, and Takaichi will be under immense pressure to demonstrate that her economic policies can make a tangible difference in the lives of ordinary citizens 5.

International Challenges

Externally, the geopolitical landscape is equally daunting. Takaichi's hard-line stance on China and her revisionist views on history are likely to create significant friction with Japan's closest neighbours, China and South Korea 4, 34. Managing these relationships while maintaining regional stability will be a critical and delicate balancing act. Her premiership could undermine recent progress made in repairing ties with Seoul 4.

The relationship with the United States, Japan's most important ally, will also require careful management, particularly under the presidency of Donald Trump 12. While Takaichi believes her "Japan First" policy aligns with Trump's "America First" agenda, his transactional and often unpredictable approach to foreign policy could present significant challenges, particularly on issues of trade and defence burden-sharing 4, 34. Navigating the complex dynamics of a multipolar world, with rising tensions in the Indo-Pacific, will demand astute and pragmatic diplomacy from a leader known for her uncompromising ideological convictions.

Conclusion

The election of Sanae Takaichi as the leader of the Liberal Democratic Party and her imminent appointment as Japan's first female prime minister undoubtedly constitute a landmark event in the nation's political history. It is a moment laden with symbolic power, breaking a long-standing barrier in one of the world's most advanced economies. However, the significance of this development is deeply complex and multifaceted. Takaichi's ascent is not the culmination of a progressive, gender-focused political movement, but rather the result of a conservative party in crisis turning to a leader who embodies a return to its traditionalist, nationalist roots.

Her premiership promises a distinct ideological shift, marked by a hawkish foreign and security policy, an adherence to the expansionary principles of 'Abenomics', and a socially conservative agenda that stands in stark contrast to the liberalising trends seen in many other developed nations. While her supporters see a strong, resolute leader capable of restoring Japan's standing and reviving the LDP's fortunes, her critics foresee a period of heightened regional tensions and a potential setback for social progress, particularly in the realm of gender equality.

The challenges confronting Takaichi are immense. She must lead a divided party, govern without a parliamentary majority, address deep-seated economic anxieties among the populace, and navigate a treacherous international environment. Her success will depend on her ability to balance her firm ideological convictions with the pragmatic necessity of consensus-building and compromise, both at home and abroad. The world will be watching closely to see whether the premiership of Japan's first woman leader will usher in a new era of dynamism and change, or if it will represent a consolidation of a more insular and conservative national identity. The answer will have profound implications not only for the future of Japan but for the stability and political alignment of the entire Indo-Pacific region.
