A New Political Epoch in Japan

The Ascendancy of Sanae Takaichi and the Dawn of the Nation's First Female Premiership

Article created and last updated on: Sunday 05 October 2025 01:39

Abstract

On the 4th of October 2025, the ruling Liberal Democratic Party of Japan elected Sanae Takaichi as its new president, a decision that has positioned her to become the nation's first female prime minister 14. This article provides a comprehensive analysis of this seminal event in modern Japanese political history. It examines the multifaceted career of Sanae Takaichi, from her early life and entry into politics to her key ministerial appointments and ideological evolution within the conservative echelons of the Liberal Democratic Party 2, 28. The article elucidates the intricate political dynamics that facilitated her rise, including the resignation of her predecessor, Shigeru Ishiba, the lingering effects of a major political fundraising scandal, and the shifting factional alignments within the party 14, 15. A significant portion of this discourse is dedicated to a meticulous examination of Takaichi's policy platform, encompassing her advocacy for a continuation of 'Abenomics', her staunchly conservative social viewpoints, and her hawkish foreign policy and national security agenda, particularly concerning constitutional revision 2, 27. The historical and societal context of female leadership in Japan is thoroughly explored to appreciate the profound significance of Takaichi's premiership in a traditionally patriarchal political landscape 20, 22. Furthermore, the article scrutinises the formidable domestic and international challenges that await the new administration, from economic revitalisation and demographic pressures to navigating complex geopolitical relationships with China, the United States, and the Korean Peninsula 9, 12, 18. Through an objective and scholarly lens, this article offers a deeply analytical perspective on the implications of Sanae Takaichi's leadership for the future of Japanese politics, society, and its role on the global stage.

Introduction

The political landscape of Japan, a nation long characterised by its deeply entrenched patriarchal norms and a political sphere dominated by male figures, experienced a momentous paradigm shift on the 4th of October 2025. On this day, the Liberal Democratic Party (LDP), which has governed Japan for the vast majority of the post-war era, elected Sanae Takaichi, a 64-year-old veteran politician, as its new president 14. This victory in the party's leadership election has, in effect, guaranteed her ascension to the office of Prime Minister, making her the first woman in Japanese history to hold this esteemed position 3, 5, 8. The election of Takaichi is not merely a symbolic breakthrough for gender representation in Japanese politics; it signals a potentially significant ideological shift to the right for the nation's governance. A self-proclaimed admirer of the former British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher and a devout protégée of the late Shinzo Abe, Japan's longest-serving prime minister, Takaichi is a figure renowned for her staunch conservative and nationalist convictions 3, 5, 6. Her policy platform, which includes the continuation of the economic stimulus programme known as 'Abenomics', a hardline stance on national security, and a socially conservative agenda, has been the subject of intense scrutiny both within Japan and internationally 2, 7. This article will provide a doctoral-level examination of the rise of Sanae Takaichi, delving into the intricate web of political, social, and historical factors that have culminated in this landmark event. It will meticulously analyse her political trajectory, her core ideologies, and the challenges and opportunities that await her as she prepares to lead the world's fourth-largest economy 4. The study will also situate her ascendancy within the broader context of Japan's political evolution, the internal dynamics of the LDP, and the ongoing struggle for gender equality in Japanese society. By offering a comprehensive and objective analysis, this article aims to furnish a profound understanding of the potential ramifications of Takaichi's premiership for Japan's domestic and foreign policy in the years to come.

The Political Climate Preceding the 2025 LDP Leadership Election

The path to Sanae Takaichi's historic victory was paved by a period of considerable political turbulence for the Liberal Democratic Party and the nation at large. Her predecessor, Shigeru Ishiba, who had assumed the premiership in September 2024 after winning a leadership contest against Takaichi herself, announced his resignation on the 7th of September 2025, having been in office for less than a year 4, 14, 15. Ishiba's brief tenure was marred by the LDP's poor performance in two major elections: the 2024 general election and the 2025 House of Councillors election. These electoral setbacks resulted in the LDP-led coalition losing its majority in both chambers of the Diet for the first time in the post-war era, creating a minority government and a state of political instability 4, 14. The party's declining public support was exacerbated by a deeply damaging political fundraising scandal that came to light in late 2023 and continued to plague the party throughout 2024. The scandal involved several major factions within the LDP, most notably the Seiwa Seisaku Kenkyūkai, or the Abe faction, which was found to have engaged in fraudulent accounting practices related to political funding 15, 32. The public outcry over this "slush fund" scandal led to a significant erosion of trust in the LDP and prompted then-Prime Minister Fumio Kishida to announce the dissolution of his own faction in early 2024, a move that was followed by the voluntary disbandment of most other factions within the party 15. This collapse of the traditional factional system, which had for decades dictated the internal power dynamics of the LDP, created a more fluid and unpredictable political environment within the party 18. It was within this context of a fractured party, diminished public trust, and a minority government that Shigeru Ishiba struggled to govern effectively, ultimately leading to his resignation and the triggering of the October 2025 LDP presidential election 7, 14.

The Procedural Pathway to the Premiership

To comprehend the significance of Sanae Takaichi's victory in the LDP leadership election, it is essential to understand the mechanics of how a prime minister is chosen in Japan. Unlike in presidential systems, the Japanese populace does not directly elect its head of government. Instead, the Prime Minister is designated by a resolution of the Diet, Japan's bicameral legislature, which consists of the House of Representatives (the lower house) and the House of Councillors (the upper house) 30. The candidate for prime minister must be a member of either house of the Diet and a civilian 30. In practice, the leader of the party that holds the majority of seats in the more powerful House of Representatives is almost invariably chosen as the prime minister. Given that the LDP has been the dominant political force in Japan for most of the post-war period, the party's internal leadership election is, for all intents and purposes, the decisive contest for the premiership 15. The LDP presidential election is a two-stage process. In the first round of voting, all LDP Diet members are entitled to a vote, and an equal number of votes are allocated to the party's rank-and-file members, distributed proportionally based on the results of votes within the LDP's 47 prefectural chapters 7, 26, 38. In the 2025 election, there were 295 votes from Diet members and 295 votes from the party's approximately 916,000 members 14, 26. If a candidate secures an outright majority in the first round, they are declared the winner. However, if no candidate achieves this, a runoff election is held between the top two candidates from the first round. In the runoff, the 295 Diet members vote again, but the rank-and-file members' votes are reduced to just one vote per prefectural chapter, for a total of 47, making the votes of the parliamentarians significantly more influential in the final outcome 7, 26. The winner of the LDP presidency is then put forward as the party's candidate for prime minister in the Diet. Even with the LDP holding a minority in both houses, as was the case in October 2025, the party's nominee is almost certain to be elected prime minister due to the LDP remaining the largest single party and the historical difficulty of opposition parties in uniting behind a single candidate 14, 37. An extraordinary session of the Diet was scheduled for mid-October 2025 to formally elect Takaichi as the new prime minister 14, 37.

A Historical Perspective on Female Leadership in Japan

The prospect of Japan's first female prime minister is a watershed moment in a nation that has consistently ranked low in international comparisons of gender equality, particularly in the political sphere 3, 26. The journey towards female political participation in Japan has been a long and arduous one. Japanese women were first granted the right to vote and stand for election in 1946, following the end of the Second World War and the enactment of a new constitution under the Allied occupation 20, 23, 30. In the first post-war general election held that year, 39 women were elected to the House of Representatives, accounting for 8.4 percent of the chamber's members 20. However, this initial surge was not sustained, and for decades, the presence of women in the Diet remained minimal. The patriarchal structures of Japanese society, which traditionally relegated women to domestic roles, coupled with the male-dominated culture of the political world, created significant barriers for aspiring female politicians 20, 29. The so-called "Madonna boom" in 1989, spurred by the Japan Socialist Party's leader Takako Doi, saw a notable increase in the number of women elected to the Diet, but this proved to be a temporary peak 20, 29. In the lead-up to the 2025 election, women constituted only about 15 percent of the members of the House of Representatives, and only two of Japan's 47 prefectural governors were women 3, 26, 36. The government's own goal, set under the "womenomics" initiative of the Abe administration, to have women occupy 30 percent of leadership positions by 2020 was quietly revised down to just 7 percent, a tacit acknowledgement of the deep-seated challenges to achieving gender parity 20. Against this backdrop, Sanae Takaichi's rise to the pinnacle of Japanese politics is all the more remarkable. However, as will be explored later in this article, her own conservative views on gender roles and social issues have led many to question whether her premiership will genuinely advance the cause of women's rights in Japan 26, 36, 37.

The Contenders and the Campaign of the 2025 LDP Leadership Election

The 2025 LDP leadership election was a keenly contested affair, with five candidates vying for the party's top post 7, 14. Besides Sanae Takaichi, the other contenders were Shinjiro Koizumi, the Minister of Agriculture and son of the popular former Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi; Yoshimasa Hayashi, the Chief Cabinet Secretary under Shigeru Ishiba; Takayuki Kobayashi, a former Minister for Economic Security; and Toshimitsu Motegi, a former Foreign Minister 14, 40. From the outset, the race was largely seen as a two-horse race between the nationalist conservative Takaichi and the more moderate reformist Koizumi 7, 39. The campaign was fought against the backdrop of the LDP's weakened position and the need for a leader who could unify the party and regain public trust 7. The candidates' policy platforms, however, were not drastically different on many key issues, with all broadly focusing on economic recovery and national security 26. Takaichi positioned herself as the heir to Shinzo Abe's legacy, advocating for expansionary fiscal and monetary policies and a robust defence posture 7, 12. Koizumi, on the other hand, presented himself as a proponent of generational change and fiscal discipline, in continuity with the Ishiba administration 7. Polling of the general public and LDP members in the run-up to the election showed Takaichi and Koizumi to be the clear frontrunners, often neck and neck 7, 39. In a Kyodo News survey in mid-September, Takaichi led Koizumi by 28 percent to 22.5 percent among those who considered themselves LDP supporters 7. As the election day approached, the contest tightened, with both candidates commanding significant but distinct bases of support within the party 7.

The Election Results: A Two-Round Contest

The leadership election on the 4th of October 2025 unfolded as predicted, proceeding to a runoff after no candidate secured a majority in the first round of voting 14, 26, 40. In the initial round, which saw a total of 590 votes cast (295 from Diet members and 295 from rank-and-file members), Sanae Takaichi emerged as the frontrunner with 183 votes (31.07 percent of the total) 14, 26. She secured 64 votes from her fellow lawmakers and an impressive 119 votes from the party's grassroots members, demonstrating her strong appeal to the party's base 26. Shinjiro Koizumi came in second with 164 votes (27.84 percent), having garnered more support from Diet members (80 votes) but less from the rank-and-file (84 votes) compared to Takaichi 26. The other candidates trailed significantly, with Yoshimasa Hayashi receiving 134 votes, Takayuki Kobayashi 59 votes, and Toshimitsu Motegi 49 votes 26. With Takaichi and Koizumi advancing to the runoff, the focus shifted to which candidate could attract the support of the lawmakers who had backed the defeated candidates. In the second round, a total of 342 votes were at stake (295 from Diet members and 47 from the prefectural chapters) 14, 26, 38. Sanae Takaichi ultimately prevailed, securing 185 votes to Shinjiro Koizumi's 156 14, 40. This narrow but decisive victory in the runoff made her the first female president of the Liberal Democratic Party, setting the stage for her to become Japan's first female prime minister 14.

Early Life, Education, and Entry into Politics

Sanae Takaichi was born on the 7th of March 1961, in the city of Nara, in western Japan 2, 5, 8. She hails from a family with no prior involvement in politics; her father was an employee of a Toyota-affiliated automobile company, and her mother served as a police officer in the Nara Prefectural Police 3, 5. After completing her secondary education at Unebi Senior High School, Takaichi went on to study business management at Kobe University 3, 8. During her youth, she exhibited an unconventional streak, playing the drums in a heavy metal band and developing a passion for riding motorcycles 8, 36. Following her graduation, she attended the prestigious Matsushita Institute of Government and Management, an institution known for grooming future political leaders 3, 10. A formative experience in her early career was her time in the United States from 1987 to 1989, where she worked as a congressional fellow in the office of Patricia Schroeder, a Democratic U.S. Representative 4, 10. This period provided her with firsthand experience of the American political system. Upon her return to Japan, she worked as a television broadcaster and author before making her foray into politics 5. Takaichi was first elected to the House of Representatives in the 1993 general election, running as an independent candidate from her home prefecture of Nara 2, 8. She later joined the New Frontier Party before finding her political home in the Liberal Democratic Party in 1996 2, 4.

Ministerial Career and Political Evolution

Sanae Takaichi's political career has been marked by a steady rise through the ranks of the LDP and a series of key ministerial appointments, particularly under the patronage of Shinzo Abe 2, 17, 35. During Abe's first term as prime minister (2006-2007), she served in several cabinet posts, including Minister of State for Okinawa and Northern Territories Affairs, Science and Technology Policy, and Gender Equality 2, 10, 28. Following the LDP's return to power in 2012 under Abe's second administration, Takaichi's influence grew significantly. She was appointed as the chairperson of the LDP's Policy Research Council, a powerful position within the party hierarchy 2, 17. From 2014 to 2017 and again from 2019 to 2020, she held the important portfolio of Minister for Internal Affairs and Communications 10, 11, 16, 28. More recently, during the premiership of Fumio Kishida, she served as the Minister of State for Economic Security from 2022 to 2024 2, 10. Throughout her career, Takaichi has consistently aligned herself with the most conservative and nationalist wing of the LDP. She is a long-standing member of Nippon Kaigi, a powerful ultranationalist lobby group that advocates for constitutional revision and a more assertive role for Japan on the world stage 5, 13. Her political ideology is deeply influenced by Shinzo Abe, whose legacy she has pledged to continue 6, 17, 31. This ideological alignment has been a cornerstone of her political identity and a key factor in her appeal to the right-wing base of the LDP. Her journey to the top of the party was not without its setbacks. She made two unsuccessful bids for the LDP leadership in 2021 and 2024, losing to Fumio Kishida and Shigeru Ishiba respectively, before her triumphant third attempt in 2025 2, 4, 17.

Economic Policy: The Continuation of Abenomics

At the heart of Sanae Takaichi's economic agenda is a commitment to the principles of "Abenomics," the three-pronged strategy of aggressive monetary easing, flexible fiscal policy, and structural reforms that was the hallmark of the Shinzo Abe administration 2, 37. Takaichi has consistently advocated for an expansionary fiscal stance, suggesting a willingness to increase government spending to stimulate economic growth, even if it means issuing more deficit-covering bonds 12. She is a proponent of maintaining an ultra-loose monetary policy, a stance that has put her at odds with those who favour a normalisation of interest rates. Her economic philosophy prioritises growth over fiscal consolidation, reflecting a belief that a robust economy is the prerequisite for addressing Japan's long-term fiscal challenges, including its substantial national debt 32. During her campaign, Takaichi also emphasised the importance of economic security, a concept that has gained prominence in Japan in recent years amid growing geopolitical tensions and supply chain vulnerabilities 12. As the former minister in charge of this portfolio, she has called for strengthening the screening of foreign investment and protecting Japan's critical technologies from foreign influence. Her approach to trade is encapsulated in a "Japan First" policy, which, while supportive of the U.S.-Japan alliance, also hints at a more assertive stance in trade negotiations to protect what she perceives as Japan's national interests. She has, for instance, raised concerns about the terms of a trade deal with the United States that was concluded under the Trump administration, suggesting a willingness to renegotiate aspects of it 8, 27.

Social Policy: A Staunchly Conservative Stance

Sanae Takaichi is one of the most socially conservative figures in the mainstream of Japanese politics. Her views on a range of social issues are deeply rooted in traditional values and have drawn considerable criticism from progressive and gender equality advocates. She is a vocal opponent of legalising same-sex marriage and has consistently resisted calls to allow married couples to retain separate surnames, arguing that such a change would undermine the traditional family unit 2, 4, 5, 13, 37. This latter issue is a significant litmus test in Japanese politics, with proponents seeing it as a crucial step towards gender equality and individual identity. Takaichi also opposes any revision to the Imperial Household Law that would allow a woman to ascend to the Chrysanthemum Throne, thereby maintaining the principle of male-only succession 2, 4, 13. Her stance on gender equality is complex and, for many, contradictory. While she is set to break the ultimate glass ceiling in Japanese politics, her policy positions do not align with the broader feminist movement in Japan. She has often espoused the LDP's traditional view of women's roles as being primarily centred on the family, as mothers and wives 8, 26, 36, 37. Despite this, during her 2025 leadership campaign, she did pledge to significantly increase the number of female ministers in her cabinet to "Nordic" levels, an apparent acknowledgement of the need for greater female representation in government 31, 38. She has also spoken publicly about her own experiences with menopausal symptoms, calling for better education for men on women's health issues, a rare public pronouncement on such a topic by a prominent politician 26. Nevertheless, her overall track record and stated positions have led many women in Japan to be sceptical about whether her premiership will herald a new era of progress for women's rights 26, 36.

Foreign Policy and National Security: A Hawkish and Revisionist Agenda

Sanae Takaichi's foreign policy and national security platform is arguably the most defining aspect of her political identity. She is a staunch advocate for revising Japan's post-war pacifist constitution, particularly Article 9, which renounces war as a sovereign right of the nation and prohibits the maintenance of land, sea, and air forces 2, 5, 6, 13. Takaichi, in line with the long-held ambition of Shinzo Abe and the conservative wing of the LDP, seeks to formally recognise the Japan Self-Defense Forces as a national military and to enable Japan to play a more proactive role in international security affairs 12, 27. She is a proponent of significantly increasing Japan's defence budget and has argued for the acquisition of counter-strike capabilities, which would allow Japan to target enemy bases in response to an attack. Her security posture is heavily influenced by what she perceives as a growing threat from China. A noted China hawk, Takaichi has been critical of Beijing's military build-up, its assertive actions in the East and South China Seas, and its economic practices 2, 36. She is a strong supporter of deepening the U.S.-Japan security alliance and has even suggested that the United States should deploy medium-range missiles in Japan to bolster deterrence 2. Her stance on Taiwan is particularly robust; she has repeatedly endorsed Abe's assertion that "a Taiwan emergency is a Japan emergency" and has called for a "quasi-security alliance" with the island 2, 12. This position is likely to be a source of significant friction with Beijing. Takaichi's views on Japan's wartime history are also a point of major controversy. She is a historical revisionist who believes that Japan's role in the Second World War has been unfairly portrayed and that the nation should not have to offer further apologies for its past actions 13. She is a regular visitor to the Yasukuni Shrine, a controversial site in Tokyo that honours Japan's war dead, including 14 convicted Class-A war criminals from the Second World War 5, 10, 13, 19, 21, 24, 33, 34. These visits are deeply offensive to China and South Korea, who view them as a symbol of Japan's past militarism and a lack of remorse for its wartime aggression 19, 21, 34. While she has been more circumspect about her intentions to visit the shrine as prime minister, her long-standing practice in this regard suggests that relations with Japan's closest neighbours could become strained under her leadership 2, 37. Her approach to immigration is also notably hardline, as she has called for stricter controls and expressed concerns about the impact of foreigners on Japanese society and culture 37, 38.

The Challenges Facing the Takaichi Administration

Sanae Takaichi will assume the premiership at a time of significant domestic and international challenges. Her ability to navigate these complex issues will be the ultimate measure of her leadership.

Domestic Challenges

On the domestic front, the most immediate task for Takaichi will be to stabilise the political situation and restore public trust in the LDP. The party is still reeling from the fundraising scandal and its recent electoral losses, and Takaichi will need to demonstrate that she can lead a clean and effective government 15, 32. Governing with a minority in both houses of the Diet will be a formidable challenge, requiring her to build consensus and cooperate with opposition parties to pass legislation 14, 32, 37. This may prove difficult given her strong ideological convictions, which could alienate more centrist political forces. The Japanese economy, despite the long-running efforts of Abenomics, continues to face significant headwinds, including sluggish growth, stagnant wages, and rising prices that are impacting households 9, 14. Takaichi's prescription of more fiscal stimulus and monetary easing may provide a short-term boost, but it also risks exacerbating Japan's already massive public debt 32. Perhaps the most intractable long-term challenge is Japan's demographic crisis. The country has one of the world's oldest populations and a rapidly declining birth rate, which has profound implications for the labour force, the social security system, and the overall vitality of the nation 9, 37. Addressing this issue will require comprehensive and politically difficult reforms. Takaichi will also have to manage the internal divisions within her own party. Her victory was a narrow one, and she will need to heal the rifts that were exposed during the leadership contest and unify the LDP behind her agenda 15, 18, 32. The absence of the traditional factional structure may make party management more complex and unpredictable 18.

International Challenges

In the international arena, Takaichi's hawkish foreign policy will be put to the test. Her tough stance on China is likely to lead to a more confrontational relationship with Beijing, potentially increasing tensions in the East China Sea and over the issue of Taiwan 2, 36. While she is a firm advocate of the U.S.-Japan alliance, her "Japan First" rhetoric and willingness to question the terms of existing agreements could introduce new dynamics into the relationship with Washington, particularly under a potentially transactional Trump administration 8, 9, 12, 27. The success of her diplomacy will depend on her ability to build a strong personal rapport with the U.S. president while also asserting Japan's own strategic interests 27. Relations with South Korea, which have often been strained by historical issues, are also likely to be challenging under Takaichi's leadership, given her revisionist views and her association with the Yasukuni Shrine 4. The ongoing security threat posed by North Korea's nuclear and missile programmes will also demand constant vigilance and close coordination with regional partners. As prime minister, Takaichi will be tasked with navigating a complex and volatile geopolitical landscape, balancing the need for a strong defence posture with the imperative of maintaining regional stability and economic prosperity.

Conclusion

The election of Sanae Takaichi as the leader of the Liberal Democratic Party and her imminent appointment as Japan's first female prime minister represents a truly historic moment. It shatters a long-standing barrier in one of the world's most enduring democracies and places a woman at the helm of a major global economic power. However, the significance of this event extends far beyond the realm of gender politics. Takaichi's ascendancy marks a decisive victory for the conservative and nationalist wing of the LDP and signals a potential doubling-down on the policies and ideologies championed by her mentor, Shinzo Abe. Her premiership promises a Japan that is more assertive on the world stage, more robust in its military posture, and more conservative in its social values. The challenges that await her are immense. She inherits a divided party, a minority government, a fragile economy, and a host of complex demographic and geopolitical issues. Her success will depend on her ability to unite her party, build coalitions with the opposition, restore public trust, and navigate the treacherous waters of international diplomacy. The world will be watching closely to see whether Japan's "Iron Lady," as some have dubbed her, can deliver on her promise of a "strong and prosperous" nation, and what the implications of her leadership will be for the future of Japan and the wider Indo-Pacific region. The dawn of the Takaichi era is not just a new chapter in Japanese politics; it is a moment of profound transformation, the consequences of which will unfold for years to come.
