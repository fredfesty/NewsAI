The Final Stand at Universal Ostrich Farms

Saturday, 08 November 2025 02:06

Summary

In the small community of Edgewood, British Columbia, a protracted and emotionally charged conflict reached its conclusion. Universal Ostrich Farms, home to a flock of several hundred birds, became the epicentre of a national debate after an outbreak of highly pathogenic avian influenza was detected in December 2024. The Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA), mandated to protect national biosecurity, issued a depopulation order. This directive initiated a nearly year-long battle that pitted the farm's owners against the federal government. The struggle unfolded in courtrooms, culminating in a final appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada. It also drew international attention, with prominent American figures like Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and Dr. Mehmet Oz intervening in an attempt to save the flock. The farm's supporters, including activists and members of the 'Freedom Convoy' movement, rallied behind the owners, framing the issue as one of government overreach. For the CFIA, however, the matter was a critical question of public and animal health, guided by established international protocols for containing a virus with pandemic potential. The final court decision sealed the fate of the ostriches, bringing a sombre end to a saga that highlighted the complex intersection of animal welfare, scientific protocol, and individual rights in the face of a significant biosecurity threat.

An Unseen Threat in Edgewood

In early December 2024, a malady swept through the flock at Universal Ostrich Farms in the rural community of Edgewood, British Columbia.11,25 Over a three-week period, what the owners described as "flu-like" symptoms resulted in the deaths of 25 to 30 of the large, flightless birds.11,25,36 The Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA) was alerted to the situation not by the farm, but through an anonymous report on 28 December.11,25 Agency officials promptly intervened, imposing a verbal quarantine order on the premises.11,25

By New Year's Eve, the nature of the threat became clear.11 Samples taken from two deceased ostriches tested positive for H5 avian influenza.11,23 Within minutes of this confirmation, the CFIA issued a formal cull order, mandating the disposal of all birds on the farm by 1 February 2025.11,33 Subsequent genome sequencing by the National Centre for Foreign Animal Disease in Winnipeg confirmed the pathogen was a highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) subtype, H5N1.36 By the time the outbreak subsided in mid-January, the death toll on the farm had reached 69 birds.11,22,25

The H5N1 virus is a significant concern for global health authorities.17 It primarily infects birds, among whom it is highly contagious, and can be transmitted through saliva, nasal secretions, and faeces.5,9 The virus can devastate domestic poultry populations, posing a severe economic threat to the agricultural sector.9 While transmission to humans is rare, it can occur through close contact with infected birds or heavily contaminated environments.6,26 Such zoonotic transmission is a serious concern due to the virus's potential to mutate and adapt, possibly leading to more efficient human-to-human spread and a pandemic.5,17 Since 1997, over 900 human cases have been reported globally, with a high fatality rate.26,22

A Battle in the Courts

Faced with the destruction of their flock, the owners of Universal Ostrich Farms mounted a vigorous legal challenge.1,22 In early January 2025, they applied for an exemption to the cull order, arguing that the ostriches possessed "rare and valuable genetics" that warranted preservation.11,14 The CFIA denied this request on 10 January.11,25 The farm then sought a judicial review of the agency's decision, securing a temporary stay of the cull order from the Federal Court on 31 January, just before the initial deadline.11,33

The farm's central argument was that the surviving birds were healthy, showed no signs of illness, and may have developed immunity, making them scientifically valuable.2,13 They contended that the CFIA's "stamping-out" policy—the complete depopulation of an infected flock—was an unreasonable and disproportionate measure in their specific case.37,42 This policy, however, is a standard international practice recommended by the World Organisation for Animal Health to contain HPAI outbreaks swiftly and effectively.3,12,42

The CFIA maintained that its actions were guided by the Health of Animals Act and were necessary to prevent the spread of the disease.1,6 The agency argued that even ostriches that appear healthy can remain carriers of the virus, posing an ongoing risk to other animals and potentially humans.8,13 Allowing the flock to live, particularly with potential exposure to wild birds, increased the risk of the virus mutating into a more dangerous form.13

The legal battle progressed through multiple levels of the Canadian judicial system.36 On 13 May, the Federal Court dismissed the farm's application for judicial review, ruling that the CFIA's decisions were both "reasonable" and "procedurally fair."11,25 The farm's owners appealed this decision, but the Federal Court of Appeal unanimously upheld the lower court's ruling on 21 August.37,39 The appeal court acknowledged the difficult financial and emotional outcome for the farm's owners but concluded that the law supported the CFIA's position.37 The final recourse was the Supreme Court of Canada, which issued an interim stay on the cull in September while it considered whether to hear the case.8,36 On 6 November 2025, the nation's highest court announced it would not hear the appeal, removing the last legal obstacle to the depopulation of the flock.8,25

An Unlikely Alliance

The plight of the Edgewood ostriches attracted a diverse and vocal coalition of supporters, transforming a local biosecurity issue into an international controversy.36 Protesters, including some associated with the "Freedom Convoy" movement that had previously demonstrated against pandemic-related government mandates, gathered at the farm.2,37 They organised events like "Ostrichfest," a music concert and protest held in May, to rally support and oppose what they viewed as government overreach.11,25,37 The situation at the farm grew tense, leading to the arrests of farm spokeswoman Katie Pasitney and her mother, co-owner Karen Espersen, in September for allegedly obstructing CFIA officials.11,25,39

The cause also garnered attention from prominent figures in the United States.18 Robert F. Kennedy Jr., in his capacity as U.S. Health Secretary, wrote to the head of the CFIA in May and again in July, urging the agency to reconsider the cull.1,21 He argued that the flock, having survived an H5N1 outbreak, represented a significant opportunity for scientific study.1,21 Kennedy proposed a collaboration between Canadian and U.S. researchers to study the birds' antibodies in a controlled environment.1,21

Dr. Mehmet Oz, the administrator for the U.S. Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services, also intervened, offering to relocate the entire flock to his ranch in Florida.3,16,20 The farm's owners, while grateful for the offer, declined, stating their desire to keep the birds in Canada.16,20 The involvement of these American officials was reportedly facilitated by New York billionaire John Catsimatidis, who also provided financial support for the farm's legal fees and publicly called for U.S. health officials to be allowed to evaluate the birds.10,16,20,22 This international pressure added a political dimension to the conflict, framing the CFIA's regulatory decision as a matter of diplomatic interest.16

The Final Act

With the Supreme Court's refusal to hear the case, the CFIA immediately prepared to enforce the depopulation order that had been contested for over ten months.12,13 On the afternoon of 6 November 2025, CFIA vehicles arrived at Universal Ostrich Farms.12 Officials, some in biohazard suits, began herding the remaining 300 to 330 ostriches into a large enclosure constructed from hay bales.4,12,13,24 As night fell, the area was illuminated by floodlights, and Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) officers blocked access to the property, turning away supporters.13

Later that evening, gunshots were heard coming from within the enclosure.1,13,24 The CFIA later confirmed that it had completed the cull.4,7 In a statement, the agency explained that after consulting with experts, it had determined that the use of professional marksmen was the most appropriate and humane method for the on-farm depopulation.4,7,18 The entire operation was conducted under the supervision of CFIA veterinarians and was designed to comply with all relevant animal welfare standards.4,7 Following the cull, the agency began the disposal phase of its disease response, with the farm remaining under strict quarantine.7

The farm's supporters who remained at the scene reacted with anguish and anger, shouting at the officials carrying out the operation.24 Farm spokeswoman Katie Pasitney had earlier made a final, emotional plea on social media, insisting the birds were healthy.1 Co-owner Dave Bilinski expressed his dismay, stating he feared "there's no justice left."3 The CFIA stated it would provide compensation to the farm owners for the value of the animals, offering up to $2,200 per bird upon completion of the necessary documentation.1

Conclusion

The culling of the ostriches at Universal Ostrich Farms marks the definitive end of a complex and divisive chapter in Canadian agricultural history. The episode brought into sharp relief the inherent tensions between the responsibilities of the state to manage biosecurity threats and the rights of individual producers. The CFIA's actions were consistently upheld by the judiciary as a reasonable application of its mandate under the Health of Animals Act, reflecting established global protocols for managing highly pathogenic avian influenza.6,14,37 The agency's primary objective was the swift eradication of a disease with the potential to devastate Canada's multi-billion dollar poultry industry and pose a risk to human health.1,40 For the farm's owners and their supporters, the outcome was a profound loss, viewed as the unnecessary destruction of healthy and scientifically valuable animals by an uncompromising bureaucracy.3,13 The intervention of high-profile American figures underscored a growing scepticism of government health policies in some quarters, a sentiment that resonated with activists in Canada.2,10 Ultimately, the arguments for the flock's unique scientific value did not outweigh the legal and scientific imperatives driving the national disease control strategy.21,37 The quieted farm in Edgewood now stands as a stark illustration of the difficult, and often painful, decisions required to safeguard public and animal health against the persistent threat of zoonotic diseases.
